# Zakat on stocks

This is theological as much as financial — be careful with the framing. The mechanics below are mainstream; the specific PKR nisab figure is recalculated annually and must be a TODO.

## What to explain

- Zakat is a **religious obligation**, not a tax. Different scholars and madhabs treat trading shares vs. long-term holdings differently.
- The mainstream Hanafi position commonly applied in Pakistan: zakat is owed on the **market value** of shares held above nisab for one **lunar year**.
- The standard rate is **2.5%** — this is a religious constant, safe to state directly.
- The **nisab** threshold is recalculated yearly based on the gold/silver standard. Leave the PKR figure as a TODO; many Pakistani Shariah boards publish the nisab annually.
- **Lunar-year holding requirement.** Zakat is owed once one full lunar year (hawl) has passed on assets above nisab.

## Two common scholarly opinions to present

Always present the conservative default first, then the alternative — never assert one as "the correct" treatment.

1. **Trading shares (bought to sell):** zakat is owed on the **full market value** at 2.5%. This is the conservative default and applies to active traders and short-term holders.
2. **Long-term investment shares:** some scholars hold that zakat is owed only on the **company's zakatable assets** per share — typically cash, receivables, and inventory — which is usually a smaller base than the full share price. This requires the company's annual report or, for mutual funds, the fund's published zakat-per-unit value.

The reader should ultimately consult a qualified scholar; emphasize this in the disclaimer.

## Worked-example skeleton

```mdx
Suppose on 1 Ramadan you hold 1,000 shares of XYZ Ltd. at a market
price of PKR 250 per share. Total market value: PKR 250,000.

Under the trading-shares treatment (the conservative default), zakat
is owed at 2.5% on the full PKR 250,000:

250,000 × 0.025 = PKR 6,250

If you instead follow a long-term-investment opinion, you would owe
zakat only on the per-share zakatable assets — that calculation
requires the company's annual report and is best done with a scholar
or a Shariah-screened mutual fund's published zakat-per-unit value.
```

## Purification (related but distinct)

If the user asks about **purification** of haram income (a topic adjacent to zakat but doctrinally separate — relevant to the Shariah-investing category):

- Purification refers to donating away the portion of returns that come from non-Shariah-compliant sources (e.g., a small interest-bearing portion in a mostly-compliant company's income).
- The amount is calculated as the **non-compliant income ratio** × **the dividend received**, then donated to charity (without expecting reward, since it's not the donor's money to keep).
- The non-compliant ratio for individual stocks is rarely published; for Shariah-compliant mutual funds the fund publishes a per-unit purification amount.
- Cross-link to the Shariah-investing category for the screening side: `/docs/10-shariah-investing/purification`.

## Tone guardrails specific to this topic

- Never state a religious ruling as if you (the agent) issued it. Phrasing like "you must" is wrong; "the mainstream Hanafi position commonly applied in Pakistan" is right.
- Never imply that one madhab or scholar is correct over another. Present positions, attribute them, and defer to the reader's own scholar.
- The disclaimer for this topic explicitly mentions consulting a scholar, not just a tax advisor — this distinction matters.
