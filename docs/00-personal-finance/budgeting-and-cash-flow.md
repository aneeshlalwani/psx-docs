# Budgeting and Cash Flow

If you have ever been surprised at how little is left in your account three days before your next salary, you do not have a salary problem, you have a budgeting problem. Budgeting and cash flow analysis are the simplest possible answer: a clear picture of what comes in and what goes out, kept up to date.

## What you'll learn

- The difference between cash flow analysis and budgeting
- The income and expense categories every Pakistani household should track
- The three rules of honest cash flow analysis
- How to actually maintain a budget month after month

## Cash flow analysis

**Cash flow analysis** is the act of tracking where your money comes from and where it goes. It is usually done by looking back at the last six months or forward at the next three. The goal is a detailed, honest record of money in versus money out.

Three rules make cash flow analysis useful:

1. **Categorize everything.** Group expenses into housing, food, transport, utilities, and so on. A pile of disconnected line items tells you nothing.
2. **Be complete.** Do not skip the small purchases. Daily chai, ride-share trips, online subscriptions, and snacks add up to a meaningful portion of monthly spend.
3. **Be realistic.** Do not understate your expenses to make the picture look better. The numbers exist whether you write them down or not.

## Budgeting

A **budget** is a plan for your money that balances income and expenses. It starts with two questions:

1. What is your income?
2. What are your expenses?

A budget should be **personalised**. There is no single correct template. The only requirement is that it be a tool you actually use, not a spreadsheet you build once and forget.

### Income categories

| Category | What it includes |
|---|---|
| **Salary** | Your regular paycheck after taxes. |
| **Bonus and commissions** | Estimated after-tax earnings. |
| **Dividends and interest** | Earnings from your investments and bank accounts. |
| **Gifts** | Money received for birthdays, Eid, weddings. |
| **Other** | Side-business income, rental income, freelance work. |

### Expense categories

| Category | What it includes |
|---|---|
| **Housing** | Rent or mortgage payment, property tax, home insurance. |
| **Utilities** | Electricity, gas, water, internet, mobile phone. |
| **Automobile** | Loan payment, fuel, insurance, maintenance. |
| **Food** | Groceries and dining out. |
| **Savings** | Money set aside for future goals. |
| **Investments** | Contributions to a pension plan, mutual fund, or PSX brokerage account. |
| **Emergency fund** | Money set aside for unexpected events. |

Treat **savings, investments, and emergency fund** as required expenses, not leftovers. If they only happen when something is left over at month end, they will rarely happen.

## Tracking your budget

**Tracking** is just the act of monitoring income and expenses regularly to make sure you are sticking to the plan. Salary is easy to track because it shows up on a payslip. Variable expenses like food, fuel, and entertainment need a system, whether that is a notes app, a spreadsheet, or a budgeting app. Pick one tool and stay with it for at least three months before you decide if it works.

## Key takeaways

- Cash flow analysis tells you what is happening with your money. Budgeting tells you what should happen with it.
- Categorize everything, be complete, be realistic.
- Treat savings, investments, and the emergency fund as required expenses.
- Pick one tracking tool and stick with it long enough to see patterns.
