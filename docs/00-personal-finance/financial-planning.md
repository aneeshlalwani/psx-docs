# Financial Planning

If "make a financial plan" sounds like something only people with a CA in the family do, this page should change that. A financial plan is a small set of decisions about how you will use the money you have, written down somewhere you can look at it later. Nothing more complicated than that.

## What you'll learn

- The six steps of taking charge of your finances
- The three principles every financial plan should respect
- The difference between a plan and good intentions

## Step one: take stock

Before you can plan, you have to know where you currently stand. Run through this short list once:

- **Determine your starting point.** Add up what you own (cash, savings, investments, property, vehicle) and what you owe (loans, credit card balances, money you owe family). Write the totals down.
- **Set goals.** Be specific. "Save Rs. 200,000 for a wedding by December next year" is a goal. "Save more" is not.
- **Develop a plan.** Decide month by month how you will get from where you are to the goal.
- **Keep simple records.** A notes file or a basic spreadsheet is enough. The point is consistency, not sophistication.
- **Make an informal budget.** Even a rough income-versus-expense list is better than guessing.
- **Deal with debt.** Address shortfalls and high-interest debt before anything else. They quietly destroy returns elsewhere.

## The three principles every plan should respect

When you write your plan, three things matter more than the specific numbers.

### 1. Flexibility

Your plan should adapt to life. A new job, marriage, the birth of a child, an unexpected illness. Events like these will arrive without warning, and a plan that breaks the moment one of them happens is not really a plan. Build it loose enough to flex.

### 2. Liquidity

**Liquidity** is how quickly an asset can be converted to cash without losing value. You need enough liquid money on hand to handle emergencies. The standard rule of thumb is **three to six months of living expenses** kept in an easily accessible savings account. The page on [saving and the emergency fund](/docs/personal-finance/saving-and-emergency-fund) covers this in detail.

### 3. Minimisation of taxes

There are legal ways to reduce both income tax and tax on inheritance. The point is not to obsess over tax savings. It is to use them as a tool that helps you reach your goals faster. The [Taxes and Zakat](/docs/category/taxes--zakat) section covers Pakistan-specific tax considerations.

## A plan only matters if you act

The cleanest plan in the world is useless if it stays on paper. Implementation takes time, and most plans need a year or two to feel routine. You can build the plan alone or with help from a qualified advisor. The important thing is to start, and to revisit the plan once a year.

## Key takeaways

- A plan is just a written record of decisions about how you will use your money.
- Take stock, set specific goals, write the plan, track it, and deal with debt.
- Build for flexibility, liquidity, and tax efficiency.
- A plan you do not act on is just a wish.
