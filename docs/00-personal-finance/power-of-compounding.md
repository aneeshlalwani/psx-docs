# The Power of Compounding

If there is one idea in personal finance that does most of the heavy lifting, it is compounding. The basic claim sounds boring: money earns money, and that money earns more money. The implication is dramatic. Small consistent savings, given enough time, can become very large sums. Albert Einstein is often quoted calling compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. Whether or not he actually said it, the math is on his side.

## What you'll learn

- The difference between simple interest and compound interest
- A worked example showing what compounding does over three years
- The formulas you need (and how to read them)
- The difference between compound interest and compound return
- How absolute return and CAGR are calculated, with the standard formulas

## Simple interest

**Simple interest** is interest paid only on the original amount you invested or borrowed. It does not include any interest earned in earlier periods.

**Formula:**

```text
I = P × R × T
```

Where _I_ is total interest, _P_ is the principal amount, _R_ is the annual rate as a decimal, and _T_ is time in years.

**Worked example.** Suppose you deposit PKR 100,000 at 10 percent simple interest for five years.

- Annual interest: 100,000 × 10% = PKR 10,000
- Total interest over five years: 10,000 × 5 = PKR 50,000
- Total at the end: 100,000 + 50,000 = PKR 150,000

The interest is paid only on the PKR 100,000 every year. Years two through five do not earn interest on the previous years' interest.

## The compound effect

The **compound effect** is what happens when each year's interest gets reinvested and starts earning its own interest. Same starting amount, different result.

Take PKR 100 at a 20 percent annual return:

| Year | Starting balance | Earnings | Ending balance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 100 | 20 (20% of 100) | 120 |
| 2 | 120 | 24 (20% of 120) | 144 |
| 3 | 144 | 28.80 (20% of 144) | 172.80 |

If you had withdrawn the profit each year, you would have earned PKR 20 a year, totalling PKR 60 over three years. By letting the profit compound, the total return is **PKR 72.80**: PKR 12.80 more, just from leaving it alone. The longer the money stays, the more dramatic the gap. Over decades, the difference is enormous.

## Compound interest

**Compound interest** is interest earned on the original principal *plus* on the accumulated interest from earlier periods. It is most often used in the context of guaranteed savings products and bonds, where the rate is fixed and predictable.

**Formula:**

```text
A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n × t)
```

Where _A_ is the final amount, _P_ is the principal, _r_ is the annual rate as a decimal, _n_ is the number of times interest compounds per year, and _t_ is the number of years.

To find just the interest earned, subtract the principal:

```text
I = A − P
```

## Compound interest vs. compound return

Same mechanism, different reliability.

- **Compound interest:** the rate is **guaranteed**. A fixed deposit at a Pakistani bank quoted at 11 percent will pay 11 percent regardless of what happens that year.
- **Compound return:** the rate is **not guaranteed**. A return on stocks, mutual funds, or other market-based investments depends on how the underlying asset performs. It can be higher than a fixed deposit, lower, or even negative in a given year.

Both rely on compounding. The question is whether you want a known small return (compound interest) or a variable larger one (compound return).

## Measuring compound return

Two formulas, depending on how long you have held the investment.

### Absolute return (under one year)

For an investment held less than a year, the simplest calculation is the gain or loss as a **percentage** of what you started with:

```text
Absolute Return = (Ending Value − Beginning Value) / Beginning Value × 100
```

**Example.** You invest PKR 100,000 and after eight months it is worth PKR 110,000.

Absolute return = (110,000 − 100,000) / 100,000 × 100 = **10 percent**.

### CAGR (over one year)

**CAGR** stands for **Compounded Annual Growth Rate**. It expresses a multi-year return as a single, smoothed annual rate, ignoring the year-to-year ups and downs.

```text
CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n) − 1
```

Where _n_ is the number of years.

**Example.** You invest PKR 100,000 and three years later it is worth PKR 133,100.

CAGR = (133,100 / 100,000)^(1/3) − 1 = 1.331^(1/3) − 1 = 1.10 − 1 = **10 percent per year**.

The investment grew from 100,000 to 133,100 in three years. CAGR tells you the equivalent steady annual rate that would produce the same end result.

<Callout type="tip" title="Time is the active ingredient">
  Compounding does almost nothing in the first year or two. Then it
  accelerates. Most of the magic happens in the last decade of any
  long term investment, which is why "start now" is the most
  important rule in personal finance, even if the amount you can
  save today feels small.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Simple interest pays only on the original principal. Compound interest pays on principal plus accumulated interest.
- Compounding accelerates over time. The longer the horizon, the larger the gap between simple and compound returns.
- Compound interest is guaranteed. Compound return depends on how the underlying asset performs.
- Absolute return is used for periods under a year. CAGR is used for periods longer than a year.
