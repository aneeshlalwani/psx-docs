# Time Value of Money

If someone offers you PKR 10,000 today or PKR 10,000 two years from now, take the money today. The instinct to wait is almost always wrong. The reason is the **time value of money** (TVM): a rupee today can be put to work, while a rupee in the future cannot work at all until it arrives. Even a modest deposit rate is enough to make today's rupee worth more than tomorrow's by an amount you should not ignore.

## What you'll learn

- The core idea behind the time value of money
- A concrete worked example using PKR
- Present Value (PV) and Future Value (FV)
- Why TVM is the foundation of nearly every financial decision

## The core idea

Money can earn a return over time. A rupee in your hand today can be deposited, invested, or used to pay off a debt that is charging interest. None of those options are available to a rupee that arrives in the future.

The implication is straightforward. When you compare two amounts of money in different time periods, you cannot just compare the numbers. You have to adjust for what the earlier money could have grown to in the meantime.

## A worked example

Suppose someone offers you a choice:

- **Option A:** PKR 10,000 today.
- **Option B:** PKR 10,000 in two years.

Most people instinctively pick Option B if they do not need the money now ("I will have it when I need it"). Wrong choice.

If you take Option A and put the PKR 10,000 in a basic Pakistani savings account paying around 7 percent per year, after two years it is worth roughly:

10,000 × (1 + 0.07)² = 10,000 × 1.1449 = **PKR 11,449**.

That is PKR 1,449 more than Option B, simply because you took the money earlier and let it work. If you put the same PKR 10,000 into a fixed deposit or a diversified investment paying a higher rate, the gap is even larger.

The same logic runs in reverse. PKR 10,000 promised in two years is worth less than PKR 10,000 today, because today's PKR 10,000 could have already started earning a return.

## Present Value and Future Value

Two terms that show up everywhere in finance.

- **Present Value (PV):** what a future amount of money is worth in today's terms, after accounting for the rate it could have earned in between.
- **Future Value (FV):** what a present amount will grow to at a given rate over a given period.

If you understand compound interest from [the previous page](/docs/personal-finance/power-of-compounding), you already understand FV. PV is just the same formula run backwards. Instead of asking "how much will this grow to?", you ask "how much would I need today, at this rate, to end up with this future amount?".

## Why TVM matters for almost every decision

The time value of money quietly underlies most major financial choices.

- **Loans and EMIs.** A bank charges interest because the money it lends you today is worth more than the same rupees returned later.
- **Insurance and pensions.** The premium you pay today is invested by the company so that future payouts can be funded.
- **Salary versus equity offers.** Cash now is more valuable than the same amount of cash promised later, unless the future amount is significantly larger.
- **Investing.** Every minute earlier you start investing is one more compounding cycle the money gets to enjoy. This is why "start now, even if the amount is small" works.

<Callout type="tip" title="When in doubt, take it earlier">
  When the choice is between the same amount of money now or later,
  the answer is almost always "now". The exception is when the
  later amount is large enough to clearly beat the return you could
  have earned on today's amount, after taxes.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Money has time value because today's money can earn a return that future money cannot.
- The same numerical amount in two different time periods is not the same value.
- Present Value pulls a future amount back to today. Future Value pushes a present amount forward.
- TVM underlies loans, insurance, pensions, salary offers, and almost every investment decision.
