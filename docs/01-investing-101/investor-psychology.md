# Investor Psychology

The market is rational over the long term. Investors are not. Most retail investors lose to the market not because of bad math but because of bad emotional control. Fear sells positions at the bottom. Greed buys them at the top. The same person who carefully calculates a six percent inflation rate will throw lacs of rupees at a stock because their cousin said so. Recognising the patterns is the first step to not falling into them.

## What you'll learn

- The two biggest psychological traps every investor falls into
- The fear and greed cycle in plain language
- Loss aversion and why losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good
- A simple counter-strategy that works for most people

## Overconfidence

**Overconfidence** is the belief that you can consistently beat the market. Research across decades and across countries has shown that the vast majority of retail investors who try to pick winning stocks underperform the broader index over time. They trade too often, pay too much in commissions and taxes, and concentrate too heavily in a few "high-conviction" picks.

The cure is humility about what you actually know. If you cannot explain why a stock is mispriced today (and persuade an honest sceptic), you probably cannot pick winners. Most professional investors who beat the index have full-time research teams behind them. A retail investor with a few hours a week is not playing the same game.

## Herd behaviour

**Herd behaviour** is the tendency to follow the crowd. When everyone you know is buying a particular stock, the urge to join in is enormous. When everyone is panic-selling during a crash, the urge to sell with them is just as strong.

Both urges are wrong on average. Bubbles inflate when the herd buys, and they pop when prices have detached from fundamentals. Crashes overshoot when the herd sells, and they recover when prices have fallen below fundamentals. The investor who buys when others are panicking and sells when others are euphoric tends to do better than the one who runs with the crowd.

Warren Buffett's version: **be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful.**

## Fear and greed cycles

Markets cycle through emotional states. The pattern repeats:

1. **Optimism.** Prices are rising, news is positive, more buyers enter.
2. **Excitement.** Returns get above average. Stories of overnight wealth circulate.
3. **Euphoria.** Prices detach from fundamentals. Everyone "knows" prices will keep rising. The phrase "this time is different" appears.
4. **Anxiety.** Prices stop rising. Reality starts to bite.
5. **Denial.** "It is just a temporary correction."
6. **Fear.** Prices fall sharply. Sellers exit at any price.
7. **Capitulation.** The last buyers panic-sell at the bottom.
8. **Despondency.** "The market is broken. It will never recover."
9. **Hope, then optimism again.** And the cycle repeats.

Most investor money enters around stages 2 and 3 (when optimism is high) and exits at stages 6 and 7 (when fear peaks). That is the worst possible timing. The investor who recognises the cycle has a chance to act differently.

## Loss aversion

**Loss aversion** is the well-documented finding that the pain of losing PKR 10,000 is psychologically larger than the pleasure of gaining PKR 10,000. People will work harder to avoid losing money than to make an equivalent gain, and they make worse decisions when sitting on a paper loss.

The most common mistake driven by loss aversion: **holding losing positions for too long** in the hope they will "come back" rather than accepting the loss and redeploying the capital elsewhere. Sometimes the position does come back. Often it does not, and the opportunity cost is enormous.

## Counter-strategies

Three simple discipline rules that work for most investors.

1. **Set written goals before you invest.** Specific, time-bound, in PKR. When emotions run high, the written goals are an anchor.
2. **Maintain a long-term horizon.** The longer you plan to hold, the less today's volatility matters. Day-to-day price moves should not change a ten-year plan.
3. **Stick to a disciplined strategy.** Decide your asset allocation, your monthly contributions, and your rebalancing schedule in advance. Then follow the plan instead of reacting to news.

<Callout type="warning" title="The 24-hour rule">
  When you feel a strong urge to make a major change to your
  portfolio (sell everything, double down on a hot stock, exit the
  market entirely), wait 24 hours before acting. Most strong urges
  feel weaker by the next morning. The trades that survive that
  pause are usually the ones worth making.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Investors lose to themselves more often than to the market.
- Overconfidence and herd behaviour are the two most common traps.
- Markets cycle through optimism, euphoria, fear, and despondency. Recognising where you are in the cycle helps.
- Loss aversion makes you hold losers too long.
- The fix is written goals, a long horizon, and a disciplined strategy you stick to even when it feels wrong.
