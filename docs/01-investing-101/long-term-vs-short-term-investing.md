# Long-Term vs. Short-Term Investing

In any country with an active stock market, the same pattern shows up: the people who got rich from the market are almost always long-term investors. Day traders, swing traders, and short-term speculators tend to make headlines, but they rarely make sustainable wealth. This page explains why, and what "long-term" actually means in practice.

## What you'll learn

- The definitions of long-term, medium-term, and short-term investing
- Why long-term investing has historically outperformed
- The hidden costs of frequent trading
- Why most short-term traders lose money
- The Pakistani tax angle that tilts the field further toward long term

## Definitions

There is no universal cutoff, but standard rough categories are:

- **Short-term:** holding for less than one year. Day trading (same-day buy and sell) and swing trading (a few days to a few weeks) are types of short-term trading.
- **Medium-term:** holding from one to about five years.
- **Long-term:** holding for more than five years, often a decade or longer.

Most successful retail investors operate in the medium-to-long range. The investors with the best historical track records (Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, and the great fund managers of every market) tend to think in decades, not days.

## Why long-term wins

Three structural reasons.

### 1. Compounding has time to work

Compounding does almost nothing in the first year. It transforms wealth in the third decade. The longer you hold an investment that is growing on average, the more dramatic the gap between your starting capital and your ending balance. The [power of compounding page](/docs/personal-finance/power-of-compounding) covers the math.

### 2. Trading costs eat short-term returns

Every trade costs money: brokerage commission, withholding tax, and capital gains tax. A short-term trader making fifty trades a year pays these costs fifty times. A long-term investor making one or two adjustments a year pays them once or twice. Over decades, the difference is enormous.

### 3. Market volatility shrinks over time

PSX can move sharply in any given month. Over a five-year period, the average return is much closer to the long-term trend. Over twenty years, year-to-year volatility almost disappears in the trend line. Long-term investors get the trend; short-term traders ride the volatility, which is harder than it looks.

## Why most short-term traders lose

Short-term trading sounds attractive: quick profits, beat the market, no need to wait. The reality is harder. To make money consistently from short-term trading, you have to:

- Be right on direction more than half the time, after fees and taxes.
- Process new information faster than the institutional traders with full-time desks and dedicated software.
- Resist the emotional pull to over-trade, which most retail traders cannot.

Studies in multiple markets have consistently found that more than half of retail day traders lose money in their first year, and the share of profitable day traders shrinks further over longer periods. The market is a competitive game, and short-term trading puts you against opponents who are better resourced than you.

## A Pakistani long-term holding bonus

In Pakistan, the tax code historically rewards long-term holding. Capital gains realised on shares held beyond a certain holding period have been treated more favourably than short-term gains, and shares held for very long periods can be entirely exempt from capital gains tax. The exact thresholds change with each Federal Budget, so check current rules in the [Taxes and Zakat](/docs/category/taxes--zakat) section before making any tax-driven decision.

The principle, however, is durable: the Pakistani tax system has consistently been kinder to patient holders than to frequent traders.

## What "long-term" looks like in practice

A long-term Pakistani investor typically:

- Picks a diversified portfolio (or a low-cost index fund) and contributes monthly.
- Reads quarterly reports of the companies held but does not check the share price daily.
- Rebalances once a year to maintain the target asset mix.
- Increases contributions when income increases.
- Does not change strategy based on short-term market moves.

It is unglamorous and slow. It also works.

## Key takeaways

- Long-term investing has historically beaten short-term trading on almost every measure.
- Compounding rewards time, fees punish frequent trading, and volatility evens out over years.
- Most retail short-term traders lose money in their first year.
- Pakistan's tax code historically rewards long holders. Verify current rules before deciding.
- Successful long-term investing is unglamorous, slow, and consistent.
