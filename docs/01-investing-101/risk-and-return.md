# Risk and Return

Every investment promise that sounds too good to be true is too good to be true. The reason is a single principle that runs through all of finance: **higher returns require higher risk**, and there is no investment that delivers high returns with no risk. Once you understand this, half the dubious investment schemes you encounter become easy to recognise.

## What you'll learn

- What "risk" actually means in an investment context
- The risk-return tradeoff and why it always holds
- Why "risk-free" is a relative term, not an absolute one
- How your investment horizon changes the calculation
- Setting your own risk tolerance

## What "risk" means

In everyday language, "risky" means "likely to go wrong". In investing, **risk** has a more specific meaning: it is the **probability and magnitude that your actual return will differ from your expected return**, in either direction.

A government bond paying a fixed interest rate has low risk because the actual return is very close to the expected return. A small-cap stock can either double or fall to nothing within a year, so the actual return varies widely. Both are valid investments. They are just different points on the risk spectrum.

## The risk-return tradeoff

The fundamental rule of finance: **investments compete on the basis of risk-adjusted returns**.

If a bank fixed deposit pays a known rate with very low risk, no rational investor would buy a stock that also pays the same expected return but with much higher risk. To compete, the riskier asset has to offer a **higher expected return**. That extra return is called the **risk premium**, and it exists in every market in the world.

The implication: any product offering "high returns with no risk" is either lying about the returns, lying about the risk, or both. There are no exceptions.

## "Risk-free" is relative

Pakistani fixed deposits and government securities are often described as "risk-free" because the principal is highly protected. But they still carry **inflation risk**: if your deposit pays 8 percent and inflation runs at 11 percent, your money is losing real value while sitting safely in the bank.

So the choice is not between risk and no risk. It is between **which risks you accept**. Cash savings carry inflation risk. Stocks carry market risk. Real estate carries liquidity risk. Every option has a tradeoff.

## Your time horizon changes the math

Risk shrinks with time. The Pakistani stock market can fall 20 percent in a single year. Over a thirty-year window, that one bad year is dwarfed by the long-term trend. The longer you can leave your money invested, the more risk you can rationally take.

This is why the standard guidance is:

- **Short-term goals (under three years):** keep money in low-risk options like savings accounts or short-term fixed deposits. You cannot afford a market drawdown right before you need the money.
- **Medium-term goals (three to ten years):** balance between fixed income and equity-like investments.
- **Long-term goals (ten or more years):** lean into equities, where the risk premium has time to play out.

## Setting your risk tolerance

Risk tolerance is a personal answer, not a calculation. Two questions help.

1. **Time:** when will you need this money?
2. **Sleep:** if your portfolio dropped 30 percent tomorrow, would you panic-sell or hold?

If your honest answer to question two is "panic-sell", your risk tolerance is lower than your time horizon might suggest, and that is fine. The investor who actually holds through a drawdown beats the investor who plans to but does not. Choose a risk level you can live with.

## Key takeaways

- Risk is the probability and magnitude that your actual return will differ from your expected return.
- Higher expected returns require higher risk. There are no exceptions.
- Every investment carries some risk; "risk-free" really means "low principal risk, ignore inflation risk".
- A long time horizon lets you accept more risk because volatility evens out.
- Your risk tolerance depends on time horizon and on what you can actually live with emotionally.
