# Bonds

If a stock makes you a part-owner of a company, a bond makes you its lender. Buying a bond means handing over money today in exchange for a written promise that the borrower will pay you a fixed amount of interest at fixed intervals and return your principal on a fixed date. The Pakistani government, large Pakistani corporations, and global institutions all issue bonds to raise money, and Pakistani investors can buy them as a steady, predictable counterweight to the volatility of the stock market.

## The four key terms

Every bond is defined by four numbers.

- **Face value (par value).** The amount the bond will repay at maturity. Usually a round number like PKR 100,000 or PKR 1,000,000.
- **Coupon rate.** The annual interest rate the bond pays, expressed as a percentage of face value. A bond with PKR 100,000 face value and an 11 percent coupon pays PKR 11,000 in interest each year.
- **Maturity.** The date on which the principal is repaid. A 3-year bond issued in 2026 will repay its face value in 2029.
- **Issuer.** The party borrowing the money. Different issuers carry different credit risk.

## Pakistani bond types

The most common bond instruments available to Pakistani investors are issued by the government.

- **Treasury bills (T-bills).** Short-term government securities with maturities of 3, 6, or 12 months. Issued by the **State Bank of Pakistan** through regular auctions. They do not pay coupons; instead, they are sold at a discount and repaid at face value (the difference is the return).
- **Pakistan Investment Bonds (PIBs).** Longer-term government securities, typically 3, 5, 10, or 20 years. Pay a fixed semi-annual coupon and return face value at maturity.
- **Sukuk (Islamic government bonds).** Shariah-compliant equivalents of T-bills and PIBs. Instead of interest, they pay periodic returns derived from underlying assets. The Pakistani government regularly issues Ijarah Sukuk for Islamic-finance investors.
- **Corporate bonds and TFCs.** Term Finance Certificates issued by Pakistani companies. Carry higher yields than government bonds but also higher credit risk, since the issuer is a company rather than the sovereign.

For most retail investors, the simplest entry point is through a fixed-income mutual fund or money-market fund (covered in [Mutual Funds](/docs/investment-basics/mutual-funds)). The fund holds a diversified mix of T-bills, PIBs, and other instruments, and you buy units of the fund instead of bonds directly.

## Yield, price, and the inverse relationship

A bond's **yield** is the return you actually earn given the price you paid and the cash flows you will receive. When market interest rates rise, the price of existing bonds (with their now-below-market coupons) falls so that the implied yield matches the new market rate. When market rates fall, existing bond prices rise. **Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions.**

This matters in Pakistan because the **State Bank of Pakistan policy rate** moves periodically, and PIB and T-bill yields move with it. A bond bought today at one yield can be worth more or less tomorrow depending on rate movements.

## The risks specific to bonds

Bonds are not risk-free. Three risks matter most.

1. **Credit risk.** The issuer might fail to pay. This is low for SBP-issued government bonds, higher for corporate TFCs, and varies by issuer.
2. **Interest-rate risk.** As above, rising rates reduce the market price of existing bonds. If you have to sell before maturity, you may sell at a loss.
3. **Inflation risk.** A 9 percent coupon when inflation runs at 11 percent is still a real loss in purchasing power, even if the principal is fully repaid.

The page on [Types of Risk](/docs/investing-101/types-of-risk) covers each of these in more depth.

## Why most balanced portfolios hold some bonds

Bonds are not glamorous. They will not make you rich. Their job in a portfolio is different.

- **Predictable income.** Coupon payments arrive on schedule, useful for funding regular expenses or for retirees living off their portfolio.
- **Stability.** When stock markets crash, government bonds often hold their value or even rise. Holding both reduces the volatility of the overall portfolio.
- **Capital preservation.** Money you cannot afford to risk for a short-term goal (a house deposit in two years, school fees next year) belongs in instruments closer to bonds than to equities.

The right mix depends on your time horizon and risk tolerance. A young investor with a thirty-year horizon might hold mostly equities and only a small bond allocation. A retiree drawing income might hold a much larger share in bonds.

## Key takeaways

- A bond is a loan to a government or corporation, with fixed interest and a fixed maturity date.
- Pakistani investors most commonly access bonds through T-bills, PIBs, sukuk, corporate TFCs, or fixed-income mutual funds.
- Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions.
- Bonds carry credit, interest-rate, and inflation risks; they are not risk-free.
- Bonds provide income, stability, and capital preservation in a balanced portfolio.
