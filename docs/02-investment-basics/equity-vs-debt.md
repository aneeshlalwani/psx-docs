# Equity vs. Debt

Every business that needs money to grow has only two real options. It can **borrow** the money from someone, with a promise to pay it back. Or it can **sell a piece of itself** to someone in exchange for the cash. Those two options, debt and equity, are the foundation of every investment instrument you will encounter. Once you understand the difference, the rest of the investment world starts to make sense.

## What you'll learn

- The difference between debt and equity at the level of a balance sheet
- What each side gets and gives up
- Why investors buy bonds, and why they buy shares
- The "residual claim" that defines what it means to own equity

## Debt: a loan with rules

When a company raises money through **debt**, it is essentially taking a loan. The lender (a bank, a bondholder, or a financier) hands over cash now in exchange for a contract that says:

- The company will pay a fixed amount of interest at fixed intervals.
- The company will repay the principal in full on a specified date.
- These obligations exist regardless of how the business performs.

The lender does not get any ownership of the company. The lender only gets the right to receive the agreed payments and to be repaid the principal. If the business does badly, the lender still gets paid (or sues to be paid). If the business does spectacularly well, the lender still only gets the agreed interest, not a share of the upside.

In Pakistan, debt instruments include bank loans (the most common), corporate bonds, government bonds (Pakistan Investment Bonds, T-bills), and Shariah-compliant equivalents (sukuk). For an investor, buying any of these is the act of becoming the lender.

## Equity: ownership with strings attached

When a company raises money through **equity**, it sells **shares** of itself. The shareholder hands over cash and receives, in exchange, a small slice of ownership in the company.

Unlike a lender, an equity holder is not promised anything specific. The company does not have to pay the shareholder a fixed amount, on a fixed date, or ever. What the shareholder gets is something different: a **residual claim**.

## The "residual claim"

The residual claim is the most important idea on this page.

It means that shareholders only get paid after every other obligation has been paid. After employees, after suppliers, after tax authorities, after debt holders, after preferred shareholders. Whatever cash and assets are left over belong, in proportion, to the ordinary shareholders.

In a good year, the residual is large, and shareholders share in that prosperity through dividends and rising share prices. In a bad year, the residual can be zero, and shareholders get nothing. If the company goes bankrupt, the residual can be negative, and shareholders walk away with nothing while the lenders still recover at least some of their loan.

That is the deal: ordinary shareholders accept the most risk in exchange for the largest potential reward. This is why equities have historically returned more than bonds over long periods. The extra return is the **risk premium** for accepting last-in-line status.

## A worked example

Suppose a company has total assets worth PKR 50 crore at the end of the year. Its obligations are:

- PKR 10 crore of bank loan (debt)
- PKR 5 crore of supplier payments still owed
- PKR 2 crore of taxes payable
- PKR 3 crore of preferred-share dividends

That is PKR 20 crore in obligations that must be paid before any ordinary shareholder sees a rupee. The remaining PKR 30 crore belongs to ordinary shareholders, divided in proportion to how many shares each one owns. That PKR 30 crore is the residual.

If the company had only PKR 18 crore in assets, the obligations could not all be paid, and ordinary shareholders would receive nothing. The lenders, suppliers, tax authorities, and preferred shareholders would split what remains. **Last in line.**

## Why investors choose one over the other

The two sides are not better or worse, just different.

| | Debt (bonds, sukuk) | Equity (shares) |
|---|---|---|
| **Income** | Fixed and predictable | Variable, may be zero |
| **Upside** | Capped at agreed interest | Unlimited, in theory |
| **Downside** | Limited if borrower stays solvent | Total loss possible |
| **Claim order on bankruptcy** | Senior | Last (residual) |
| **Long-term return (historical)** | Lower | Higher |

A balanced portfolio usually includes both. Debt provides predictable income and lower volatility; equity provides growth and inflation-beating returns over the long run. The right mix depends on your time horizon and risk tolerance, covered in [Risk and Return](/docs/investing-101/risk-and-return).

## Key takeaways

- Companies raise money in two fundamentally different ways: debt (a loan) and equity (selling ownership).
- A debt holder is a lender with fixed claims; an equity holder is an owner with a residual claim.
- The residual claim means equity holders get paid last, but they share in unlimited upside if the business does well.
- Equity has historically returned more than debt because of the risk premium for accepting last-in-line status.
- Most balanced portfolios hold both.
