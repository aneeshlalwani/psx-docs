# ETFs

If a mutual fund is professional management with a daily NAV, an **Exchange-Traded Fund** (ETF) is a basket you can buy and sell on the stock exchange itself, just like a single share. ETFs combine the diversification of a fund with the convenience of a stock. For a Pakistani investor who already has a PSX trading account, buying an ETF is the easiest way to own a diversified slice of the entire market in a single transaction.

## What you'll learn

- What an ETF is and how it differs from a mutual fund
- How ETFs trade on PSX
- The two main flavours of ETF you will encounter

- When an ETF is the right vehicle and when a mutual fund is

## What an ETF is

An **ETF** is an investment fund whose units are listed and traded on a stock exchange. The fund holds a portfolio of securities, typically designed to track an **index**: a fund tracking the KSE-100 owns shares of the same hundred companies that make up the KSE-100, in the same proportions. When you buy a unit of the ETF, you indirectly own a sliver of all hundred companies at once.

The price of an ETF unit moves throughout the trading day, reflecting changes in the underlying portfolio. This is the main mechanical difference from a mutual fund, where the unit price (NAV) is calculated only once at the end of each day.

## How ETFs trade on PSX

The Pakistan Stock Exchange has been operating ETFs as a product category alongside equities, corporate debt, and government securities. To buy a Pakistani ETF, you do not need to open a separate AMC account; your existing PSX brokerage account works. Place a buy order at the current market price, settle on the standard PSX cycle (now T+1 since 9 February 2026), and the units appear in your CDC account. Selling works the same way: place a sell order, units leave your account, cash arrives at settlement.

For the latest list of ETFs currently trading on PSX, the most reliable source is the [PSX Data Portal](https://dps.psx.com.pk/). The available products and their providers change over time, so always check current information rather than relying on a list that may be out of date.

## The two main flavours of ETF

Most ETFs available to retail investors fall into one of two categories.

### Index ETFs

The classic and most common type. The ETF holds the same securities as a benchmark index, in the same proportions. A KSE-100 ETF holds the KSE-100 constituents. A KMI-30 ETF holds the thirty Shariah-compliant companies in that index. The fund's job is to mirror the index as closely as possible.

This is **passive management**: there is no fund manager picking stocks, just a rule-based portfolio that adjusts when the index itself adjusts. Passive management means lower fees.

### Sector or thematic ETFs

Less common in Pakistan, but available in larger markets. These hold a basket of stocks within a specific theme: energy, banks, technology, dividend payers, and so on. They give you concentrated exposure to a sector without picking individual stocks within it.

## The cost difference

ETFs are usually cheaper than mutual funds. Two reasons.

1. **No fund manager picking stocks.** A passive index ETF needs minimal staff and minimal trading, so the annual management fee is much lower than for an actively managed equity mutual fund.
2. **Trading on an exchange instead of through the AMC.** No front-end load. You pay the standard PSX brokerage commission and any applicable taxes when you buy or sell, the same as for any other share.

The lower fee compounds. Over twenty or thirty years, an ETF charging 0.5 percent per year versus a mutual fund charging 2 percent per year produces a substantial difference in ending wealth on the same underlying portfolio.

## ETF vs. mutual fund: which to choose

| | ETF | Mutual fund |
|---|---|---|
| **How you buy** | Through PSX brokerage | Direct from AMC |
| **Pricing** | Live throughout the day | NAV at end of day |
| **Annual fees** | Typically lower | Typically higher |
| **Front-end load** | None (just brokerage commission) | Often 1 to 3 percent |
| **Active management** | Usually no (passive index tracking) | Often yes |
| **Best when you want** | Low-cost, broad-market exposure | Active management, specific themes, or simplicity |

For most retail investors building a long-term portfolio on PSX, a low-cost index ETF is one of the most efficient single instruments available.

## A worked example

Suppose you have PKR 100,000 to deploy and want diversified exposure to the Pakistani equity market. Three approaches:

1. **Buy 10 individual stocks.** You spend hours researching, you pay brokerage on ten transactions, and your portfolio is concentrated in your ten picks (good and bad).
2. **Buy units of an actively managed equity mutual fund.** One transaction, immediate diversification across 30 to 70 stocks, but you pay a 1 to 3 percent front-end load and roughly 2 percent annually in management fees.
3. **Buy units of a KSE-100 ETF on PSX.** One transaction, immediate diversification across 100 stocks, no front-end load, and a much lower annual fee.

The three approaches have different tradeoffs. ETFs win on cost and simplicity for broad-market exposure.

## Key takeaways

- An ETF is an exchange-listed investment fund, usually designed to track an index.
- ETF units are bought and sold on PSX through a regular brokerage account, with prices updating live.
- Pakistani ETFs typically track the KSE-100, KMI-30, or other PSX indices.
- ETFs are usually cheaper than mutual funds because they are passively managed and trade on the exchange.
- For broad-market exposure with minimal cost, ETFs are one of the most efficient instruments available.
