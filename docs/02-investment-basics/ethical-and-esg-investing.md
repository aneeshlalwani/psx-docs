# Ethical and ESG Investing

For some investors, the question is not just "will this make money?" but also "is this the kind of business I want to put my money into?". A growing number of Pakistani investors are choosing to align their portfolios with their values, through either Shariah-compliant investing or international **ESG** frameworks. Both approaches start from the same place: that capital allocation is itself a moral choice.

## What you'll learn

- What ethical investing actually means in practice
- The three pillars of ESG investing
- How Shariah-compliant investing differs from ESG

## What ethical investing means

**Ethical investing** is the practice of choosing investments based on values, not only on financial return. Different investors have different values, but the underlying idea is consistent: avoid investing in companies whose business activities you find objectionable, and prefer companies whose activities you support.

This can take many forms. Some investors simply avoid specific industries (alcohol, tobacco, gambling, weapons). Others actively prefer companies with strong environmental records or fair labour practices. The most rigorous frameworks (ESG and Shariah-compliant) define screening criteria explicitly and apply them consistently.

## The three pillars of ESG

**ESG** stands for **Environmental, Social, and Governance**. It evaluates a company across three dimensions.

### Environmental

How does the company affect the natural world? Carbon footprint, energy efficiency, waste management, water usage, pollution, and impact on biodiversity. A cement company with high emissions and a renewable-energy company with very low emissions sit at opposite ends of this dimension.

### Social

How does the company treat people inside and outside the business? Employee welfare, workplace safety, fair wages, diversity, customer protection, supply-chain ethics, and community impact. A factory with regular safety incidents and a company with a strong safety record again sit at opposite ends.

### Governance

How is the company actually run? Board independence, executive compensation, transparency, shareholder rights, and freedom from corruption or fraud. A company with a strong independent board and clean accounting differs sharply from one with insider self-dealing and opaque reporting.

An ESG investor uses these three pillars to either **screen out** weak performers or **tilt toward** strong ones, often using a third-party ESG rating to do the comparison.

## Shariah-compliant investing

In Pakistan, **Shariah-compliant investing** is a much larger movement than ESG, with [Islamic mutual funds crossing PKR 2 trillion in assets under management in early 2026](https://propakistani.pk/2026/03/25/pakistans-islamic-mutual-funds-cross-rs-2-trillion-mark-for-the-first-time/).

Shariah-compliant investing follows Islamic principles, which prohibit interest (riba), gambling (maysir), and excessive uncertainty (gharar), and which exclude businesses whose primary activity is haram (alcohol, tobacco, conventional banking, conventional insurance, pork, adult entertainment, and so on).

Shariah-compliant funds and indices use a Shariah board to apply explicit screening criteria. The PSX **KMI-30 index** tracks 30 Shariah-compliant companies and is the most prominent Pakistani Shariah equity benchmark. The full screening rules and the concept of **purification** of non-compliant income are covered in the [Shariah Investing](/docs/category/shariah-investing) section.

## Where ESG and Shariah overlap

The two frameworks come from different traditions but share considerable ground.

| | Both exclude | Distinct emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| ESG | Tobacco, weapons, environmentally destructive activities | Climate, social impact, governance metrics |
| Shariah | Tobacco, alcohol, gambling, conventional banking, adult entertainment | Riba, gharar, debt-to-asset ratios, business-activity rules |

A Shariah-compliant investor in Pakistan often inadvertently scores well on ESG dimensions too, because the underlying values overlap on multiple practical questions. The reverse is less consistent: a global ESG fund might still hold conventional banks, alcohol companies, or businesses incompatible with Islamic finance.

## Pakistani options

Three practical paths for a Pakistani investor who wants to align investments with values.

1. **Buy individual Shariah-compliant stocks.** Many KMI-30 constituents trade on PSX and you can build a portfolio of them directly.
2. **Use a Shariah-compliant mutual fund or ETF.** Al Meezan Investment Management is Pakistan's largest Shariah-focused AMC, and most major AMCs (HBL, NBP, UBL, ABL) offer Islamic fund variants. A KMI-30 tracking fund gives you instant Shariah-compliant diversification.
3. **Apply ESG-style screens in addition to financial analysis.** While Pakistan's domestic ESG fund market is smaller than its Islamic finance market, you can apply your own ESG criteria when picking individual stocks (avoid companies with serious environmental violations, weak governance, or labour-rights issues).

## Key takeaways

- Ethical investing means aligning your portfolio with your values, not only chasing returns.
- ESG evaluates companies on Environmental, Social, and Governance dimensions.
- Shariah-compliant investing follows Islamic principles and is the dominant ethical-investing framework in Pakistan.
- ESG and Shariah overlap significantly but are not identical; Shariah has stricter rules around interest and specific industries.
- Pakistani investors can build values-aligned portfolios through KMI-30 stocks, Islamic mutual funds, ETFs, or applied ESG screens.
