# Mutual Funds

If picking individual stocks sounds like a full-time job (it is), and if you would rather have someone else do the research while you focus on your actual career, mutual funds are the answer. A mutual fund pools money from thousands of small investors and hires professional managers to invest it. You buy units of the fund instead of picking individual stocks. The fund handles the diversification, the research, the monitoring, and the rebalancing. You get exposure to dozens of companies through a single transaction.

## What a mutual fund is

A **mutual fund** is a professionally managed investment vehicle that pools money from many investors and invests it in a portfolio of securities (stocks, bonds, money-market instruments, or a mix) according to a stated strategy.

When you invest PKR 50,000 in an equity mutual fund, your money joins the money of thousands of other unitholders, and the **Asset Management Company** (AMC) running the fund deploys the combined pool across (typically) twenty to a hundred different stocks. Your PKR 50,000 buys a number of fund **units**, each representing a tiny slice of the entire portfolio.

The unit price is calculated daily. Each day's **Net Asset Value (NAV)** per unit reflects the current market value of everything the fund holds, divided by the number of units outstanding. You can buy or redeem units at the NAV (subject to small front-end or back-end fees).

## The Pakistani AMC landscape

Most major Pakistani banks operate Asset Management Companies. According to the [Mutual Funds Association of Pakistan (MUFAP)](https://www.mufap.com.pk/), the regulator-recognised industry body, the largest AMCs include:

- **Al Meezan Investment Management** (Pakistan's largest AMC, focused on Shariah-compliant funds)
- **NBP Fund Management** (subsidiary of National Bank of Pakistan)
- **HBL Asset Management** (subsidiary of Habib Bank)
- **UBL Fund Managers** (subsidiary of United Bank Limited; also offers Islamic funds under "UBL Ameen")
- **MCB Investment Management**
- **ABL Asset Management**
- **Atlas Asset Management, Faysal Asset Management, Lakson Investments, Alfalah Asset Management**, and others

Opening an investment account at any of these AMCs is similar to opening a bank account. The AMC provides KYC forms, you submit the usual documents (CNIC, salary slip or source of income, bank account details), and you can begin investing in any of their funds.

## The four main fund types

Pakistani mutual funds typically fall into four categories.

1. **Money-market funds.** Invest in short-term, high-quality instruments (T-bills, term deposits, commercial paper). Aim to provide higher returns than a savings account with broadly comparable safety. Best for short-term parking of cash.
2. **Income funds (fixed-income / sovereign).** Invest in PIBs, T-bills, sukuk, and corporate bonds. Provide steady income. Lower volatility than equity funds.
3. **Equity funds.** Invest in PSX-listed shares. Highest long-term return potential but also the highest volatility. Best for long-term wealth building.
4. **Balanced or asset-allocation funds.** Hold a mix of stocks and bonds, often rebalanced by the manager. Provide a middle ground for investors who want simplicity.

Each of these categories also has a **Shariah-compliant** version (in Pakistan, Islamic mutual funds have crossed PKR 2 trillion in assets under management). [Shariah Investing](/docs/category/shariah-investing) covers the screening rules that make a fund Shariah-compliant.

## The fees you actually pay

Mutual funds are not free. Three fees show up most often.

- **Front-end load** (paid when you buy). Typically 1 to 3 percent of your investment.
- **Annual management fee** (deducted from the fund daily, embedded in the NAV). Typically 1 to 2 percent per year for equity funds, lower for money-market and income funds.
- **Back-end load or redemption fee** (paid when you sell, sometimes only if you redeem within a short period). Often zero after a certain holding period.

Across a long holding period the management fee compounds against you, so paying close attention to the **expense ratio** matters. A fund charging 2.5 percent per year will eat a meaningful share of long-term returns versus a fund charging 1 percent. Verify the current fees on the AMC's website or fund manager's report before investing.

## When mutual funds make sense

Mutual funds are usually the better choice when:

- You do not have the time or inclination to research individual companies.
- Your investable amount is small enough that buying a diversified portfolio of individual stocks would be impractical.
- You want professional management of fixed-income instruments you cannot easily buy directly.
- You want Shariah-compliant exposure without doing the screening yourself.

Direct stock investing is usually the better choice when:

- You enjoy the research and want full control over what you own.
- Your portfolio is large enough that the management-fee drag becomes meaningful in absolute terms.
- You want to actively pursue specific stocks that no fund holds.

Many Pakistani investors do both: a core position in mutual funds for breadth, plus a smaller direct portfolio for high-conviction picks.

## Key takeaways

- A mutual fund pools money from many investors and is managed by a professional AMC.
- Pakistan has dozens of AMCs (Al Meezan, NBP Funds, HBL Asset Management, UBL Fund Managers, MCB, ABL, etc.) regulated by SECP and represented by MUFAP.
- The four main fund types are money market, income, equity, and balanced. Each has a Shariah-compliant variant.
- Front-end loads, annual management fees, and back-end loads all eat into returns. Check expense ratios before investing.
- Mutual funds are better when you want diversification, professional management, and simplicity; direct investing is better for control and conviction.
