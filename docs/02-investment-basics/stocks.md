# Stocks

Imagine your friend Aseem runs a successful lemonade stall. He wants to expand into a chain of stalls but does not have the capital. He has two options: take a bank loan, or list the stall publicly on the Pakistan Stock Exchange and sell small slices of ownership to ordinary people. He picks the second option. He files for an **Initial Public Offering** (IPO), and you and a thousand other Pakistanis pay PKR 10 each for a small piece of his business. Just like that, you are now a part owner of his lemonade stall. Welcome to the world of stocks.

## What you'll learn

- What a stock actually is and what owning one entitles you to
- How a stock is created (through an IPO)
- The two ways stocks pay you back: capital gain and dividends
- A worked example showing both kinds of return in PKR
- What can go wrong

## What a stock is

A **stock** (or **share**) is a small unit of ownership in a company. If a company has issued one million shares and you own one of them, you own one millionth of the company. That sounds tiny, but the implication is significant: you are entitled to one millionth of the company's profits, one millionth of any dividends it declares, and one millionth of whatever is left if the company is ever sold or wound up.

Stocks are how ordinary people, on ordinary salaries, end up owning slivers of large successful businesses. Without the stock market, only the wealthy could become part-owners of growing companies.

## How a stock is created

Companies that need to raise capital can sell shares to the public through an **IPO**. The company sets an initial price, books arrange for institutional and retail investors to buy at that price, and on listing day the shares start trading on the Pakistan Stock Exchange. From that day onward, the share price rises and falls based on **supply and demand** in the secondary market.

The company itself receives the capital only at the IPO. After listing, when investors buy and sell shares among themselves, the company is not part of those transactions. It still benefits indirectly: a higher share price makes it cheaper to raise more money in the future, and shareholders who feel wealthy spend on the company's products. But the day-to-day trading on PSX moves money between investors, not to the company.

## The two ways stocks pay you back

Owning a stock can return money to you in two ways, sometimes both at once.

### 1. Capital gain

If you buy a share for PKR 100 and later sell it for PKR 130, the PKR 30 difference is your **capital gain**. The share price rises when more people want to buy than sell, which happens when the company's prospects look better, when the broader market is rising, or when news shifts sentiment. Capital gain is the headline source of long-term wealth creation in stocks.

### 2. Dividends

If the company is profitable, it can pay a portion of those profits back to shareholders as a **dividend**. A company might announce a PKR 4 per share dividend twice a year. If you own 500 shares, you receive PKR 2,000 in cash, twice a year, just for holding the shares.

Not every company pays a dividend. Some, especially fast-growing companies, prefer to reinvest all profits back into the business in the hope that this will produce a much larger capital gain later. Established, mature companies in stable industries (large banks, fertiliser companies, fuel utilities) tend to pay regular dividends.

## A worked example

You buy 500 shares of a Pakistani company at PKR 200 each. Total cost: PKR 100,000.

Over the next three years:

- The company pays a PKR 6 per share dividend each year. You receive 500 × 6 = **PKR 3,000 per year**, or PKR 9,000 over three years.
- The share price rises to PKR 280. You sell. Capital gain: 500 × (280 − 200) = **PKR 40,000**.

Total return: PKR 49,000 on a PKR 100,000 investment over three years. That is a 49 percent total return, or roughly 14 percent per year compounded. Tax (capital gains tax on the sale, dividend tax on the dividends) would reduce this in practice. The exact rates change with each Federal Budget, so check current rules in [Taxes and Zakat](/docs/category/taxes--zakat).

## What can go wrong

Stocks can also return less than zero. If the company performs badly, the share price can fall below your purchase price, and selling means realising a loss. In rare cases, companies go bankrupt and shares become worthless.

Three principles reduce this risk:

1. [Diversification](/docs/investing-101/diversification): never put your entire savings into one company.
2. **Long time horizon**: short-term losses recover more often than not over five-plus year horizons.
3. **Fundamental research**: understanding what you are buying. The [Fundamental Analysis](/docs/category/fundamental-analysis) section is dedicated to this.

## Key takeaways

- A stock is a small unit of ownership in a company.
- Stocks are created through IPOs and then trade among investors on PSX.
- Stocks pay you back through capital gains and through dividends, often both.
- Returns are not guaranteed; companies can underperform or fail.
- Diversification, time, and research are the three classical defences against single-stock risk.
