# Types of Shares: Ordinary vs. Preferred

When you buy "a share" of a Pakistani company, you are almost always buying an **ordinary share**. But it is not the only kind. Some companies issue **preferred shares** as well, and the difference between the two affects voting power, dividend behaviour, and what happens if the company is ever wound up. If you have ever wondered why one share of the same company can trade differently from another share of the same company, this is usually why.

## What you'll learn

- The two main types of shares Pakistani companies issue
- How ordinary and preferred shares differ in voting, dividends, and asset claims
- Why preferred shares are often called "hybrid" securities
- Which one most retail investors actually buy

## Ordinary shares

**Ordinary shares** (sometimes called common shares) are the standard form of company ownership and the kind most retail investors hold.

- **Voting rights.** One ordinary share usually carries one vote in shareholder meetings, where shareholders vote on board members, major business decisions, and other governance matters.
- **Dividends.** Ordinary shareholders receive dividends only after preferred shareholders have been paid theirs. The amount is not fixed and changes year to year.
- **Claim on assets.** If the company is wound up, ordinary shareholders are last in line. Lenders, suppliers, tax authorities, and preferred shareholders are all paid first. Whatever is left over (the **residual**, covered in [Equity vs. Debt](/docs/investment-basics/equity-vs-debt)) is divided among ordinary shareholders.

Because ordinary shares carry the most risk (last in line) and the most upside (unlimited share of profits), they are the kind that produce both the highest historical long-term returns and the largest paper losses during downturns.

## Preferred shares

**Preferred shares** sit between debt and equity. They are sometimes called a **hybrid security** because they share characteristics with both.

- **Dividends.** Preferred shareholders receive a **fixed dividend** at a fixed rate, similar to interest on a bond. They get this dividend before any ordinary shareholder gets a rupee.
- **Voting rights.** Preferred shareholders generally do **not** have voting rights. They give up governance influence in exchange for the predictable dividend.
- **Claim on assets.** In a wind-up, preferred shareholders are paid before ordinary shareholders, but after all debt holders. They sit in the middle of the priority list.

This combination makes preferred shares closer in behaviour to a bond than to an ordinary share, while still being technically equity. They are issued by some Pakistani companies, especially banks and large industrial groups, but they are far less common than ordinary shares.

## Side-by-side

| | Ordinary shares | Preferred shares |
|---|---|---|
| **Voting rights** | Yes (typically one vote per share) | Usually no |
| **Dividend** | Variable, paid only if declared | Fixed, paid before ordinary dividends |
| **Claim in wind-up** | Last (residual) | After debt, before ordinary |
| **Upside** | Unlimited (share in profits and price growth) | Capped at the fixed dividend |
| **Downside** | Total loss possible | Higher protection than ordinary |
| **Behaves like** | Pure equity | A hybrid of bond and equity |

## Which one most retail investors buy

For nearly every retail investor on PSX, the answer is **ordinary shares**. They are by far the most common type of share traded, they are what stock-picking strategies and fundamental analysis are designed for, and they offer the long-term capital growth that draws most people to the stock market in the first place.

Preferred shares are worth knowing about, but they are typically used by sophisticated investors looking for steady income with somewhat lower risk than ordinary equity, and they tend to trade in lower volumes. If you are starting out, your portfolio will almost certainly be made up of ordinary shares, mutual funds, and ETFs.

## Key takeaways

- Pakistani companies issue two main types of shares: ordinary and preferred.
- Ordinary shares offer voting rights, variable dividends, and a residual claim on assets.
- Preferred shares offer fixed dividends, no voting rights, and a senior claim ahead of ordinary shareholders.
- Preferred shares are a hybrid between equity and debt; ordinary shares are pure equity.
- Most retail investors hold ordinary shares.
