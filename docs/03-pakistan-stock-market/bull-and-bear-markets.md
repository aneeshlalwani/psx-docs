# Bull and Bear Markets

If you have followed any market news for more than a few weeks, you have come across the words **bull** and **bear**. They are the financial industry's shorthand for two opposite states of the market. A bull market is when prices are rising and confidence is high; a bear market is when prices are falling and pessimism dominates. Most investors live through several of each in a typical career, and how they behave during each one determines a meaningful part of their long-term returns.

## What you'll learn

- The traditional definitions of bull and bear markets
- The four characteristics that define each one
- A brief look at the Pakistani market through bull and bear cycles
- Common mistakes investors make in each environment
- How to keep a long-term perspective regardless of which one you are in

## Where the names come from

Two competing folk explanations exist for why these animals stand for rising and falling markets. The most common: a bull attacks by thrusting its horns **upward**, while a bear strikes by swiping its paws **downward**. The metaphor stuck. Whatever the origin, the convention is now universal across global markets.

A useful rule of thumb: a **bull market** is typically defined as a sustained rise of **20 percent or more** from a recent low, while a **bear market** is a sustained fall of **20 percent or more** from a recent high. Anything smaller is usually called a **correction** (a moderate drop) or a **rally** (a moderate rise) rather than a full bull or bear market.

## Bull market characteristics

A bull market is more than just rising prices. Four features tend to show up together.

1. **Rising prices on increasing volume.** The trend is consistently upward, and trading activity grows as more investors join in.
2. **High investor confidence.** People are optimistic about the economy, corporate profits, and their own financial futures. Stories of overnight wealth circulate. New investors enter the market for the first time.
3. **More buying than selling.** Demand exceeds supply for most stocks. Even average companies see their share prices rise because there is so much money chasing equity exposure.
4. **Economic tailwinds.** Bull markets often (but not always) coincide with strong GDP growth, low inflation, falling interest rates, and improving corporate earnings.

In Pakistan, the KSE-100 has been through multiple bull-market periods over its decades-long history. As recent context, the [KSE-100 returned more than 50 percent over the year ending in mid-2026](https://tradingeconomics.com/pakistan/stock-market), which is bull-market territory by any reasonable definition.

## Bear market characteristics

A bear market is the inverse.

1. **Falling prices on heavy volume.** The trend is consistently downward, and panic selling can spike volume in the worst weeks.
2. **Low investor confidence.** Pessimism dominates. Analysts forecast further declines. Retail investors who entered near the top experience real losses and become emotionally unwilling to hold equity at all.
3. **More selling than buying.** Supply exceeds demand. Prices fall even on neutral news because anyone holding shares is increasingly desperate to exit.
4. **Economic headwinds.** Bear markets often coincide with rising inflation, rising interest rates, currency stress, slowing GDP, geopolitical shocks, or banking crises.

The Pakistani market has been through several bear phases over its history, often coinciding with broader political or economic stress. The recovery, in every previous case, has eventually come; long-term investors who held through downturns came out ahead. Short-term investors who sold at the bottom locked in their losses.

## What investors typically do wrong

Behaviour predictably goes wrong at both extremes.

### In a bull market

- **Chasing returns.** New investors pile into whatever has been going up the most, often near the top.
- **Concentrating in hot stocks.** Diversification gets abandoned in favour of the year's best performers.
- **Increasing leverage.** Some investors borrow to buy more, magnifying gains during the bull and losses during the eventual reversal.
- **Believing "this time is different".** The phrase that has historically marked the late stage of every major bubble.

### In a bear market

- **Panic selling at the bottom.** Investors who held through the early decline finally capitulate when their pain becomes unbearable, often within a few weeks of the eventual low.
- **Avoiding the market entirely afterwards.** The new investor who lost money in their first downturn often refuses to re-enter even when valuations are most attractive.
- **Switching strategies repeatedly.** Trying day trading, then bonds, then gold, then crypto, in search of whatever is currently working, instead of sticking to a long-term plan.

## How to keep perspective

Three habits help.

1. **Define your time horizon in writing, before either kind of market starts.** A ten-year horizon does not change because of a six-month decline.
2. **Use rebalancing, not timing.** When stocks have outperformed (bull), trim your equity allocation back to your target weight. When stocks have underperformed (bear), buy more to bring the weight back up. This automatically buys low and sells high.
3. **Check the index, not the headlines.** A 1.5 percent down day in the KSE-100 will be reported as if Pakistan is collapsing. The same down day looks like ordinary weekly noise on a five-year chart.

The page on [Investor Psychology](/docs/investing-101/investor-psychology) covers the cyclical emotions that drive most of these mistakes, with a more detailed nine-stage market-emotion cycle.

## Key takeaways

- A bull market is a sustained rise of 20 percent or more from a recent low; a bear market is a sustained fall of 20 percent or more from a recent high.
- Bull markets feature rising prices, high confidence, more buyers than sellers, and economic tailwinds.
- Bear markets feature falling prices, fear, more sellers than buyers, and economic headwinds.
- Common bull-market mistakes: chasing returns, concentration, leverage, "this time is different".
- Common bear-market mistakes: panic-selling at the bottom, avoiding the market afterwards, switching strategies repeatedly.
- Defining your horizon, rebalancing on schedule, and checking long-term charts instead of daily headlines are the three habits that keep you steady through both.
