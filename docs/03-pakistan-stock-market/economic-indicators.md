# Economic Indicators

Stock prices do not move in a vacuum. They respond to the broader Pakistani economy, and the broader economy is itself summarised by a handful of regularly-published numbers called **economic indicators**. If you know which indicators matter and roughly how they affect equities, you can read economic news with much better understanding of what it means for your portfolio.

## What you'll learn

- The five main economic indicators that move the Pakistani stock market
- Why each one matters for equity prices
- The difference between leading, coincident, and lagging indicators
- Where to find the official numbers
- How to use indicators without being misled by them

## The five indicators that matter most

Most Pakistani equity moves driven by macro news track to one or more of these five.

### 1. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

**GDP** is the total value of all goods and services produced in Pakistan over a given period. Rising GDP signals a healthy economy: companies sell more, profits grow, and the case for owning shares improves. Falling GDP signals slowing or contracting activity, which weighs on corporate earnings and equity prices.

GDP is reported quarterly and annually by the **Pakistan Bureau of Statistics**, with reviews and revisions over time. Markets pay particular attention to GDP **growth rates** and to revisions of earlier estimates.

### 2. Inflation (CPI)

**Inflation**, measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI), captures the rate at which prices for goods and services are rising. Pakistani inflation [reached 10.9 percent in April 2026](https://www.brecorder.com/news/40419167), well above the State Bank's 5 to 7 percent target range.

Inflation matters for stocks in two ways:

- **Direct effect on companies.** Inflation raises input costs (raw materials, wages, energy). Companies that can pass costs through to customers (often consumer staples, branded goods, energy companies) maintain margins. Those that cannot see profits squeezed.
- **Indirect effect through interest rates.** Central banks usually respond to high inflation by raising interest rates, which affects equities through the channel below.

CPI is published monthly by the Pakistan Bureau of Statistics.

### 3. Interest rates (SBP policy rate)

The **State Bank of Pakistan policy rate** is the benchmark short-term interest rate set by the SBP's Monetary Policy Committee. It directly affects what banks pay on deposits, what borrowers pay on loans, and what yields government bonds offer.

The relationship to equities is consistent.

- **Higher rates** make fixed deposits and bonds more attractive relative to stocks. Some money flows out of equities into fixed income. Equity prices tend to fall.
- **Lower rates** make stocks relatively more attractive again. Some money flows the other way. Equity prices tend to rise.

This relationship is why the announcement day for SBP monetary policy decisions often produces above-average market volatility. The [SBP press releases page](https://www.sbp.org.pk/m_policy/index.asp) is the primary source for the current policy rate.

### 4. Currency (PKR/USD)

The exchange rate of the Pakistani rupee against the US dollar is one of the most-watched numbers in the country. A weaker PKR has multiple effects on the equity market.

- **Exporters benefit.** Pakistani textile, IT services, and other export-heavy companies see their PKR revenue rise when the rupee weakens against currencies they invoice in.
- **Importers and dollar-debt holders suffer.** Companies that import raw materials or carry dollar-denominated debt see costs rise.
- **Inflation rises.** A weaker rupee makes imported goods more expensive, pushing up CPI and (eventually) interest rates.
- **Foreign investors react.** A weakening rupee means foreign investors holding PSX shares lose value when they translate gains back to dollars. They often pull capital out, pressuring the market further.

### 5. Trade and current account balance

Pakistan's **trade balance** (exports minus imports) and **current account balance** (the broader measure including services and remittances) summarise the country's external accounts. A widening current-account deficit usually pressures the currency and increases the likelihood that interest rates will rise. A narrowing deficit usually points the other way.

Remittances from overseas Pakistani workers are an important part of this picture. Strong remittance flows offset trade deficits and support the currency.

## Leading, coincident, and lagging

Economic indicators are usually classified by their timing relationship to the business cycle.

- **Leading indicators** turn before the broader economy turns. Examples: stock-market index moves themselves, building permits, new export orders, business confidence surveys.
- **Coincident indicators** move with the economy in real time. Examples: industrial production, retail sales, employment.
- **Lagging indicators** turn after the economy has already turned. Examples: unemployment rate, inflation, interest rates.

Inflation and interest rates often **lag** the business cycle, which is why the central bank can be raising rates even as the economy is already slowing, and cutting rates well into a recovery. This timing mismatch is one of the reasons macro investing is hard.

## Where to find the numbers

The primary sources for Pakistani economic data are usually free and authoritative.

- **State Bank of Pakistan (SBP)** publishes the policy rate, monetary policy statements, currency reserves, and a range of monetary indicators at [sbp.org.pk](https://www.sbp.org.pk/).
- **Pakistan Bureau of Statistics (PBS)** publishes GDP, CPI, and labour-market data at [pbs.gov.pk](https://www.pbs.gov.pk/).
- **Federal Board of Revenue (FBR)** publishes tax-collection data at [fbr.gov.pk](https://www.fbr.gov.pk/).
- **PSX Data Portal** publishes market data and reports at [dps.psx.com.pk](https://dps.psx.com.pk/).

Reading the actual press releases from these sources, even occasionally, is more useful than relying entirely on second-hand summaries from news headlines.

## How to use indicators without being misled

Three principles.

1. **Direction matters more than levels.** Whether inflation is rising or falling matters more for equity prices than the specific rate today. Markets respond to the rate of change.
2. **Surprises move markets, not the data itself.** If the market expects inflation of 11 percent and the actual print is 11 percent, prices move very little. If the actual print is 13 percent, prices move sharply. The gap between expectations and reality is what matters.
3. **Macro is hard to time.** Even professional macro investors often get the timing wrong. For most retail investors, the right approach is to maintain a diversified long-term portfolio that can weather different macro environments, not to trade aggressively on each new data point.

## Key takeaways

- Five economic indicators dominate Pakistani equity-market behaviour: GDP, inflation, interest rates, the PKR/USD rate, and the trade or current-account balance.
- Inflation hurts companies through input costs and indirectly through higher interest rates.
- Higher interest rates pull capital out of equities into fixed income; lower rates do the opposite.
- A weaker rupee benefits exporters and hurts importers, while pushing up inflation.
- The primary sources for Pakistani economic data are SBP, PBS, FBR, and PSX. Reading press releases occasionally is more useful than headlines.
- Direction and surprise matter more than the absolute level of any indicator on any given day.
