# Market Participants

A stock exchange is not a single thing. It is a coordinated set of organisations and people, each with a specific role. Knowing who plays which role makes everything else easier to understand: where to take a complaint, who actually holds your shares, who sets the rules, and who handles the money. This page maps the cast of characters around PSX and what each one does.

## What you'll learn

- The five main groups of PSX market participants
- The two kinds of investors and how they differ
- The role of brokers and how to think about choosing one
- The four regulatory and infrastructure organisations behind every trade
- How market makers and authorised intermediaries fit in

## The five groups

PSX brings five groups together.

1. **Listed companies** (issuers) whose shares are bought and sold.
2. **Investors** (retail and institutional) who buy and sell those shares.
3. **Brokers** (intermediaries) who execute trades on behalf of investors.
4. **Regulators and infrastructure organisations**: SECP, PSX, NCCPL, CDC.
5. **Specialised intermediaries**: market makers, authorised participants, asset management companies, and registrars.

Each one has a specific job, and the system works because each one does its own job and stays out of the others' way.

## 1. Listed companies

A **listed company** is a Pakistani public company whose shares trade on PSX. Listing requires meeting minimum size, profitability, and disclosure standards set jointly by SECP and PSX, and once listed the company has ongoing obligations (quarterly and annual financial reports, disclosure of material events, corporate-governance rules).

Listed companies come from every major sector of the Pakistani economy: cement, banks, fertilisers, oil and gas, fuel, automobiles, pharmaceuticals, technology, food, and many more. Each listed company is an issuer of one or more classes of [shares](/docs/investment-basics/types-of-shares) that you can buy.

## 2. Investors

PSX has two broad investor types.

### Retail investors

Individuals investing their personal savings, typically through a brokerage account. Retail investors range from people putting in PKR 5,000 to high-net-worth individuals deploying lacs or crores. They buy and sell relatively small amounts compared to institutions, but collectively they make up a meaningful share of daily trading volume.

### Institutional investors

Larger entities investing on behalf of others or themselves. Examples in Pakistan include:

- **Mutual funds** (managed by AMCs like Al Meezan, NBP Funds, HBL Asset Management, UBL Fund Managers, MCB, ABL).
- **Pension funds** managing retirement savings.
- **Insurance companies** investing premiums to fund future claims.
- **Banks** with treasury portfolios.
- **Foreign institutional investors** allocated to Pakistan as part of an emerging-market strategy.

Institutional investors trade in much larger sizes and have more research resources, which is why retail investors face structural disadvantages when trying to time the market against them. The page on [Investor Psychology](/docs/investing-101/investor-psychology) covers this dynamic.

## 3. Brokers

A **broker** is a SECP-licensed intermediary who places buy and sell orders on PSX on your behalf. You cannot trade on PSX directly; the exchange only deals with its registered **trading members**, and a broker is what gives you access to that pipeline.

Pakistan has a substantial number of brokers, ranging from large bank-owned brokers to independent firms. The [Choosing a Broker](/docs/getting-started-with-psx/choosing-a-broker) page covers what to look for; the [Broker Comparison](/docs/getting-started-with-psx/choosing-a-broker) section compares specific firms against the canonical column set the project uses.

A broker's responsibilities to you:

- **Account opening** with proper KYC and SECP-mandated documentation.
- **Order routing** to PSX's KATS engine.
- **Custody coordination** with CDC so settled shares end up in your CDC sub-account.
- **Statements and tax reports** that you can use to file your annual return and reconcile your portfolio.

Broker risk: not all brokers are equal. Larger, bank-owned brokers tend to have stronger compliance and capital strength but charge higher fees. Smaller independent brokers may charge less but carry more counterparty risk. The page on [CDC accounts](/docs/getting-started-with-psx/cdc-account) covers how a CDC Investor Account protects you against broker fraud.

## 4. Regulators and infrastructure

Four organisations sit behind every PSX trade.

### SECP (Securities and Exchange Commission of Pakistan)

The **apex regulator** for the Pakistani capital market. SECP writes the rules covering listing requirements, broker conduct, market manipulation, insider trading, and disclosure. It supervises PSX itself, all licensed brokers, AMCs, and other market participants. When something goes wrong at the regulatory level, SECP is the body that investigates and enforces.

### PSX (Pakistan Stock Exchange)

The **front-line regulator** under SECP authority. PSX runs the trading infrastructure (KATS), enforces real-time market rules (circuit breakers, listing standards, disclosure timelines), and refers serious violations up to SECP.

### NCCPL (National Clearing Company of Pakistan Limited)

Handles the **money side** of every PSX trade. NCCPL acts as the **central counterparty**: when you buy shares, NCCPL legally stands between you and the seller, guaranteeing settlement. Neither side has to trust the other; both have to trust NCCPL. This is what makes large-scale anonymous trading possible.

### CDC (Central Depository Company)

Holds **shares** electronically. Every PSX-listed share is recorded in CDC's books rather than as a physical certificate. When you buy shares, CDC records the transfer of ownership from seller to buyer; when you sell, the reverse. Your CDC sub-account (or CDC Investor Account) is where your shares actually live.

## 5. Specialised intermediaries

Beyond the main four organisations, several specialised intermediaries fill specific roles.

- **Asset Management Companies (AMCs)** like Al Meezan, NBP Funds, HBL Asset Management, UBL Fund Managers, and MCB run [mutual funds](/docs/investment-basics/mutual-funds) and ETFs. They are themselves institutional investors but also manufacture investment products that retail investors buy.
- **Market makers** quote two-way prices (bid and ask) on specific securities, providing liquidity especially for smaller and less-traded stocks.
- **Authorised participants** create and redeem ETF units, ensuring the ETF price stays close to the value of its underlying portfolio.
- **Registrars** maintain shareholder registers and process corporate actions like dividends, bonus issues, and rights issues for listed companies.
- **Investment advisers** are SECP-licensed professionals who advise investors on portfolio construction, often for a fee or asset-based percentage. The page on [Selecting a Portfolio Manager](/docs/getting-started-with-psx/selecting-a-portfolio-manager) covers how to evaluate them.

## The flow in one diagram

```text
Listed Company ─── shares ──→  PSX (KATS)  ←── orders ─── Broker ─── you (Investor)
                                   ↓
                              Match recorded
                                   ↓
                         NCCPL (cash)  +  CDC (shares)
                                   ↓
                         T+1 settlement complete
```

Above all of this sits **SECP**, with rules and oversight applying at every layer.

## Key takeaways

- PSX brings together listed companies, retail and institutional investors, brokers, regulators (SECP, PSX), infrastructure organisations (NCCPL, CDC), and specialised intermediaries.
- You access PSX only through a SECP-licensed broker; you never deal with PSX or NCCPL directly.
- SECP is the apex regulator. PSX is the front-line market regulator and operator. NCCPL handles money. CDC holds shares.
- Institutional investors trade in much larger sizes than retail and have research advantages; this is the structural reason short-term trading is hard for retail.
- Specialised intermediaries (AMCs, market makers, authorised participants, registrars, advisers) fill specific operational roles around the core market.
