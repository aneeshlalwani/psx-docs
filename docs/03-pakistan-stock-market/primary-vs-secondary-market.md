# Primary vs. Secondary Market

Most people imagine "the stock market" as one place where shares change hands. In reality, the equity market has two distinct halves with very different mechanics. The **primary market** is where shares are *created*: a company sells brand-new shares to investors and pockets the cash. The **secondary market** is where those same shares are then traded among investors, with the company no longer involved. When you press buy on your broker's app, you are almost always in the secondary market, even though most retail investors do not realise it.

## What you'll learn

- The difference between the primary and secondary markets
- How an Initial Public Offering (IPO) actually works in Pakistan
- The two flavours of secondary market trading: ready market and futures
- Why both halves matter for the market to function
- The role of stock buybacks (the rare case where a company re-enters the secondary market)

## The primary market

The **primary market** is where new securities are created and sold for the very first time. Companies and governments use it to raise capital directly.

The most visible kind of primary-market transaction is an **Initial Public Offering** (IPO). The flow is roughly:

1. A privately held Pakistani company decides it wants to grow and needs capital.
2. It hires investment bankers (often called the **lead manager**) to structure and price the offering.
3. It files documents with SECP and PSX, including a detailed prospectus disclosing the business, financials, risks, and use of proceeds.
4. The company sets an offer price (or a price band) and opens subscription. Both institutional and retail investors apply for shares.
5. On listing day, the shares begin trading on PSX. The company has now received the capital it raised, and the shares are in the hands of their first owners.

After the IPO, the company can return to the primary market again later through a **right shares** issue or a follow-on offering, but each of those is a separate event.

**The defining feature of the primary market is that the money flows from investors to the company.** The company receives the cash; investors receive newly created shares.

## The secondary market

The **secondary market** is where existing shares are traded among investors. This is what most people mean when they say "the stock market".

When you buy 100 shares of a Pakistani company on PSX:

- The seller is another investor (or institution), not the company.
- The cash goes to that seller, not to the company.
- The company is not party to the transaction.
- The shares are simply transferring ownership from one private holder to another.

The company benefits indirectly from a healthy secondary market: a higher share price makes it cheaper to raise capital in the future, makes existing shareholders feel wealthier, and signals strength to lenders and customers. But none of the cash from secondary-market trades actually reaches the company.

The secondary market exists because investors want **liquidity**: the ability to sell their holdings whenever they need to. Without a working secondary market, holding a share would be like holding a private partnership interest with no easy way to exit. Few people would buy in.

## Two halves of the secondary market: ready and futures

PSX's secondary market is itself divided into two segments.

### Ready market (spot market)

The **ready market** is where most trades happen. Buyers and sellers transact for **immediate** delivery, with the trade settled on the standard PSX cycle (now T+1, since 9 February 2026). When a retail investor on a broker's app buys 100 shares of a company, that trade goes through the ready market.

### Futures market

The **futures market** is where investors trade **contracts** to buy or sell a security at a predetermined price on a future date. PSX offers several futures products including stock-index futures and deliverable futures on individual companies.

Futures are used by:

- **Hedgers:** investors with existing positions who want to lock in a price to protect against adverse moves.
- **Speculators:** traders betting on short-term price direction without committing the full capital required to buy the underlying shares.

Futures contracts have an expiration date (typically one to three months in Pakistan). At expiration, they are either settled (cash or physical delivery) or rolled over into a new contract. They are higher-risk than ready-market shares and are not appropriate for most beginners.

## Stock buybacks: the exception

There is one case where a company does come back into the secondary market: a **stock buyback**, where the company purchases its own shares from existing investors. This effectively reverses part of the original IPO, reducing the share count and concentrating ownership among remaining shareholders.

Buybacks are subject to regulatory rules and disclosure. They are used when a company has surplus cash, believes its shares are undervalued, or wants to return capital to shareholders without paying a dividend.

## Worked example

A new Pakistani technology company, "Aseem Tech Limited", wants to raise PKR 500 crore for expansion.

- **Primary market:** It launches an IPO at PKR 50 per share, offering 10 crore (100 million) shares. Investors (institutional and retail) collectively pay PKR 500 crore for these shares. **The PKR 500 crore goes to Aseem Tech Limited**, which uses it for expansion. The investors now hold the shares.
- **Secondary market:** A retail investor who applied for 1,000 shares at IPO and was allocated 500 decides to sell 200 of them three months later. She finds a buyer on PSX at PKR 65 per share. The PKR 13,000 (200 × 65) flows from the buyer to the original investor. **Aseem Tech Limited receives nothing** from this transaction; it has already received its IPO money.

Both transactions are real. They just have different mechanics, different parties, and very different consequences for the company.

## Key takeaways

- The primary market is where shares are created and sold for the first time. Cash flows to the company.
- The secondary market is where existing shares are traded among investors. Cash flows between investors.
- Most retail trading happens in the secondary market.
- The secondary market exists to give investors liquidity, which makes them more willing to participate in the primary market in the first place.
- PSX's secondary market splits into a ready (spot) market and a futures market.
- Stock buybacks are the rare case where a listed company returns to the secondary market.
