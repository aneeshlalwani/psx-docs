# PSX Indices

When a TV anchor says "the market is up 1.2 percent today", the market they are referring to is almost always one specific number: the KSE-100. A **stock-market index** is a single number designed to represent the overall performance of a broader group of shares, calculated from the prices of those shares. PSX publishes several indices, and which one you watch depends on what slice of the market you care about.

## What you'll learn

- What a market index actually is and why it exists
- The difference between a price-return and a total-return index
- Pakistan's main four indices: KSE-100, KSE-30, KMI-30, and KMI All-Share
- How free-float methodology works and why it matters
- Practical uses of indices: benchmarks, sentiment, and passive investing

## What an index is

A **market index** is a calculated number that summarises the performance of a chosen basket of stocks. It rises when the basket on average rises and falls when the basket falls. The index itself is not directly tradable; it is a statistic. But you can buy products (index funds, ETFs) that hold the same stocks in the same proportions, which lets you "own the index" in practical terms.

Indices solve a real problem. Without them, no single number captures whether the Pakistani equity market did well today. With them, anyone can tell at a glance.

### Price return vs. total return

Two ways an index can be calculated.

- **Price-return index:** tracks only the change in the prices of the constituent shares. It ignores any dividends paid out.
- **Total-return index:** tracks both price changes **and** dividends, assuming dividends are reinvested. It gives a more accurate picture of what an investor actually earns.

The KSE-100, KSE-30, and KMI-30 are all calculated as **total-return** indices, meaning they reflect the full investor experience including reinvested dividends.

## KSE-100

The **KSE-100 Index** is the headline benchmark of the Pakistani equity market. The numbers most Pakistanis encounter as "the stock market did X today" almost always refer to this index.

Per the [PSX KSE-100 methodology document](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/uploads/KSE_100_Index_New_Brochure.pdf):

- **Launched:** November 1991, with a base value of 1,000 points.
- **Constituents:** 100 companies. 35 are selected as the largest free-float-cap company from each PSX sector (one per sector, excluding open-end mutual funds), and the remaining 65 are picked by largest free-float capitalisation regardless of sector.
- **Coverage:** captures around 80 percent of the total free-float market capitalisation of all PSX-listed companies.
- **Methodology:** free-float capitalisation weighted (since October 2012). Total return.

What "free-float" means in practice: only the shares that are actually available for public trading count toward the index weight. Shares held by the government, founders, or strategic investors who do not regularly trade are excluded from the calculation, so the index reflects market liquidity rather than just paper ownership.

## KSE-30

The **KSE-30 Index** tracks the 30 most **liquid** stocks on PSX, where liquidity is measured by trading activity. While the KSE-100 emphasises broad market representation, the KSE-30 emphasises tradability. The thirty companies that make it into the KSE-30 are typically large, frequently traded names where institutional investors and ETFs can deploy meaningful capital without moving the price.

Like the KSE-100, the KSE-30 uses free-float methodology and is a total-return index.

## KMI-30 (the Shariah benchmark)

The **KSE Meezan Index 30 (KMI-30)** is a Shariah-compliant equity index. It contains 30 PSX-listed companies that pass a set of Shariah-screening criteria managed in partnership with **Meezan Bank**.

To be eligible for KMI-30, a company must satisfy several rules covering both its primary business activity and its financial structure:

- **Core business activity** must not be incompatible with Shariah (so no conventional banks, alcohol producers, gambling, or similar).
- **Interest-bearing debt to total assets** must be below a defined threshold.
- **Non-Shariah-compliant investments to total assets** must be below a defined threshold.
- **Income from non-Shariah-compliant sources** must be below a defined threshold of total revenue.
- **Illiquid assets to total assets** must exceed a defined threshold.
- **Market price per share** must exceed the company's net liquid assets per share.

The exact thresholds change occasionally and are managed by the Shariah board. The [Shariah Investing](/docs/category/shariah-investing) section covers the screening rules and the concept of **purification** of any non-compliant income in detail.

KMI-30 is the most-referenced Shariah benchmark for Pakistani equity, and a number of Shariah-compliant mutual funds and ETFs track it.

## KMI All-Share

The **KMI All-Share Index** is broader than KMI-30. It includes every PSX-listed company that passes the Shariah-screening criteria, not just the top 30 by free-float capitalisation. It is useful for investors who want a wider Shariah-compliant benchmark or who run Shariah-compliant strategies that are not constrained to the largest names.

## Sector and style indices

Beyond the four headline indices above, PSX publishes a range of **sector** indices (oil and gas, banks, fertiliser, cement, technology, and so on) and **style** indices for specific characteristics (growth, value, dividend, smaller-cap). Sector indices help investors track and benchmark slices of the market they are particularly interested in. Style indices are less commonly referenced by retail investors but matter for institutional portfolios.

## Practical uses of indices

Three reasons indices matter for retail investors.

1. **Sentiment gauge.** A glance at the KSE-100 tells you whether the market overall is having a good or bad day, week, or year. This is far more useful than tracking individual stocks for a quick read.
2. **Performance benchmark.** When you compare your portfolio's annual return to the KSE-100 (or KMI-30, if you invest Shariah-compliant), you can tell whether your stock-picking is actually adding value or whether you would be better off in a passive index fund.
3. **Passive investing.** Index-tracking [mutual funds](/docs/investment-basics/mutual-funds) and [ETFs](/docs/investment-basics/etfs) let you buy the entire basket at once, without picking individual stocks. For most retail investors this is the simplest path to diversified equity exposure.

## Key takeaways

- A market index is a single number that summarises a basket of stocks, calculated by a defined formula.
- KSE-100 is the headline Pakistani benchmark, launched November 1991, base 1,000, free-float, 100 companies, total-return.
- KSE-30 tracks the 30 most liquid PSX stocks.
- KMI-30 is the main Shariah-compliant benchmark, with explicit screening rules covering business activity and financial ratios.
- KMI All-Share extends the Shariah universe to every compliant PSX-listed company.
- Indices are useful as sentiment gauges, performance benchmarks, and the basis for passive index investing.
