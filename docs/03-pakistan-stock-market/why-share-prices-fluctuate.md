# Why Share Prices Fluctuate

Open a price chart of any PSX-listed stock and you will see something obvious: prices move constantly. Up a paisa, down a paisa, up a rupee, down a rupee, all day long, every day the market is open. There is no central authority setting prices. No one decides that a share of Lucky Cement should be worth Rs. 800 today and Rs. 815 tomorrow. The price is set, in real time, by the collision of buyers and sellers in the order book. This page explains the three forces behind that constant motion.

## What you'll learn

- The fundamental mechanism that sets every share price: supply and demand
- Why "market sentiment" is more than a vague word
- The external factors that can move prices in seconds
- Why short-term price moves can look noisy and meaningless
- How long-term investors should think about daily fluctuations

## The basic mechanism: supply and demand

Every share price on PSX is the most recent price at which a buyer and a seller agreed to transact. Nothing more. There is no committee setting prices, no government rate sheet. The exchange's KATS engine simply matches buy and sell orders, and the last matched price is what shows on the screen.

This means the price is the **clearing price** of supply and demand at that moment.

- When **demand** for a share exceeds **supply** at the current price, the next buyer has to bid higher to convince a seller. The price rises.
- When **supply** exceeds **demand**, the next seller has to ask a lower price to find a buyer. The price falls.

Every news item, every analyst report, every retail investor's mood feeds into this single mechanism by changing the willingness of buyers and sellers to trade at given prices.

## Three forces that change supply and demand

If supply and demand set the price, the question becomes what changes supply and demand. Three categories of force show up most often.

### 1. Information about the company itself

The most direct driver. When a company announces:

- Strong quarterly results
- A large new contract
- An attractive acquisition or merger
- A regulatory approval

...buyers want to own the share at the current price more, sellers are reluctant to part with it cheaply, and the price rises. Conversely, missed earnings, a major lawsuit, fraud allegations, the loss of a key client, or executive departures push the price down.

Information theoretically reaches all market participants at the same moment because of disclosure rules. In practice, traders with faster systems and better analysis can act first, which is one reason short-term trading is hard for retail investors.

### 2. Market sentiment

**Sentiment** is the prevailing mood of the market: optimism or pessimism not directly tied to specific company news. Sentiment can move share prices even on days when nothing material has happened at any specific company.

Common drivers of sentiment shifts:

- A crisis in a related market (oil price collapse, bank failure abroad, currency stress at home)
- Political uncertainty (elections, policy changes, regulatory shifts)
- Technical signals (a major index breaking through a long-watched level)
- Herd behaviour: when everyone else is buying, the urge to buy is strong even without a fresh reason to

The page on [Investor Psychology](/docs/investing-101/investor-psychology) covers sentiment and the herd dynamic in more depth.

### 3. External factors

Many things outside the stock market affect stock prices.

- **Macroeconomic data.** Inflation prints, GDP figures, current account numbers, central bank policy moves.
- **Currency moves.** A weaker PKR makes imports more expensive (bad for some companies), benefits exporters (good for others), and changes the relative attractiveness of holding PKR-denominated assets at all.
- **Interest rates.** When the [SBP raises the policy rate](https://www.sbp.org.pk/), bonds become more attractive relative to stocks, which usually drags down equity prices in the short term. Rate cuts often have the opposite effect.
- **Global market moves.** Major moves in US, European, or regional Asian markets often spill into PSX through investor sentiment and through the behaviour of foreign institutional investors.
- **Political events.** Election outcomes, cabinet changes, regulatory announcements, geopolitical tensions.
- **Natural events.** Floods, monsoon variations, supply disruptions can directly affect specific sectors (agriculture, fuel, transport) and indirectly affect overall sentiment.

The page on [Economic Indicators](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/economic-indicators) covers the macro side in more detail.

## Why short-term moves often look meaningless

If you watch a share price for a single day, you will see dozens of small moves with no obvious cause. This is normal. Most intraday volatility is **noise** rather than signal: the byproduct of large institutional orders being filled in pieces, of automated systems reacting to small price moves, and of brief mismatches between buyers and sellers.

A useful mental separation:

- **Noise:** moves of less than a percent or two on no specific news. Not worth analysing.
- **Signal:** moves of several percent on identifiable news, sustained over multiple sessions, and ideally on higher-than-usual volume.

Most retail investors who lose money do so by treating noise as signal: panicking when a stock drops 3 percent on no news, or rushing in when one rises 4 percent on no news.

## How to think about daily fluctuations

For a long-term investor, the framing is simple. The day-to-day price moves you see are the visible byproduct of millions of small decisions across thousands of participants. Over short horizons, they are dominated by noise. Over long horizons, they converge on the underlying fundamentals: how profitable the company actually is, how fast it grows, and how much of that profit reaches shareholders.

Watching prices daily, especially during stressful market periods, is one of the most reliable ways to make worse decisions. The investor who checks once a quarter and reads the financial reports tends to act calmer and earn more than the one who watches a price ticker every morning.

## Key takeaways

- Share prices are set by supply and demand in PSX's order book. There is no central authority setting them.
- Three forces change supply and demand: company-specific information, market sentiment, and external factors (macro data, rates, currency, politics, global markets).
- Most intraday moves are noise rather than signal. Not all price changes mean something.
- Short-term volatility is normal. Long-term prices converge on company fundamentals.
- Watching prices daily makes most retail investors worse, not better, at investing.
