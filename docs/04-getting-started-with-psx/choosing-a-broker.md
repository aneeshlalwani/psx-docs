# Choosing a Broker

Your broker is the gateway between you and the Pakistan Stock Exchange. They place your orders, hold your shares (in a Sub-Account), and provide the platform you use every day. They are also one of the few decisions that affects you for years and can be expensive to undo. This page walks through how to evaluate brokers in Pakistan, what to look for, and what to be cautious about.

## What you'll learn

- The seven criteria that matter when choosing a broker
- The difference between bank-owned and independent brokers
- The difference between traditional and Sahulat brokers
- Red flags to watch for
- How to use the broker comparison framework on this site

## The seven criteria that matter

When evaluating brokers, work through these seven questions in order.

### 1. SECP registration

Before anything else, verify that the broker is a **SECP-licensed Trading Right Entitlement Certificate (TREC) holder** of PSX. A broker without proper licensing is not legal to operate. Always check directly on the SECP and PSX websites rather than trusting marketing material.

### 2. Financial strength

A broker holds your money (briefly, during settlement) and your shares (in a Sub-Account, if you use one). If the broker fails financially, recovering your assets can be slow and painful. Look for:

- **Bank ownership.** Brokers owned by major Pakistani banks generally have stronger capital and tighter compliance.
- **Length of operation.** A broker that has been continuously licensed for 10+ years has been through multiple market cycles.
- **Public reputation.** Search for the broker's name plus terms like "fraud", "scandal", "court case", "complaint" on Google. Past disputes are public record.

### 3. Account types offered

Confirm that the broker offers what you need. The two main account types are:

- **Sahulat Account:** simplified, capped at Rs. 3 million, regular market only. Ideal for first-time investors.
- **Normal trading account:** full access including leverage and futures. Required if you want to invest more than Rs. 3 million per broker or trade in any market beyond the regular one.

The page on [Opening an Account](/docs/getting-started-with-psx/opening-an-account) covers both in detail.

### 4. Fees and charges

Broker fees fall into a few buckets:

- **Account opening fee** (one-time, sometimes zero).
- **Brokerage commission** (charged on every trade, usually as a percentage of trade value with a minimum amount). Rates often differ by trade size and by **filer vs non-filer** status. The page on [Filer vs Non-Filer](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/filer-vs-non-filer) explains why this matters.
- **Account maintenance fee** (usually annual).
- **Custody / CDC charges** (passed through from CDC).
- **Withholding tax and capital gains tax** (deducted by NCCPL on your behalf, not the broker's fee but visible in your statements).

Always ask for the broker's full fee schedule in writing before opening an account. Verbal quotes are not enforceable.

### 5. Trading platform and mobile app

You will use the platform daily. Test the demo or the public app before committing.

- **Reliability** during market hours (test it during peak times).
- **Order types supported** (market, limit, stop, day vs good-till-cancelled).
- **Mobile app quality** on both iOS and Android.
- **Real-time data** vs delayed quotes.
- **Statements and reporting** (clear PDF statements, useful tax reports).

### 6. Customer service

When something goes wrong (a trade did not execute, a transfer is delayed, a corporate action was missed), you need a responsive broker. Test the customer-service channel before committing:

- Call them during market hours and time the response.
- Send an email and time the reply.
- Check whether they have a physical office you can visit.
- Read recent reviews on independent platforms.

A broker that is hard to reach during a normal week will be impossible to reach during a market crisis.

### 7. Research and educational content

Some brokers provide research reports, market summaries, and educational content as part of the relationship. Others are bare-bones order-execution providers. Neither is wrong. Decide which model suits you. Beginners often benefit from research-rich brokers; experienced traders sometimes prefer cheaper, no-frills brokers.

## Bank-owned vs independent

Pakistani brokers fall into two broad categories.

| Bank-owned | Independent |
|---|---|
| Subsidiaries of major banks (HBL, MCB, NBP, AKD, BMA, etc.) | Standalone licensed firms (Topline Securities, Arif Habib Limited, JS Global, Foundation Securities, IGI Securities, and many others) |
| Generally stronger compliance and capital | Often more competitive on fees, more focused on retail experience |
| May charge higher fees on small trades | May have less brand-name reassurance |
| Often integrated with the parent bank's online banking | Standalone platforms |

Both kinds have produced both excellent and disappointing client experiences. The seven criteria above matter more than the bank-owned vs independent split.

## Traditional vs Sahulat brokers

A second cut: not every broker offers Sahulat Accounts. The PSX maintains a list of brokers that participate in the Sahulat scheme. If you specifically want to start with a Sahulat Account, confirm that your prospective broker is on that list before going through the application process.

## Red flags

Walk away if you see any of the following.

- **No SECP licence verifiable on the SECP website.** No exceptions.
- **Promises of guaranteed returns.** Investments do not work that way. Anyone making this promise is misrepresenting risk.
- **Pressure to make trades immediately.** A legitimate broker informs and executes; they do not pressure.
- **Reluctance to provide a written fee schedule.** Vague answers about fees are a sign of bigger problems.
- **No physical office.** Online onboarding is fine, but the broker should still have a verifiable physical address.
- **Past fraud cases or unresolved complaints** that show up in public search.

## Key takeaways

- Verify SECP registration before anything else.
- Evaluate brokers on seven criteria: registration, financial strength, account types, fees, platform, customer service, research.
- Bank-owned and independent brokers both have valid use cases; the seven criteria matter more than the category.
- Confirm Sahulat-scheme participation if you specifically want a Sahulat Account.
- Walk away from any broker that promises guaranteed returns, refuses to provide a written fee schedule, or fails the SECP verification.
