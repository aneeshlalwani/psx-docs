# Your First Trade

If you have an open trading account, an active CDC sub-account, and at least Rs. 5,000 in your broker account, you are ready to place your first trade on PSX. The mechanics are simple and the entire process takes minutes. The hard parts are the decisions you make before you press the button. This page walks through the full flow, end to end, so you know what to expect and where the pitfalls are.

## What you'll learn

- A pre-flight checklist for your first trade
- How to fund your trading account
- How to choose what to buy
- The difference between a market order and a limit order

## Pre-flight checklist

Before you place a single order, confirm:

- [ ] Your trading account is open and you can log in.
- [ ] Your CDC Sub-Account is linked to the trading account.
- [ ] Your bank account is linked for funding and withdrawals.
- [ ] You have read your broker's fee schedule.
- [ ] You have an [emergency fund](/docs/personal-finance/saving-and-emergency-fund) covering three to six months of expenses.
- [ ] The money you are about to invest is genuinely money you can leave invested for years.

If any item is unchecked, fix it before continuing.

## Step 1: fund the trading account

Money sitting in your savings account does not show up in your trading account automatically. You need to transfer it.

- **Online transfer** from your linked bank account is the standard method. Most brokers issue an Account Title and IBAN you transfer into; the funds typically reflect on the same business day.
- **Demand draft, pay order, or cheque** at the broker's office is supported by most brokers as a backup.

Confirm that the money has reflected in your trading account before placing an order. The amount you transfer should cover the trade value plus the broker's brokerage commission and any taxes that will be deducted at settlement.

## Step 2: choose what to buy

This is the hardest step on the whole page, and the one most beginners get wrong by overthinking. A few sensible defaults for a first trade.

- **Buy a stock you understand.** A consumer-facing Pakistani company whose products you actually use (a major bank you have an account with, a fertiliser company whose products are visible across Pakistan, a fuel utility you pay every month) is easier to track and reason about than something obscure.
- **Buy a large, liquid stock first.** Your first trade should not be in a thinly-traded small-cap where the bid-ask spread is wide. KSE-100 constituents are large and liquid by definition; that is a safe starting universe.
- **Or buy an index fund or ETF.** If choosing a single stock feels overwhelming, [a KSE-100-tracking mutual fund or ETF](/docs/investment-basics/etfs) gives you instant diversification across the entire benchmark in one transaction. For most beginners this is the better choice.
- **Start small.** Your first trade is for learning, not for changing your financial life. Rs. 5,000 to Rs. 20,000 is a reasonable starter amount.

The pages on [Investment Strategies](/docs/investment-basics/investment-strategies), [Diversification](/docs/investing-101/diversification), and the upcoming [Fundamental Analysis](/docs/category/fundamental-analysis) section cover how to choose stocks more rigorously over time. For the first trade, simple is fine.

## Step 3: pick your order type

Two basic order types you should understand for the first trade. The page on [Order Types](/docs/trading-and-orders/order-types) covers more variants.

### Market order

A **market order** says: "buy at whatever price the market is currently asking". The order fills immediately at the best available price. Use this when you want certainty of execution and do not care about a few paisa of price slippage.

### Limit order

A **limit order** says: "buy only at this price or lower". The order may or may not fill, depending on whether the market reaches your limit price. Use this when you want price discipline and are willing to risk the order not filling.

For a first trade in a large, liquid PSX stock during regular trading hours, a market order is usually fine. For thinly traded stocks or when you want to set a specific entry price, use a limit order.

## Step 4: place the order

The actual order entry screen on most Pakistani broker apps asks for:

- **Symbol or company name.** PSX uses three-to-five-letter ticker symbols.
- **Buy or sell.**
- **Number of shares.**
- **Order type** (market, limit, etc.).
- **Price** (if limit order).
- **Validity** (good for the day, or good-till-cancelled).

Double-check the symbol and the number of shares before submitting. Typos here are surprisingly common and surprisingly expensive.

Press confirm. Your broker forwards the order to PSX's KATS engine. If a matching opposite order exists, the trade fills, often within seconds. You receive a confirmation in the app and usually by SMS or email.

## Step 5: settlement (T+1)

Your trade is matched and confirmed today. The actual movement of money and shares happens **one business day later** under [PSX's T+1 settlement cycle](https://www.nccpl.com.pk/transition-to-t1-settlement-cycle), in effect since 9 February 2026.

What happens between trade and settlement:

- **Trade Date (T):** money is reserved in your trading account, but not yet sent. The shares are reserved at the seller's CDC account, not yet transferred.
- **T+1 (one business day later):** money flows from your trading account to the seller through NCCPL. Shares flow from the seller's CDC account to yours through CDC.

If you log in to your CDC Sub-Account on T+1, you should see the shares listed there. If they are not there by end of T+1, contact your broker.

## Step 6: keep records

From day one, build a habit of keeping records.

- **Save the trade confirmation** (PDF or screenshot).
- **Note the purchase price, date, and quantity** in a personal log or spreadsheet.
- **Save annual tax statements** from your broker for FBR filings.

This habit pays off years later when you need to compute capital gains, reconcile dividend receipts, or just remember what you actually paid.

## A small mental framing for not panicking

Two things almost always happen after a first trade:

1. **The price moves immediately.** Sometimes up, sometimes down, often by 1 to 3 percent in either direction within hours. This is normal. It means nothing about your decision.
2. **You feel the urge to monitor it constantly.** Resist this. The strongest predictor of poor first-time-investor outcomes is checking the price every hour and reacting.

The most successful Pakistani long-term investors describe their first trade as a non-event in retrospect. Place it, log it, and check back in a quarter. The pages on [Investor Psychology](/docs/investing-101/investor-psychology) and [Long-Term vs Short-Term Investing](/docs/investing-101/long-term-vs-short-term-investing) cover the emotional discipline this requires.

## Key takeaways

- Confirm your trading account, CDC Sub-Account, bank link, and emergency fund before placing a first trade.
- Fund the trading account by online transfer from your bank.
- A first trade is best done in a large liquid stock you understand, or an index fund or ETF for instant diversification.
- Use a market order for a simple first trade in a liquid stock; use a limit order if you want price discipline.
- Settlement happens on T+1, one business day after the trade.
- Keep records from day one for future tax filings.
- Resist the urge to check the price hourly. The next decade is what matters.
