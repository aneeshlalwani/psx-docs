# Selecting a Portfolio Manager

Most retail investors manage their own portfolios. A growing minority hand the work to a professional, either through a discretionary investment management arrangement or through advisory services attached to a mutual fund. Done right, this can be a good trade. Done wrong, you pay fees for years to underperform a simple index. The difference is in the questions you ask before you sign anything. This page walks through who portfolio managers are, when hiring one makes sense, and the questions that separate good from bad ones.

## What you'll learn

- The two kinds of professional you might hire

## Two kinds of professional

Not all "investment professionals" are the same.

### Investment adviser

A SECP-licensed individual or firm that **gives advice** but does not directly hold or trade your money. You retain control of the trading account; the adviser tells you what to do, and you decide whether to act. Advisers typically charge a fixed retainer or an hourly fee.

### Portfolio manager (discretionary)

A SECP-licensed individual or firm that **takes discretionary control** of your money under an investment management agreement. They make trading decisions on your behalf, within the agreed strategy. You sign over execution authority but retain ownership. Portfolio managers typically charge a percentage of assets under management, sometimes plus a performance fee.

In Pakistan, most retail clients access discretionary portfolio management indirectly, through a mutual fund where the AMC manages the fund's portfolio collectively for all unit-holders. Pure one-on-one discretionary management tends to be reserved for higher-net-worth clients (often Rs. 50 lacs and above, depending on the AMC).

## When DIY investing is enough

Hiring a manager is not always the right call. DIY investing is usually sufficient if:

- Your portfolio is small enough that fees would eat a meaningful share of returns.
- You have time to read company reports and follow markets.
- You are comfortable with [investment-basics](/docs/category/investment-basics) and [fundamental analysis](/docs/category/fundamental-analysis).
- You have the discipline to stick to a long-term strategy without panicking.
- You are happy to capture the market return through an index fund or ETF.

If any of these are not true, paying a manager (or buying actively managed mutual funds) can be worth the cost.

## The five questions to ask any prospective manager

Before you commit money to any manager, get satisfactory answers to all five.

### 1. What licences do you hold? Are you registered with SECP?

Verify their SECP registration **directly** on the SECP website. Do not take the manager's word for it. Ask:

- Specific licence numbers and categories
- How long they have held the licence
- Whether the licence has ever been suspended or revoked

A manager who is not SECP-registered is not legal to operate. End the conversation if you cannot verify registration.

### 2. Do you have any past disciplinary actions or customer complaints?

Search SECP filings, court records, and Google for the manager's name plus terms like "disciplinary", "complaint", "fraud", "lawsuit". Cross-check what you find against what the manager tells you. Past actions are not automatic disqualifiers (some are minor and old), but unwillingness to disclose them is a serious red flag.

### 3. What is your investment philosophy? What do you NOT do?

A clear philosophy ("we run a long-only Pakistani large-cap portfolio with a value tilt and average holding period of 3 to 5 years") is a sign of a serious manager. Vague answers ("we adapt to market conditions") often indicate either lack of discipline or unwillingness to commit to a strategy. Equally important is what they **refuse** to do. A manager who has clear no-go areas (no leverage, no penny stocks, no derivatives, no Shariah-incompatible companies) gives you a way to verify their behaviour later.

### 4. How are you paid, and what conflicts of interest exist?

Get the full fee structure in writing. Ask specifically about:

- Management fee (percentage of AUM, billed monthly or quarterly).
- Performance fee, if any (often a percentage of returns above a benchmark).
- Front-end load, exit load, redemption fee.
- Any commissions the manager receives from products they recommend (if they recommend a fund and earn a separate commission from it, that is a conflict you should know about).
- Minimum account balance and minimum holding period.

Conflicts of interest are not always disqualifying, but they should always be disclosed. A manager who hides them once will hide them again.

### 5. Will you sign an Investment Policy Statement?

The **Investment Policy Statement** (IPS) is a document that captures the agreed terms of the relationship: your goals, your risk tolerance, the manager's investment style, the agreed asset allocation, the schedule for performance review and rebalancing, and any constraints (Shariah compliance, ESG, sector exclusions). A manager unwilling to sign an IPS is unwilling to be held accountable to specifics. Walk away.

## Fee structures explained

Three common fee structures in Pakistan.

### Fixed fee

A flat percentage of assets under management, usually 1 to 2 percent per year, billed monthly or quarterly. Predictable and aligns moderately well with the client's interests (the manager wants assets to grow because their fee grows with it).

### Performance-based fee

A base fee plus an additional percentage of returns above a benchmark (often the KSE-100 or KMI-30, sometimes a fixed hurdle rate). Aligns the manager more strongly with returns but creates an incentive to take more risk in pursuit of the bonus.

### Fee cap

A ceiling on the total fee, often combined with a performance component. Limits the upside the manager can earn from a given account, which discourages excessive risk-taking purely to chase fees.

For most retail investors, a simple **fixed fee** structure with full transparency is the cleanest arrangement. Performance fees can work for sophisticated clients but require careful structuring to avoid bad incentives.

## What you should commit to: an IPS and an SAA

Before money flows to any manager, two documents should exist in writing.

### Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

As covered above, the IPS captures the rules of engagement: goals, time horizon, risk tolerance, special considerations (Shariah, ESG, exclusions), permitted asset classes, performance benchmarks, and review schedule. The IPS is your protection against drift in the manager's strategy.

### Strategic Asset Allocation (SAA)

The IPS combined with capital market expectations produces a **Strategic Asset Allocation**, the target weights for each asset class (equities, fixed income, gold, cash). The SAA also defines minimum and maximum bands the manager can move within. This lets the manager respond tactically to market conditions while staying inside your risk comfort zone.

Both documents should be reviewed and updated at least once a year, more often if your circumstances change significantly.

## Red flags

Walk away if you encounter any of these.

- **No verifiable SECP registration.**
- **Promises of guaranteed returns.** Investments do not work this way.
- **Refusal to provide written fees or sign an IPS.**
- **Pressure to invest immediately.** A legitimate manager understands this is a major decision.
- **Past disciplinary actions you discover the manager did not disclose.**
- **A track record cherry-picked to recent good years only.** Ask for full history.

## Key takeaways

- Two kinds of professional: investment advisers (advice only, you control trading) and portfolio managers (discretionary, they trade for you).
- DIY investing is enough for many retail investors. Hiring a manager makes sense when you lack time, knowledge, or discipline.
- Verify SECP registration before anything else.
- Ask five questions: licensing, disciplinary history, investment philosophy, fee structure and conflicts, willingness to sign an IPS.
- Common fee structures: fixed fee, performance-based fee, fee cap. Fixed fee is usually cleanest for retail.
- Always have an IPS and SAA in writing before money moves.
- Walk away from any manager who promises guaranteed returns, refuses written terms, or hides past disciplinary actions.
