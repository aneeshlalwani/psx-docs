# Bonus Shares

If you have ever opened your CDC sub-account and noticed that you suddenly own more shares than you bought, with no email about a purchase, you have probably received a **bonus issue**. Bonus shares are one of the most commonly misunderstood corporate actions on PSX, partly because the word "bonus" sounds like free money. The mechanics are not quite that, and once you understand what is actually happening, you can read bonus announcements clearly.

## What you'll learn

- What a bonus issue is and where the shares come from
- The difference between a bonus issue and a cash dividend
- A worked example showing what changes (and what does not) in your portfolio
- Why companies prefer bonus issues in some situations
- Tax treatment under FBR section 236Z

## What a bonus issue is

A **bonus issue** (sometimes called a stock dividend, capitalisation issue, or scrip issue) is when a company issues additional shares to existing shareholders for free, in proportion to their current holdings. The new shares are funded out of the company's **reserves** (retained earnings or capital reserves), not by raising new money from the market.

A common ratio is **1 for 5** ("**1R5**" in some announcements), meaning that for every 5 shares you currently hold, you receive 1 additional share. So if you owned 1,000 shares before, you now own 1,200.

The economic reality is that the company has converted some of its retained reserves into share capital. The company's net worth has not changed. The number of slices the pie is cut into has gone up.

## What changes in your portfolio (and what does not)

Here is the part that confuses many beginners. You receive new shares, but the **value of your holding does not change** at the moment of the bonus issue.

Consider a worked example.

Before the bonus issue:

- You own **1,000 shares** at a market price of **PKR 60 each**.
- Your holding is worth **PKR 60,000**.
- The company has 100 million shares outstanding; market cap is PKR 6 billion.

The company announces a 1-for-5 bonus issue. After the bonus issue:

- You own **1,200 shares**.
- The market price adjusts down to approximately **PKR 50 each** (since the same total value is now spread across more shares).
- Your holding is still worth **PKR 60,000**.
- The company has 120 million shares outstanding; market cap is still PKR 6 billion.

The price-adjustment formula is approximately:

```
New price = Old price × Old shares / New shares
PKR 60 × 100 / 120 = PKR 50
```

Nothing has been created. You did not get richer. You got more slices of the same pie at a smaller price each.

So why does this matter at all?

## Why companies issue bonus shares

A few reasons, none of them as simple as "rewarding shareholders".

### 1. Lower the per-share price for liquidity

Pakistani retail investors often prefer lower per-share prices. A stock at PKR 1,000 per share feels expensive even though one share at PKR 1,000 and ten shares at PKR 100 are economically identical. Bonus issues bring the price down without changing economics, which can improve trading volume and the number of retail participants.

### 2. Conserve cash

A cash dividend takes money out of the company. A bonus issue does not. If the company wants to "reward" shareholders symbolically while keeping cash inside the business for reinvestment or to weather a downturn, a bonus issue accomplishes the symbolism without the cash outflow.

### 3. Signal confidence

Issuing bonus shares converts reserves into share capital, which is harder to undo than a one-time cash dividend. Some investors read this as a signal that management is confident enough about future earnings that they are willing to permanently raise share capital.

### 4. Maintain the share-capital growth narrative

For some companies, a regular pattern of bonus issues is a way of growing reported share capital over time, which can be relevant for credit analysis, regulatory ratios in regulated industries, or investor-relations narratives.

## Bonus issue vs cash dividend

In substance, a bonus issue and a cash dividend are very different.

| Feature | Cash dividend | Bonus issue |
|---|---|---|
| Cash leaves the company | Yes | No |
| Shareholder receives cash | Yes | No |
| Total share count changes | No | Yes (increases) |
| Share price typically drops | Yes (by approximately the dividend) | Yes (proportionally to new share count) |
| Tax mechanism | Withholding tax on dividend | Withholding tax on bonus issuance |
| Mental model | The company gives you a slice of profit | The company recaps reserves into share capital |

If a company has cash and wants to return it, a cash dividend is the cleaner instrument. If the company wants to keep the cash but still issue something to shareholders, a bonus issue is the tool.

## Tax treatment

Pakistan has historically taxed bonus issues. Under [FBR section 236Z](https://download1.fbr.gov.pk/Docs/20258181281745641WHT-RateCard.pdf) and predecessor sections, bonus shares issued by a company are treated as a deemed distribution and **withholding tax is collected at the time of issuance**, with separate rates for ATL (active filer), late filer, and non-ATL shareholders.

Recent rate cards published by Pakistani tax practitioners describe the bonus-share withholding mechanism with **filer rates around 10 percent and non-filer rates roughly double**, but the exact percentages have changed several times in recent Finance Acts. Some cards also reference exemptions or special treatment for specific categories such as Roshan Digital Account holders.

In practice, the company deducts the tax at source. The shareholder sees the post-tax number of shares deposited in their CDC sub-account. There is nothing for the retail investor to file separately for the bonus itself; it appears in the broker's annual tax statement.

<Callout type="warning" title="Verify the current rate">
  Bonus-share tax treatment has been one of the most-amended areas in
  recent Finance Acts. Always verify the current section, rate, and
  applicability from the most recent FBR rate card or with a Pakistani
  tax practitioner before assuming a specific figure.
</Callout>

## What about the share price drop?

Right after the bonus issue takes effect, the share price drops in proportion to the new share count. Many retail investors check their portfolio that morning, see the lower per-share price, and panic. The portfolio total is the same; only the per-share price has changed. Read the corporate-action notification before assuming a stock has dropped on bad news.

The pages on [Stock Splits](/docs/corporate-actions/stock-splits) and [Why Share Prices Fluctuate](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/why-share-prices-fluctuate) cover related mechanics that can also produce sudden price drops without underlying bad news.

## Key takeaways

- A bonus issue gives existing shareholders additional shares for free, funded from reserves, in proportion to existing holdings.
- The total value of your holding does not change at issuance. You own more shares at a lower per-share price.
- Companies use bonus issues to lower per-share price, conserve cash, signal confidence, or grow reported share capital.
- A cash dividend takes money out of the company; a bonus issue does not.
- Withholding tax under FBR section 236Z applies at issuance, with filer / non-filer rate differentials.
- A sudden lower share price right after a bonus issue is mechanical, not a sign that something is wrong with the company.
