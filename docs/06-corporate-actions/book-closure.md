# Book Closure

Every Pakistani corporate action that depends on knowing exactly *who owns what* has a **book closure** at its core. When a company is about to pay a dividend, issue bonus shares, run a rights subscription, or convene an AGM, it has to freeze its share-transfer register for a few days so that the entitlement list is unambiguous. Book closure is that freeze. Understanding it makes the difference between qualifying for a dividend and missing it.

- What book closure is and why companies need it

## What book closure is

Pakistani companies maintain a **share-transfer register** showing who owns the shares at any given time. This register changes constantly because PSX is matching trades during every market session. CDC updates the register on settlement (T+1).

When the company needs to do something that depends on knowing the owner list at a specific instant (paying a dividend, allocating bonus shares, mailing AGM notices), it instructs CDC to **freeze** the register for a defined window. During that freeze:

- No share transfers are recorded against the company.
- The list of shareholders entitled to the corporate action is determined and locked.
- The company can compute the exact dividend or bonus or AGM-vote allocation per shareholder.

This freeze period is the **book closure**. Outside the book closure window, normal share transfers continue as usual.

## The 14-day notice rule

According to [PSX's Correspondence Manual for Listed Companies](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/documents/legal-framework/PSX/guidelines-criterion-and-notices/procedures/CorspndceManual.pdf), companies must give a minimum of **14 days notice to PSX prior to closure of share transfer books for any purpose**. Intimation of dividends and other entitlements must reach PSX **not later than 14 days before** the commencement of book closure.

This notice period gives the market time to communicate the upcoming corporate action, lets investors decide whether to buy or sell before the cut-off, and lets brokers update their systems.

## Record date vs book closure

Two terms get used interchangeably but mean slightly different things.

- The **record date** is a specific *day* on which the entitlement list is captured. Whoever appears as a shareholder on this single day is entitled to the dividend, bonus, or other action.
- The **book closure** is a multi-day *window* during which the share-transfer register is frozen. The register typically opens at the start of one date (often the day after the record date) and reopens on a later date.

Most modern PSX announcements make this clean by stating both:

```
Dividend: Rs. X per share
Record date: Friday, [date]
Book closure: Saturday, [date+1] to Friday, [date+7]
Payment date: [date+30 to 45]
```

In some announcements, only the book-closure window is given. In that case, the record date is conventionally the **last business day before book closure begins**.

## A complete worked timeline

Tying together the pages on dividends, T+1 settlement, and book closure, here is a full timeline for a typical dividend.

Suppose ABC Ltd announces, on **Wednesday, 1 October**, an interim dividend with the following dates:

- **Record date:** Friday, 31 October
- **Book closure:** Saturday 1 November to Friday 7 November
- **Payment date:** Friday 28 November

Let's walk through what happens day by day for an investor.

| Date | Event | What it means for you |
|---|---|---|
| Wednesday, 1 October | **Declaration date.** | Dividend announced. PSX is notified. |
| 1 October to 29 October | Trading window. | You can buy and qualify if the trade settles by the record date. |
| Wednesday, 29 October | **Last day to buy and qualify.** | Trade matched today; T+1 settles on Thursday 30 October; you are on the books before Friday's record date. |
| Thursday, 30 October | **Ex-dividend date.** | Buying today does not qualify; trade would settle on Friday 31 October but the daily process locks the register late in the day, by which point the cut-off has passed. |
| Friday, 31 October | **Record date.** | Whoever is on the register today receives the dividend. Trades after this date are for cum-dividend-stripped shares. |
| Saturday, 1 November to Friday, 7 November | **Book closure window.** | No transfers are recorded against the company. New trades execute on PSX as normal but the resulting register changes apply only after book closure ends. |
| Saturday, 8 November | **Books reopen.** | Normal transfer recording resumes. |
| Friday, 28 November | **Payment date.** | Dividend hits your bank account. |

The exact day-of-week alignment varies by company. The structural point is that there are two cut-offs to track: the **last day to buy** (under T+1, this is two business days before the record date if the record date is on a normal weekday, or earlier if the record date is on a Monday and a weekend intervenes) and the **payment date**.

## What you can and cannot do during book closure

Common questions during book closure.

### Can I still buy and sell during book closure?

**Yes.** PSX trading continues normally during book closure. The market does not stop. What is frozen is the *register update at the company level*. Your trades during book closure will be recorded once books reopen.

### If I sell during book closure, do I lose the upcoming dividend?

**No.** The dividend goes to whoever was on the register on the record date. If you sold after the record date but during book closure, you have already qualified, and the dividend will still be paid to you on the payment date. The buyer who picked up the share during book closure does not get this dividend; their share is "cum-dividend-stripped" (sometimes called "ex-dividend" in informal usage).

### If I buy during book closure, do I get the dividend?

**No.** Trades during book closure cannot land you on the register before the record date (which has already passed). The seller has already qualified for the dividend; you have not. Your share will participate in the *next* corporate action, not the one currently being settled.

### Can the company pay early?

In rare cases yes, but the standard practice is to pay on the announced payment date. Always rely on the announcement, not on rumours of early payment.

### Why is book closure usually about 7 days?

There is no fixed legal duration; the typical 7-day window is convention, balancing operational time the company needs against the disruption a longer freeze would cause. Some companies use 5 days; some use 10. The minimum 14-day pre-notice rule is what's regulated, not the duration of the closure itself.

<Callout type="tip" title="Calendar your dividend dates">
  If you are a long-term holder of dividend-paying stocks, add the
  record date and payment date to your calendar when each
  announcement comes out. Two reminders, one for the buy / sell
  cut-off and one for the expected cash credit, save you from
  surprises and missed entitlements.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Book closure is the period during which a company's share-transfer register is frozen so it can lock the entitlement list for a corporate action.
- Companies must notify PSX at least 14 days before book closure begins.
- The record date is the single day when entitlement is captured; book closure is the multi-day window after the record date during which transfers are not recorded.
- PSX trading continues during book closure. The freeze is at the register level, not the trading level.
- If you owned the share on the record date, you receive the dividend or bonus regardless of whether you sell during book closure.
- Calendaring record dates and payment dates avoids missed entitlements.
