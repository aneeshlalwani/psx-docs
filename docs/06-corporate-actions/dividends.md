# Dividends

When a Pakistani company has a profitable year and decides to share some of that profit with the people who own its shares, the mechanism is called a **dividend**. For long-term investors, dividends are not a side benefit. They are a major part of the total return on PSX, and they have a few Pakistan-specific features (face-value percentages, the four-date cycle, the filer vs non-filer split) that you should understand before you buy your first dividend-paying stock.

## What you'll learn

- What a dividend is and where the money comes from
- The difference between interim and final dividends
- The four key dates: declaration, ex-dividend, record, and payment
- The percent-of-face-value convention used in Pakistan
- Withholding tax treatment for filers and non-filers
- Why "buy the dividend, sell the next day" is rarely as profitable as it sounds

## What a dividend is

After a company's financial year (or quarter) closes, the board decides how much of the after-tax profit to pay out to shareholders versus how much to retain inside the business for reinvestment. The portion paid out is the **dividend**. It is paid per share, so if you own 1,000 shares of a company that pays a PKR 5 dividend, you receive PKR 5,000 (before tax) regardless of how many other shareholders there are.

Companies are not legally required to pay dividends. Many profitable companies, in Pakistan and globally, pay none and reinvest the entire profit instead. The argument is that if reinvestment can grow the business faster than the dividend would compound in the shareholder's hands, no dividend creates more value. Different investors prefer different policies; neither is universally correct.

## Interim vs final dividends

Pakistani companies typically declare dividends at one of two kinds of meetings.

- **Interim dividends** are declared by the board at quarterly review meetings (after Q1, Q2, or Q3). A company can announce one and, in rare cases, cancel it later.
- **Final dividends** are declared at the **Annual General Meeting (AGM)** after the full-year results. Once a final dividend is declared at the AGM, the company is legally bound to pay; it cannot be cancelled.

Many Pakistani companies follow either a **January to December** calendar year or a **July to June** fiscal year (mirroring the federal budget cycle). Both are common.

## The four key dates

To actually receive a dividend, you have to be on the company's share register on a specific cut-off day. Four dates matter.

### 1. Declaration date

The day the company's board announces the dividend. This is when you first see it on the news ticker or PSX's announcements page. The announcement names the dividend amount, the record date, and the book-closure window.

### 2. Record date

The date on which you must be a registered shareholder in the company's books to qualify. The company pays the dividend only to whoever is recorded as the owner on this specific date.

### 3. Ex-dividend date

Pakistan settles trades on **T+1** since [9 February 2026](https://www.nccpl.com.pk/transition-to-t1-settlement-cycle), so the ex-dividend date is **one business day before the record date**. The ex-dividend date is the first day on which buying the share *no longer* qualifies you for the dividend. The seller on the ex-dividend day keeps the dividend; the buyer does not get it.

If you want the dividend, you must buy **on or before the day before the ex-dividend date**. After that, the trade settles too late to land your name on the books before the record date.

### 4. Payment date

The day the company actually credits the dividend to shareholders' bank accounts. Most Pakistani companies pay 30 to 45 days after the record date; the announcement specifies the exact date.

### A worked timeline

Suppose company ABC Ltd holds a board meeting on **1 October** and announces an interim dividend with a **record date of Monday, 3 November**. Under T+1 settlement, the cycle looks like this:

| Date | What happens |
|---|---|
| 1 October | **Declaration date.** Dividend announced. |
| Up to last business day before ex-dividend | Buying still qualifies. Trades settle on T+1, so the share lands in your CDC account in time. |
| Friday, 31 October | **Ex-dividend date** (one business day before record date, accounting for weekend). Buying today does not qualify; trade would settle on Monday 3 November. |
| Monday, 3 November | **Record date.** Whoever is on the books today gets the dividend. |
| 4 November onwards | **Book closure** begins (covered on a separate page). |
| Around mid-November to mid-December | **Payment date.** Dividend hits your bank account. |

If the record date falls on a Monday and a Friday-bought trade only settles on the Monday itself, you cannot rely on Friday to qualify. Buying on the **Thursday before** is the safe latest-buy day.

## The percent-of-face-value convention

Here is a Pakistan-specific feature that confuses many first-time investors.

In Pakistan, **dividends are announced as a percentage of face value, not market price**. Face value is the original par value of the share at issuance, almost always PKR 10 (or, after recent stock splits, PKR 5 or PKR 2 or PKR 1).

So if Toyota Indus Motor announces a "300% dividend" and the face value is PKR 10, the cash you actually receive is **PKR 30 per share**, not 300% of the current market price of around PKR 1,200 to PKR 1,300. A "300% dividend" sounds enormous until you do the face-value arithmetic.

The way to think about percentage announcements:

```
Cash dividend per share = Face value × Announced percentage
```

A 100% dividend on a PKR 10 face value is PKR 10 per share. A 50% dividend is PKR 5 per share. A 1000% dividend is PKR 100 per share.

The percentages can look impressive but they translate to single-digit or low-double-digit yields when measured against the share's actual market price.

## Withholding tax on dividends

Dividend tax in Pakistan is **deducted at source** by the company before the money reaches your bank account. The rate depends on whether you are on the **Active Taxpayer List (ATL)** maintained by the FBR.

According to the [FBR withholding tax rate card for tax year 2026](https://download1.fbr.gov.pk/Docs/20258181281745641WHT-RateCard.pdf) and tax-card summaries published by Pakistani tax practitioners, dividend withholding rates have moved upward in recent years. Most retail investors should expect:

- **Active filer (ATL):** roughly 15 percent for typical PSX equity dividends, with higher rates applying to certain categories (such as dividends from companies whose income is exempt) and lower rates for specific structures (such as power companies with subsidised rates and Roshan Digital Account holders).
- **Non-filer / not on ATL:** typically double the filer rate, often 30 percent on equity dividends. Recent finance acts have also introduced a **late filer** intermediate category with rates between filer and non-filer.

Mutual fund dividend distributions are taxed differently from direct equity dividends. The page on [Filer vs Non-Filer](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/filer-vs-non-filer) covers the broader implications of ATL status and why getting on it is one of the highest-return decisions a retail investor can make.

## Dividend yield

A useful comparable across companies is the **dividend yield**, the annual cash dividend per share divided by the current market price.

```
Dividend yield = Annual dividend per share / Current market price
```

A PKR 6 annual dividend on a PKR 100 share is a 6 percent yield. A 6 percent dividend yield in Pakistan is typical for a profitable, dividend-paying company at a reasonable price. Yields above 10 percent often signal either a recent price drop, a high-payout policy that may not be sustainable, or both. Always check sustainability before chasing high yields.

## The "buy the dividend" trap

A common beginner question: can you buy a stock just before the ex-dividend date, collect the dividend, and sell shortly after for a quick profit?

In theory, this would always pay. In practice, it almost never does, for two reasons:

1. **The price typically drops by approximately the dividend amount on the ex-dividend day.** A PKR 100 share that announces a PKR 5 dividend often opens around PKR 95 on the ex-dividend day, because new buyers are no longer paying for the dividend and existing holders are happy to sell at a price that reflects that. You collect PKR 5, your share is worth PKR 5 less.
2. **Brokerage and tax eat into the small spread.** After paying brokerage on both legs, the withholding tax on the dividend, and any capital-gains-tax implications, the strategy is roughly break-even on a good day and net-negative on a bad one.

Long-term holders benefit from dividends. Short-term traders rarely do.

## Reinvesting dividends

The most powerful use of dividends is reinvestment. If a stock pays a 6 percent dividend and you take the cash and buy more of the same stock (or another one in your portfolio), you are compounding your position. The page on [Power of Compounding](/docs/personal-finance/power-of-compounding) shows how that compounding effect grows over decades. Warren Buffett's famous "snowball" metaphor is essentially a description of dividend reinvestment.

Pakistani brokers do not always have automated dividend-reinvestment plans (DRIPs) the way US brokers do, but you can do the same thing manually by buying more shares the next time the dividend hits your account.

## Key takeaways

- A dividend is a portion of after-tax profit paid per share to shareholders. Companies are not required to pay dividends.
- Dividends are either interim (declared at quarterly meetings, cancellable) or final (declared at the AGM, binding).
- Four dates matter: declaration, ex-dividend, record, and payment. Under T+1 settlement, the ex-dividend date is one business day before the record date.
- Pakistan announces dividends as a percentage of face value, not market price. A "300% dividend" on a PKR 10 face value is PKR 30 per share.
- Withholding tax on dividends is deducted at source by the company. Filer vs non-filer status often doubles the rate.
- "Buying the dividend" rarely produces a profit because the share price typically drops by the dividend amount on the ex-dividend day.
- Reinvesting dividends is where the long-term wealth-building actually happens.
