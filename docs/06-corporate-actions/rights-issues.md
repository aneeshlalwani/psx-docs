# Rights Issues

A **rights issue** is the third major way (after IPOs and bonus issues) that a Pakistani company can change its share count. Unlike a bonus issue, a rights issue is **not free**. It is an offer to existing shareholders to buy *more* shares at a discounted price, in proportion to their current holding. Many retail investors confuse rights issues with bonus issues because both go to existing shareholders, but the two have very different cash implications. This page walks through the mechanics so the distinction is clear.

## What a rights issue is

A **rights issue** is when a company offers existing shareholders the **right** to buy additional shares directly from the company at a fixed price, usually below the current market price, in proportion to their current holding.

Unlike an IPO, the offer is not open to the general public. Only people who already own the share on a designated record date can subscribe.

The offer is governed by the [Companies (Further Issue of Shares) Regulations, 2020](https://www.secp.gov.pk/media-center/press-releases/secp-notifies-regulations-regarding-further-issue-of-shares/) issued by SECP, which specify minimum disclosure requirements, eligibility conditions, and the process for unsubscribed shares.

A rights issue announcement specifies:

1. **The ratio** (e.g. "1 for 4", meaning 1 right share offered for every 4 currently held).
2. **The subscription price** (e.g. PKR 25 per share, which might be a discount versus the current PKR 60 market price).
3. **The record date** (you must own the share on this date to receive the rights).
4. **The subscription period** (the window during which you can pay and accept the rights).
5. **Use of proceeds** (what the company plans to do with the new money raised).

## Why companies do rights issues

The purpose is **to raise new capital**. Unlike a bonus issue (which converts reserves) or a stock split (which changes face value), a rights issue actually pulls fresh cash into the company.

Common reasons:

- **Fund expansion or new investment**. The company wants to build a new factory, acquire a competitor, or enter a new market, and needs cash that is not currently available.
- **Reduce debt**. The company wants to deleverage by replacing debt with equity capital.
- **Strengthen the balance sheet**. Regulated industries (banks, insurance) sometimes need to raise capital to meet ratio requirements.
- **Fund a working-capital crunch**. Less commonly, a struggling company turns to a rights issue when banks are unwilling to extend credit.

The use of proceeds matters. A rights issue funding a high-return expansion is very different from a rights issue funding survival.

## How a rights ratio works

If a company announces a 1-for-4 rights issue at a subscription price of PKR 25, and you own 1,000 shares, you have the right to subscribe to **250 additional shares at PKR 25 each**, which is PKR 6,250 of new investment in the company.

After full subscription, you own:

- 1,250 shares (the original 1,000 plus 250 new ones).
- You have spent PKR 6,250 in new cash.

The new shares carry the same dividend, voting, and other rights as the existing shares (subject to recent SECP rules on shares with differential rights, [described below](#secp-regulatory-context)).

## Your three options

When a rights issue is announced and you are eligible, you have three choices.

### Option 1: Subscribe in full

Pay the subscription price and take all the rights shares offered to you. Your ownership percentage in the company is preserved (no dilution), and you own more shares.

Use this option when:

- You believe the company's use of proceeds is value-creating.
- The subscription price is meaningfully below your estimate of fair value.
- You have the cash available without disrupting other plans.

### Option 2: Renounce the rights

Pakistani rights issues sometimes allow you to **transfer your rights** to someone else (the technical term is "renouncing" them). The renouncee pays you for the rights and then exercises them with the company. The economic logic is that if the subscription price is below market, the rights themselves have positive value, and you can monetise that value without putting up the full new investment.

Whether renunciation is allowed and how it works depends on the specific rights issue announcement. Read the rights letter carefully.

### Option 3: Let the rights lapse

Do nothing. The rights expire at the end of the subscription period.

This is the most common choice for retail investors who either do not understand the announcement, do not have the cash, or have lost interest in the stock. It is also usually the worst option, because:

- You are economically diluted: your ownership percentage drops while other shareholders increase theirs.
- You did not capture any value from the rights, even though they likely had positive value in the market.

If you cannot subscribe and renunciation is allowed, renounce. Letting rights lapse is rarely the best move.

## The price-adjustment effect

After the ex-rights date, the share price drops to reflect the dilution effect of the new shares. The theoretical post-rights price is approximately:

```
Theoretical ex-rights price = (Market cap before + Cash raised) / New share count
```

Worked example. A share trades at PKR 60. The company has 100 million shares outstanding (market cap PKR 6 billion). It announces a 1-for-4 rights issue at PKR 25. After full subscription, 25 million new shares are issued, raising PKR 625 million.

```
Theoretical post-rights price = (6,000 million + 625 million) / 125 million
                              = 6,625 / 125
                              = PKR 53
```

The share opens around PKR 53 on the ex-rights date, not PKR 60. A shareholder who **subscribed in full** has the same total value (more shares at a lower price plus the cash spent). A shareholder who **let rights lapse** sees their per-share price drop without compensation; they have been diluted.

## SECP regulatory context

[SECP recently amended the Companies (Further Issue of Shares) Regulations, 2020](https://www.dawn.com/news/1967970/regulations-amended-for-rights-shares) to facilitate capital raising while maintaining investor protection. Key updates:

- Companies can announce rights issues even when promoters or directors have overdue amounts in CIB reports, **provided** the relevant persons obtain a No Objection Certificate from the financial institution and disclose the situation in the rights offer document.
- [SECP also reinforced the "one share, one vote" principle](https://pakobserver.net/secp-amends-regulations-on-shares-with-differential-rights-privileges/), ensuring that ordinary shares retain at least 75 percent of total voting power even when companies issue shares with differential rights.

These regulations are aimed at making rights issues more accessible while keeping minority-shareholder protections intact. As a retail investor, the practical implication is that you should always read the rights offer document for the use of proceeds, the subscription price relative to market, and any disclosed defaults or restructuring.

## Red flags

Walk away from (or vote against) a rights issue if you see any of these.

- **Subscription price at or above current market price.** Defeats the entire point of a rights issue. No rational shareholder will subscribe; the announcement is usually a sign of a struggling company.
- **Vague use of proceeds.** "General corporate purposes" is not a use of proceeds. The offer document should name the specific projects, debt to be repaid, or capital ratio to be hit.
- **Repeated rights issues year after year.** A pattern of repeated rights issues without commensurate growth in earnings is a sign that the company is funding losses with shareholder cash.
- **Sudden announcement when the share price has crashed.** A struggling company turning to shareholders to fund a survival round is a different proposition than a profitable company funding expansion.

## Key takeaways

- A rights issue offers existing shareholders the right to buy more shares at a discount, in proportion to current holdings.
- Unlike a bonus issue, a rights issue raises real cash for the company.
- You have three options: subscribe in full, renounce the rights, or let them lapse. Letting them lapse is rarely the best option.
- The share price drops to a theoretical ex-rights price after the offer; subscribers stay whole, lapsers are diluted.
- SECP has amended the rights-issue regulations recently to facilitate capital raising while maintaining minority-shareholder protections.
- Read the use of proceeds and subscription price carefully before subscribing. Repeated rights issues without growth are a red flag.
