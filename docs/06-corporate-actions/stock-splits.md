# Stock Splits

In the past few years, **stock splits** have become noticeably more common on PSX. Big names including Lucky Cement, Systems Limited, AirLink, Arif Habib Corporation, and National Foods have all reduced their face value to bring the per-share market price down to more accessible levels. If you own one of these stocks, your account suddenly shows more shares at a lower price. This page explains what is actually happening and why it matters (or does not).

## What you'll learn

- What a stock split is in Pakistani terms (a face-value reduction)
- Real PSX stock-split examples from the last two years
- Why companies choose to split
- The difference between a stock split and a bonus issue
- What happens to your holding during the split

## What a stock split is

A **stock split** on PSX is the corporate action of **reducing a share's face value** and issuing additional shares to existing holders so that the total value of their holding stays the same.

In Pakistan, the **standard face value** of a listed share is **PKR 10**. Most splits reduce it to PKR 5, PKR 2, or PKR 1. The ratio describes how many new shares replace each old share.

- **2 for 1 split:** face value goes from PKR 10 to PKR 5. You receive 2 new shares for every old one.
- **5 for 1 split:** face value goes from PKR 10 to PKR 2. You receive 5 new shares for every old one.
- **10 for 1 split:** face value goes from PKR 10 to PKR 1. You receive 10 new shares for every old one.

Per [PSX's Guidelines on Stock Split for Listed Companies](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/uploads/Guidelines_on_Stock_Split.pdf), a split increases the number of outstanding shares by reducing the face value, while keeping market capitalisation unchanged.

## Recent PSX examples

A few real-world splits in the last two years.

| Company | Ratio | Old face value | New face value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arif Habib Corporation (AHCL) | 10 for 1 | PKR 10 | PKR 1 |
| Systems Limited (SYS) | 5 for 1 | PKR 10 | PKR 2 |
| Lucky Cement (LUCK) | 5 for 1 | PKR 10 | PKR 2 |
| AirLink Communication (AIRLINK) | 5 for 1 | PKR 10 | PKR 2 |
| National Foods (NATF) | 2 for 1 | PKR 10 | PKR 5 |

In each case, trading was [briefly suspended on PSX while the split was processed in the central depository](https://propakistani.pk/2025/05/23/systems-limited-to-suspend-trading-from-may-29-to-implement-stock-split/), typically for one to a few business days, before the new face value and share count became effective.

## What happens to your holding

A worked example using a 5-for-1 split.

Before the split:

- You own **100 shares** at a market price of **PKR 1,000 each**, face value **PKR 10**.
- Your holding is worth **PKR 100,000**.

After the 5-for-1 split:

- You own **500 shares** at a market price of **approximately PKR 200 each**, face value **PKR 2**.
- Your holding is worth **PKR 100,000**.

The price-adjustment formula:

```
New price = Old price × Old face value / New face value
PKR 1,000 × 10 / 2 = PKR 200
```

Nothing economic has changed. You did not get richer. The market cap is the same, the company is the same, your ownership percentage is the same. You just have more, smaller slices.

## Why companies split

The most-cited reasons.

### 1. Per-share price accessibility

A high per-share price (PKR 1,000 plus) feels expensive to many Pakistani retail investors, even though that is psychological rather than economic. Splitting brings the price down to a range where retail investors are willing to buy meaningful quantities. Brokers also report that retail order sizes are sometimes more meaningful in shares-bought terms when the per-share price is lower.

### 2. Liquidity

Lower per-share prices typically come with higher daily trading volumes, because a wider pool of retail investors can take economically meaningful positions. Higher liquidity means tighter bid-ask spreads, which is a real benefit even though the per-share price is lower.

### 3. Index inclusion and weighting effects

PSX indices like the [KSE-100](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/psx-indices) use **free-float methodology**. While a split itself does not change the free-float number of shares (just the per-share price), the resulting liquidity changes can affect index weightings over time.

### 4. Reaching retail-friendly price levels

Some companies explicitly state that they are splitting to make their share more accessible to retail investors. The argument is that wider retail ownership creates a more stable, long-term shareholder base, which reduces volatility. The empirical evidence on this is mixed but the rationale is widely accepted in Pakistani finance commentary.

## Stock split vs bonus issue

Beginners often confuse these two corporate actions. The mechanics overlap (both increase your share count and reduce per-share price) but they are economically distinct.

| Feature | Stock split | Bonus issue |
|---|---|---|
| Face value of share | **Changes** (reduced) | **Stays the same** (usually PKR 10) |
| Source of new shares | Face-value reduction | Reserves capitalised into share capital |
| Total share capital on the balance sheet | **Unchanged** | **Increases** |
| Reserves on the balance sheet | Unchanged | **Decreases** |
| Tax treatment for shareholder | No withholding tax on the split itself | Withholding tax applies on the bonus shares |
| Typical ratio | 2 for 1, 5 for 1, 10 for 1 | 1 for 5, 1 for 10, 2 for 5 |

The simplest way to remember: a **split slices the existing pie into smaller pieces**. A **bonus issue capitalises some of the bakery's reserves into new pieces** of the same-sized slice.

For tax-aware investors, the distinction matters because bonus issues attract withholding tax under FBR section 236Z whereas a pure stock split does not.

## What you actually do during a split

Almost nothing.

1. The company announces the split, naming the effective date.
2. PSX briefly suspends trading in the share (typically one to a few business days) while CDC updates records.
3. When trading resumes, your CDC sub-account shows the new share count at the reduced face value, and the market opens the share at the adjusted price.
4. You continue trading as normal.

You do not need to take any action, fill in any forms, or notify your broker. The mechanics are handled centrally between the company, PSX, and CDC.

<Callout type="tip" title="Check your cost basis after a split">
  Some broker statements reset the per-share cost basis to the new
  face value automatically; some leave it unadjusted. If you are
  tracking your cost basis manually for capital-gains-tax reasons,
  recompute it after every split: divide your old per-share cost by
  the split ratio.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- A stock split on PSX reduces the face value of a share and issues additional shares to existing holders in proportion.
- Total value of your holding does not change. Market cap is unchanged. Only the per-share price drops proportionally.
- Recent PSX examples include AHCL (10 for 1), SYS / LUCK / AIRLINK (5 for 1), and NATF (2 for 1).
- Companies split to make per-share prices more retail-accessible, increase liquidity, and improve index participation.
- A split is mechanically different from a bonus issue: a split changes face value, a bonus capitalises reserves. Tax treatment is also different.
- You do not need to take any action during a split. CDC updates your account automatically.
