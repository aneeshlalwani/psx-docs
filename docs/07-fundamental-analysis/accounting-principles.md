# Accounting Principles

If two friends each ran a small shop and one tracked profit when cash arrived while the other tracked profit when goods were delivered, you could not meaningfully compare their results. The whole point of accounting principles is to ensure that every PSX-listed company applies the same rules so that an investor can compare two cement companies, two banks, or two textile mills on a like-for-like basis. This page introduces the four core principles that come up again and again when you read Pakistani financial statements.

## What you'll learn

- Why accounting principles exist
- The going concern assumption
- The accrual basis (and why it differs from cash flow)
- Historical cost
- Materiality
- The IFRS / IAS framework that governs Pakistani financial reporting

## Why accounting principles exist

Financial statements are useful only if they can be **compared and trusted**. Comparison requires consistency: every company has to record similar transactions in similar ways. Trust requires verifiability: the rules must be specific enough that an auditor can check them.

Pakistan follows **International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)** and the older **International Accounting Standards (IAS)**, with some local modifications adopted by the Institute of Chartered Accountants of Pakistan (ICAP) and SECP. These standards are largely consistent with what is used in major global markets, which makes Pakistani financials readable to international investors and vice versa.

Within those standards, four foundational principles come up everywhere.

## 1. Going concern

The **going concern** principle assumes that the company will continue to operate indefinitely into the future, not be wound up or sold off in pieces in the near term.

Why this matters:

- It allows the company to value long-term assets (factories, machinery, buildings) at their **useful operating value** rather than at their **liquidation value**. A factory that is genuinely worth PKR 200 crore in operation might fetch only PKR 50 crore in a fire sale; the going-concern assumption means it stays on the books closer to its operating value.
- It allows expenses to be matched to revenue across multiple periods. A new machine that will be used for ten years can be depreciated over ten years rather than expensed immediately.
- It allows long-term contracts and obligations to be recognised in the periods when they actually take effect.

When the going concern assumption is **threatened**, the auditor will say so. A "going concern qualification" or "material uncertainty regarding going concern" in the [Independent Auditor Report](/docs/fundamental-analysis/auditor-report) is a serious signal: the auditor is not confident the company will survive the next 12 months. Most retail investors should walk away from any Pakistani company carrying a going-concern qualification unless they have a very specific thesis for why the business will recover.

## 2. Accrual basis

The **accrual basis** records revenues and expenses **when they are earned or incurred**, not when cash actually changes hands.

A worked example. Suppose a Pakistani cement company sells PKR 1 crore worth of cement in March: PKR 60 lakh paid in cash, PKR 40 lakh on credit due in June.

- On the **accrual basis**, the income statement for March shows **PKR 1 crore in revenue**. The full sale was earned in March, regardless of when cash arrives.
- On the **cash basis**, the income statement for March would show only **PKR 60 lakh in revenue**. The credit portion would not appear until June when cash actually arrives.

Why accrual is the standard:

- It matches **revenue with the costs that produced it**. The cement was made in March, the sale happened in March, and the profit should be recognised in March.
- It gives a **truer picture of business performance** within a period.
- It is **harder to game** through timing of cash receipts and payments.

The trade-off is that accrual-basis revenue is not the same as cash. A company can be profitable on the income statement while running out of cash. This is exactly why the [Cash Flow Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement) exists. It restates the same business activity on a cash basis, so you can see whether the income-statement profit is actually translating into cash that can be used to pay dividends, repay debt, or fund future investment.

The page on the [Income Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/income-statement) and the page on the [Cash Flow Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement) together cover the practical implications.

## 3. Historical cost

The **historical cost** principle requires assets to be recorded on the balance sheet at the **price the company actually paid** when it bought them, not at current market value.

A worked example. Suppose a textile company bought a piece of land in Karachi in 1995 for PKR 50 lakh. The land is now worth maybe PKR 50 crore. On the balance sheet, the land typically appears at PKR 50 lakh, not PKR 50 crore.

Why this seems strange but is generally a good idea:

- **Objectivity**. The PKR 50 lakh figure has supporting documentation (the original purchase deed). The PKR 50 crore current value is an estimate that could be inflated.
- **Verifiability**. An auditor can check the original purchase price unambiguously. Current market value is a moving target.
- **Conservatism**. Recording at historical cost prevents companies from inflating their balance sheets through speculative valuations.

Caveats:

- For some asset classes, IFRS allows or requires **fair value accounting** (revaluation to current market value). Land and buildings can be revalued under specific options; financial assets are often marked to market.
- This is why the **note disclosures** matter so much. The notes will tell you whether a particular line is at historical cost or fair value, and the date of any revaluation.
- For asset-heavy Pakistani companies (real estate, banks with property portfolios), the gap between historical cost and current market value can be enormous. The book value of equity can dramatically understate the true economic value.

The pages on [Non-Current Assets](/docs/fundamental-analysis/non-current-assets) and [Current Assets](/docs/fundamental-analysis/current-assets) cover specific asset categories and their valuation rules.

## 4. Materiality

The **materiality** principle says that financial statements should disclose every item that is **significant enough to influence an investor's decision**, but not every trivial detail.

A worked example. A large Pakistani company with PKR 500 crore in revenue does not need to separately list every PKR 5,000 stationery purchase. It can roll thousands of small expenses into a single "administrative expenses" line. But a single PKR 50 crore loss on a failed investment definitely needs its own disclosure, because that figure could move the share price.

Why this matters:

- It keeps financial statements **readable**. Without materiality, every annual report would be a million-line document of every transaction.
- It focuses attention on **what could move investment decisions**.
- The threshold for materiality is **company-specific**. PKR 1 crore is material for a small company and immaterial for a multi-billion-rupee one.

In practice, materiality is what justifies the level of detail in the **Notes to the Financial Statements**. If a line is large enough to matter, it gets a note; if it is too small to matter, it gets bundled with similar items.

## How these principles interact

The four principles are not independent. They reinforce each other:

- **Going concern** lets the company use accrual recognition meaningfully, because there is a future to match revenues against costs.
- **Accrual** with **historical cost** keeps both revenue recognition and asset valuation grounded in verifiable transactions.
- **Materiality** tunes the level of detail at which each of the others is applied.

When you read a Pakistani annual report, all four are working together implicitly. You usually do not see them named, but they shape every number on the page.

## When principles fall short

Two practical caveats.

**Earnings management.** Within the rules, companies have legal flexibility in how aggressively or conservatively they apply principles like accrual recognition. A company that wants to flatter near-term profit might recognise revenue earlier; one that wants to defer profit might recognise it later. The page on the [Auditor Report](/docs/fundamental-analysis/auditor-report) covers how Key Audit Matters often flag exactly this kind of timing judgement.

**Off-balance-sheet items.** Some legitimate economic exposures do not appear on the balance sheet directly: operating leases (sometimes), contingent liabilities, derivatives in some configurations. The notes to the financial statements should disclose these, but they are easy to miss on a quick read.

The structured way to handle both: read the notes, compare cash flow with income, and watch for sudden changes in accounting policy from year to year.

<Callout type="tip" title="Read the accounting-policy note">
  Every Pakistani annual report has a "Significant Accounting Policies"
  note near the top of the notes section. It declares which IFRS
  options the company has chosen for revenue recognition, depreciation
  methods, inventory valuation, and so on. Compare this note across
  two consecutive annual reports. Any change in policy is worth
  understanding before you trust the latest year's numbers.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Accounting principles exist so financial statements are comparable across companies and verifiable by auditors.
- Going concern assumes the company will continue operating; a going-concern qualification in the auditor report is serious.
- Accrual basis records transactions when earned or incurred, not when cash moves. The cash flow statement reconciles to actual cash.
- Historical cost records assets at their original purchase price; some assets are revalued to fair value under IFRS options.
- Materiality keeps statements readable by bundling small items and disclosing large ones.
- Pakistani financial statements follow IFRS / IAS as adopted by ICAP and SECP, with local modifications.
