# Reading the Annual Report

The single most important document published by any PSX-listed company is its **annual report**. Everything in this category, from balance sheet to ratios to management evaluation, ultimately depends on facts that come from this one document. Annual reports look intimidating the first time you open one (often 100 to 200 pages of dense financial statements, notes, and corporate-governance disclosures), but they have a predictable structure. Once you know what is where, navigating them becomes routine.

## What you'll learn

- Where to download Pakistani annual reports
- The standard sections of an annual report
- The five core financial statements
- Capital expenditure vs revenue expenditure
- Standalone vs consolidated statements
- Parent, subsidiary, and associate company terminology

## Where to find annual reports

Every PSX-listed company is required to publish its annual report. You can find them in several places.

- **[PSX Data Portal](https://dps.psx.com.pk/)** is the official source. Search for the company by name or symbol; scroll to the Financial Reports section to download annual and quarterly reports.
- **The company's own investor relations page**, usually linked from its corporate website.
- **Third-party data sites** like sarmaaya.pk, scstrade.com, and various broker research portals re-host these reports.

Always cross-check that the report is the most recent audited annual report (which usually ends with "Audited Financial Statements" in its file name), not an unaudited quarterly report.

## The standard sections

A typical Pakistani annual report has the following sections, in roughly this order.

1. **Cover page and Vision / Mission statements**
2. **Company information** (registered office, directors, auditors, banks)
3. **Notice of the Annual General Meeting**
4. **Chairman's review and CEO's report**
5. **Director's report** (most substantive narrative)
6. **Pattern of shareholding** (who owns the company)
7. **Statement of compliance with Code of Corporate Governance**
8. **Independent Auditor Report**
9. **Five financial statements** (covered below)
10. **Notes to the financial statements**
11. **Six-year financial summary** (or ten-year, depending on company)
12. **AGM proxy form**

For a deep-dive, you will read sections 4 to 11 carefully. Sections 1 to 3 are usually formal; sections 12 onwards are administrative.

## The five financial statements

By Pakistani regulation and IFRS-aligned practice, the financial-statements section contains five distinct statements:

1. **Statement of Financial Position** (the **Balance Sheet**) — what the company owns, owes, and has as net equity at year-end.
2. **Statement of Profit or Loss** (the **Income Statement**) — revenue and expenses over the year.
3. **Statement of Comprehensive Income** — net income plus other comprehensive items (currency translation, asset revaluation, etc.).
4. **Statement of Changes in Equity** — how shareholder equity moved during the year (issuances, dividends, retained earnings).
5. **Statement of Cash Flows** (the **Cash Flow Statement**) — actual cash in and out by activity type.

The dedicated pages on [Balance Sheet](/docs/fundamental-analysis/balance-sheet), [Income Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/income-statement), and [Cash Flow Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement) cover the three you will spend most time on.

After the five statements come the **Notes to the Financial Statements**, often 50 to 100 pages of detailed breakouts of individual line items. The notes are where the real specificity lives. A balance-sheet line "Stock-in-trade Rs. 1,623,000 thousand" might look opaque, but Note 9 (or whatever the relevant note number is) will break it into raw materials, work in process, finished goods, and spare parts, with prior-year comparisons.

If you skim past the notes, you are missing most of the useful information.

## Capital expenditure vs revenue expenditure

A distinction that comes up everywhere in annual-report reading.

**Revenue expenditure** is the cost of running the business day to day. It is consumed within the financial year. Examples for a petrol station: monthly fuel purchases, employee salaries, electricity bills, monthly rent. These hit the income statement in the year they happen.

**Capital expenditure (capex)** creates an asset that benefits the business for multiple years. Examples for the same petrol station: installing two new fuel pumps in 2026, building a car wash on adjacent space, expanding the office. These go onto the balance sheet as assets and are then depreciated over their useful lives, with depreciation hitting the income statement gradually.

Why this matters for analysis:

- A company with high capex relative to revenue is **investing in growth**.
- A company with low capex but high revenue is **harvesting** existing assets without reinvesting; this can flatter near-term profit but eventually leads to decay.
- The Cash Flow Statement's investing-activities section is where capex is most visible.

## Standalone vs consolidated statements

Pakistani annual reports often contain **two parallel sets** of financial statements: standalone (also called "separate") and consolidated. Understanding the difference is essential.

Imagine **Company A**, a textile business, has accumulated PKR 200 crore in cash and decides to invest in other businesses. It buys:

- **All of Company B** (a textile company worth PKR 50 crore). 100 percent ownership.
- **51 percent of Company C** (an IT company). Majority ownership.
- **25 percent of Company D** (an electronics company). Minority ownership.

Company A is now the **parent**. The other three are below it in different relationships:

- **Wholly-owned subsidiary**: 100 percent owned. Company B in our example.
- **Subsidiary**: more than 50 percent owned. Company C in our example. ("More than 50 percent of voting rights" if the company is unlisted; "more than 50 percent of shares" if listed.)
- **Associate**: between roughly 20 and 50 percent owned. Company D in our example.

When Company A reports financial results, it has two choices:

- **Standalone (separate) statements** report only Company A's own textile business. Subsidiary and associate results are shown only as line items (investments, dividends received).
- **Consolidated statements** report Company A's business *plus* full results of all subsidiaries (both wholly-owned and majority-owned), with associates included via the equity method (the parent's share of the associate's profit is added).

A parent company might show PKR 10 crore profit standalone and PKR 15 crore profit consolidated, because PKR 5 crore of additional profit is coming from subsidiaries.

### Which one should you read?

For investors trying to understand the *whole group*, consolidated statements give the truer picture. For investors who care specifically about the parent company's standalone business, the standalone statements are cleaner.

In practice, look at both. Discrepancies between them (parent in profit but consolidated in loss, or vice versa) often reveal interesting things about subsidiary performance.

To find out which subsidiaries and associates a parent has, look at the **Notes to the Consolidated Financial Statements**. Near the top, there is usually a section listing each subsidiary and associate with the percentage holding and country of incorporation.

## How to actually read an annual report (a practical walk)

A first-pass reading order that works for most retail investors:

1. **CEO's report and Director's report** (5 to 10 pages). Get the narrative on what happened during the year: major events, growth or contraction, new projects, regulatory changes. This is your context for everything else.
2. **Pattern of shareholding** (1 to 2 pages). Who owns the company? Major sponsors, institutions, retail. A pattern dominated by a single family that has been there for decades is normal in Pakistan; recent ownership upheaval is worth noting.
3. **Independent Auditor Report**. Look for the "true and fair view" verdict and any qualifications or key audit matters. Detailed coverage on the [Auditor Report page](/docs/fundamental-analysis/auditor-report).
4. **Six-year financial summary**. A snapshot of revenue, profit, dividends, and key ratios over six years. Lets you see trends at a glance before you dive into the detailed statements.
5. **Income Statement** and **Balance Sheet** (consolidated). Walk down each line slowly. For any number that surprises you, find the corresponding note for the breakdown.
6. **Cash Flow Statement**. Cross-check whether the profit reported on the income statement is actually translating into cash flow.
7. **Selected notes** that match the lines you cared about most.
8. **Statement of Compliance with Code of Corporate Governance**. Skim for any non-compliance disclosures.

A first-pass reading like this takes one to three hours per company. A deep dive (where you also pull last five years of reports and compute trends) takes a full weekend. Both are normal.

<Callout type="tip" title="Print the income statement and balance sheet">
  Some experienced investors print the income statement and balance
  sheet for the last three to five years of a company they are
  evaluating, lay them side by side, and physically circle the lines
  that changed materially. Doing this on paper for a single company
  the first time builds intuition that scrolling through PDFs does
  not.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- The annual report is the foundational document for fundamental analysis. Download from PSX Data Portal or the company's investor relations page.
- A typical annual report has predictable sections: directors' reports, auditor report, five financial statements, detailed notes, and corporate-governance disclosures.
- Five financial statements: Balance Sheet, Income Statement, Comprehensive Income, Changes in Equity, and Cash Flows.
- Capital expenditure creates a long-lived asset; revenue expenditure is consumed within the year.
- Standalone statements show parent only; consolidated statements include subsidiaries and associates.
- A first-pass reading takes one to three hours per company; deep dives take longer.
