# The Independent Auditor Report

If a company tells you it earned PKR 50 crore last year, how do you know that is true? You cannot walk into its head office and ask to see the books; the company would not allow it, and even if it did, you do not have the time or the auditing skills to verify thousands of transactions yourself. The **Independent Auditor Report** in every PSX-listed company's annual report is the answer to that problem. An external auditing firm, hired separately and required to follow international auditing standards, has done that verification work on your behalf. Their conclusion sits at the top of the financial-statements section.

## What you'll learn

- Why an independent auditor exists in the first place

## Why an independent auditor exists

Pakistan's [Companies Act 2017](https://www.secp.gov.pk/companies-act-2017/) and the listing regulations of PSX require every listed company to have its annual financial statements audited by an external **Independent Auditor**, registered with the Institute of Chartered Accountants of Pakistan (ICAP).

The auditor's job is straightforward to state and complex to execute:

- Verify the company's financial statements against its underlying books, transactions, and supporting evidence.
- Confirm that the statements were prepared according to applicable accounting and auditing standards.
- Issue a written opinion on whether the statements present a "true and fair view" of the company's financial position and performance.

The auditor is **paid by the company being audited** (a real conflict of interest, regulated by rotation requirements and ICAP oversight) but is **professionally and legally responsible** to the shareholders and to ICAP for the accuracy of the opinion. Audit firms that issue misleading opinions can lose their licences.

The pattern of named auditing firms in Pakistan tends to be the major international firms (KPMG, EY, A.F. Ferguson which is part of PwC, Deloitte, Grant Thornton, and BDO) plus a number of well-regarded local firms.

## The "true and fair view" verdict

The most important sentence in the Independent Auditor Report is something like:

> *"In our opinion, the consolidated financial statements give a **true and fair view** of the consolidated financial position of the group as at [date] and of its consolidated financial performance and its consolidated cash flows for the year then ended in accordance with the accounting and reporting standards as applicable in Pakistan."*

The key phrase is **"true and fair view"**. It does *not* mean the auditor agrees that the company is doing well or that the business will succeed. It means the *numbers in the statements are accurate*. If the company reports a PKR 20 crore loss, "true and fair" means the auditor checked and the loss is genuinely PKR 20 crore. If the company reports PKR 50 crore in profit, the auditor verified that the profit really is PKR 50 crore.

In other words: the auditor confirms the *numbers*. The *health of the business* is something you have to judge yourself.

## The doctor and nurse analogy

A useful way to remember the auditor's role.

You are the doctor. The company is the patient. The auditor is the nurse. The nurse takes the vitals: blood pressure, temperature, sugar level, pulse. The nurse does not diagnose the patient or recommend treatment; the nurse just records measurements accurately.

The doctor (you, the investor) takes those accurate measurements and makes the diagnosis: *is this patient healthy, sick, recovering, or in trouble?* That is your job, and the auditor's clean opinion does not do it for you.

A clean auditor report tells you the financial numbers in front of you are reliable. It does not tell you the company is a good investment.

## Types of audit opinion

The auditor's opinion can take several forms.

### Unqualified (clean) opinion

The most common in Pakistan. The auditor confirms the financial statements give a true and fair view, no significant qualifications. This is what you want to see.

### Qualified opinion

The auditor agrees the statements are mostly fine **except** for one or two specific issues. The qualifying paragraph names what the auditor disagrees with or could not verify.

A qualified opinion is not necessarily fatal but is worth taking seriously. Read the qualifying paragraph carefully. If it describes a specific accounting treatment the auditor questioned, decide whether you agree with the auditor or with the company. If it describes inventory the auditor could not verify, that is a sign of weak internal controls.

### Adverse opinion

The auditor concludes that the financial statements do **not** give a true and fair view. This is rare and serious. An adverse opinion essentially says the financial statements are not reliable.

If you ever see an adverse opinion on a Pakistani listed company's annual report, walk away. The company has not satisfied the auditor that its numbers are accurate, and you have no basis to invest.

### Disclaimer of opinion

The auditor declined to issue an opinion at all because they could not gather enough evidence to form one. This is even more serious than a qualified opinion. It usually signals severe issues with corporate governance or internal controls.

## Key Audit Matters

After the main opinion, the auditor report includes a section called **Key Audit Matters** (KAM). This section identifies the matters that, in the auditor's professional judgement, were most significant in the audit.

KAM are not the same as qualifications. The financial statements may be perfectly fine; the auditor is just flagging the areas that required the most attention or judgement.

Common Key Audit Matters in Pakistani annual reports:

- **Revenue recognition timing**. When does a sale "count" as revenue?
- **Inventory valuation**. How is slow-moving or damaged stock valued?
- **Allowance for expected credit losses on receivables**. How much of the receivables ledger is realistically collectable?
- **Goodwill or intangible asset impairment**. When does goodwill from a past acquisition need to be written down?
- **Tax provisions** including deferred tax. How aggressive or conservative are the tax estimates?
- **Going concern**. In financially stressed companies, the auditor may flag whether the company will continue operating for the next 12 months.

KAM disclosures are useful even when the audit opinion is clean, because they tell you which lines on the financial statements involve the most management judgement (and therefore the most room for error or manipulation).

## A worked example: revenue recognition for an automaker

Suppose an automobile company has an income statement showing PKR 200 crore in revenue from car sales for the year. The auditor's KAM section says:

> *"The Company has 50 vehicles in transit to dealers as at the year-end. Revenue has been recognised on these vehicles at the time of dispatch. We have considered this matter as a Key Audit Matter as the timing of revenue recognition involves significant judgement."*

The auditor is flagging that the company has booked revenue on vehicles that were dispatched but not yet delivered. The auditor's view is implicitly that revenue should ideally be recognised on delivery. The company has chosen the dispatch date.

For you as an investor, this is useful information. If the company has overstated revenue by booking on dispatch (vehicles could be damaged in transit, customers could cancel, or there could be other issues), the next year's revenue might dip when those bookings get reconciled. KAM disclosures like this give you a window into where the income statement might be optimistic.

## How to actually use the auditor report

A practical reading sequence.

1. **Find the opinion paragraph**. The auditor's actual opinion sits as a paragraph after the introduction and basis-of-opinion sections. Look for the phrase "true and fair view".
2. **Confirm it is unqualified**. If it is qualified, adverse, or a disclaimer, read the qualifying paragraph carefully and treat the company with extra caution.
3. **Read every Key Audit Matter**. Each KAM tells you where the auditor put extra effort. Anything that looks judgement-heavy or one-off (impairments, large provisions, contingent liabilities) deserves attention.
4. **Note the auditor's name**. A switch in auditor between years is sometimes routine (rotation requirements) but sometimes a signal of a disagreement. Cross-check whether the company has explained why the auditor changed.
5. **Skim the rest of the report**. Most of the rest is standard boilerplate, but some sections (auditor's responsibility, going-concern statements) can occasionally include useful nuance.

A clean unqualified opinion with reasonable Key Audit Matters and consistent auditor identity over years is the baseline expectation for a serious PSX-listed company. Anything else is worth investigating before you invest.

<Callout type="warning" title="Auditor cleanliness is necessary, not sufficient">
  A clean auditor opinion does not mean the company is a good
  investment. It only means the financial statements are reliable.
  Plenty of clean-audited Pakistani companies have produced poor
  investor returns because of bad business fundamentals. Treat the
  auditor report as a baseline check, not as a buy signal.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- The Independent Auditor verifies the accuracy of the financial statements; the doctor (you) makes the investment diagnosis.
- The "true and fair view" phrase confirms the numbers are accurate, not that the business is healthy.
- An unqualified opinion is the baseline; qualified, adverse, or disclaimer opinions are progressively more serious.
- Key Audit Matters disclose the areas that required the most judgement during the audit; they are useful even with a clean opinion.
- Cross-check the auditor name across years for unexplained switches.
- Auditor cleanliness is necessary but not sufficient. Pair it with the rest of fundamental analysis.
