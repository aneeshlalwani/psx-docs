# Balance Sheet Structure

The **Balance Sheet**, formally called the **Statement of Financial Position**, is a snapshot of a company at a single moment in time. Where the income statement covers the activity of the last 12 months, the balance sheet captures everything the company owns and owes on the last day of the financial year. For investors trying to assess solvency, leverage, and the underlying capital structure of a Pakistani business, the balance sheet is the most important single statement to understand.

## What you'll learn

- The fundamental balance sheet equation
- The two-column structure used in Pakistani annual reports
- Equity components: authorised, issued / paid-up capital, share premium, reserves
- Current vs non-current liabilities
- Current vs non-current assets
- How to read the balance sheet first when you open an annual report

## The fundamental equation

Every balance sheet in the world, regardless of country or industry, is built on a single equation:

```
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

In words: everything the company owns (assets) is funded either by money it borrowed (liabilities) or by money the owners contributed and accumulated (equity). The two sides must always balance, which is why the document is called a *balance* sheet.

Rearranging: Equity = Assets - Liabilities. If a company has PKR 100 crore in assets and PKR 60 crore in liabilities, the equity (the residual claim of the shareholders) is PKR 40 crore.

This equation is the foundation everything else rests on.

## The two main sections

A Pakistani balance sheet is laid out in two main sections, in roughly this order:

### Section 1: Equity and Liabilities

This is what funds the company. It has two sub-sections:

- **Share Capital and Reserves** (the equity side).
- **Liabilities**, further split into **Non-current Liabilities** and **Current Liabilities**.

### Section 2: Assets

This is what the company owns. It is split into:

- **Non-current Assets** (long-term, not converted to cash within a year).
- **Current Assets** (short-term, expected to be converted to cash within a year).

The total of Section 1 always equals the total of Section 2. That is the equation working.

## Equity components in detail

The **Share Capital and Reserves** section deserves a careful look because it tells you about the company's capital-raising history.

### Authorised capital

The **maximum** number of shares the company is legally allowed to issue. This is set in the company's memorandum of association and can only be changed with shareholder and regulatory approval, which is slow and bureaucratic.

A worked example: a company has authorised capital of PKR 1 crore (10 million) at face value PKR 10 each, meaning it can issue up to 1 million shares.

### Issued, subscribed, and paid-up capital

The shares the company has **actually issued** (sold to the public, sponsors, or institutions). This is always less than or equal to the authorised capital.

Continuing the example: the company decided to issue 700,000 shares at face value PKR 10. Issued, subscribed, and paid-up capital is PKR 70 lakh.

Why didn't the company issue all 1 million shares? Because keeping headroom (300,000 shares of authorised but unissued capital) means it can raise additional money by issuing those remaining shares without having to go through the slow process of increasing authorised capital. This is normal corporate practice in Pakistan.

### Share premium

The amount the company received for shares **above face value**.

Continuing the example: instead of issuing the 700,000 shares at PKR 10 each (face value), the company issued them at PKR 30 each. The PKR 20 difference per share is the share premium.

- Total raised: 700,000 × PKR 30 = PKR 2.1 crore
- Share capital line: 700,000 × PKR 10 = PKR 70 lakh
- Share premium line: 700,000 × PKR 20 = PKR 1.4 crore

### Reserves

Profits that have been retained inside the company over its history rather than paid out as dividends. This is the running total of every year's retained earnings (Profit After Tax minus dividends declared) plus any other reserves accumulated through revaluations or specific reserve transfers.

A company with large reserves has been profitable for years and has chosen to retain rather than fully distribute. This is generally a sign of financial strength. It is also the pool from which [bonus shares](/docs/corporate-actions/bonus-shares) can be issued.

### Total equity

Share capital + share premium + reserves = total equity. This is the **book value** of the shareholders' stake. The page on [Return on Equity (ROE)](/docs/fundamental-analysis/roe) shows why this number matters for measuring profitability.

## Liabilities

Liabilities are the company's obligations to others. Pakistani balance sheets always split them into current and non-current.

### Non-current liabilities

Debts that fall due **after** one financial year. The most common items:

- **Long-term loans** (the portion not due within the next year).
- **Long-term financing arrangements** (sukuk, term-finance certificates, etc.).
- **Deferred tax liability** (tax that will become payable in future periods due to timing differences).
- **Deferred revenue** (the long-term portion of revenue received in advance for goods or services not yet delivered).
- **Long-term lease liabilities** (under IFRS 16).
- **Employee benefit obligations** (pension or gratuity provisions for the long term).

### Current liabilities

Debts that fall due **within** one financial year. The most common items:

- **Current portion of long-term loans** (the part of long-term debt due within the next 12 months).
- **Trade and other payables** (money owed to suppliers, plus tax dues, customs duty, government levies).
- **Short-term borrowings** (working-capital lines, running finance from banks).
- **Unclaimed dividends** (dividends declared but not yet collected by shareholders).
- **Advances from customers** (money received before delivering goods or services).

The dedicated [Liabilities](/docs/fundamental-analysis/liabilities) page covers individual line items in more detail with worked Pakistani examples.

## Assets

Like liabilities, assets are split into non-current and current.

### Non-current assets

Things the company owns that are held for the long term and either cannot or will not be converted to cash within a year. Examples:

- **Property, Plant and Equipment (PP&E)**: factory buildings, machinery, vehicles, computers, furniture.
- **Intangible assets**: software, patents, trademarks, goodwill from acquisitions.
- **Long-term loans and advances**: loans the company has given out (e.g., to employees) that are due back after a year.
- **Long-term deposits**: money locked in long-term fixed deposits or invested in long-term instruments.
- **Deferred tax asset**: prepaid tax that becomes a relief in future periods.

The [Non-Current Assets](/docs/fundamental-analysis/non-current-assets) page goes deeper on each line.

### Current assets

Things the company owns that are expected to be converted to cash within a year. Examples:

- **Cash and bank balances**: the most liquid asset.
- **Short-term investments**: term deposits, treasury bills, Pakistan Investment Bonds maturing within a year.
- **Trade debts (receivables)**: money owed by customers for goods sold on credit.
- **Stock-in-trade (inventory)**: raw materials, work-in-process, finished goods, spare parts.
- **Stores and spares**: small consumable items used in production.
- **Short-term prepayments**: rent or insurance paid in advance.
- **Accrued return**: profit earned on investments but not yet received.

The [Current Assets](/docs/fundamental-analysis/current-assets) page covers each item with worked examples.

## How to read the balance sheet first

A practical first-pass approach when you open the balance sheet of a new Pakistani company.

1. **Total assets**. Just the absolute size. This tells you whether you are looking at a small, mid, or large company.
2. **Total equity**. The book value of the shareholders' stake. Compare with market cap to get a price-to-book sense.
3. **Total non-current liabilities** and **total current liabilities** separately. The mix tells you whether the company runs on long-term debt or short-term working capital.
4. **Quick debt-to-equity check**: total liabilities divided by total equity. Above 1 means more debt than equity. The page on [Solvency Ratios](/docs/fundamental-analysis/solvency-ratios) covers this in detail.
5. **Cash position**. How much actual cash is sitting on the balance sheet? A company with PKR 50 crore in cash relative to PKR 100 crore in market cap has a strong cash cushion.
6. **Year-on-year changes**. Look at the prior-year column. Are non-current assets growing (signal of investment in the business)? Are liabilities ballooning (signal of stress)?

You can do this first-pass review of a balance sheet in about 20 minutes once you are familiar with the layout. The deeper read (with notes for individual line items) takes longer.

## A note on industry differences

The basic balance-sheet structure is the same for every Pakistani listed company, but the *content* varies sharply by industry.

- A **bank** has loans (assets) and deposits (liabilities) as its largest items.
- A **textile mill** has property, plant, and stock-in-trade as its largest items.
- An **investment company** has equity investments and short-term investments dominating.
- A **fertiliser company** has PP&E plus subsidies receivable from government as significant.

Always compare a company against peers in its own industry, not against companies in different sectors. The page on [Comparative and Trend Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/comparative-and-trend-analysis) covers this in detail.

<Callout type="tip" title="Print and circle">
  When you read a Pakistani balance sheet for the first time, print it
  out, sit with the prior-year column in front of you, and circle
  every line that changed by more than 20 percent year-over-year.
  Those circles are the questions you take into the notes.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- The balance sheet is a snapshot at a single moment, governed by the equation Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
- Pakistani balance sheets split equity into share capital (issued / paid-up), share premium, and reserves.
- Liabilities and assets are each split into current (within one year) and non-current (beyond one year).
- A first-pass reading checks size, leverage, cash position, and year-on-year changes.
- Industry context matters; compare against peers, not across sectors.
