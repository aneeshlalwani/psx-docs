# Cash Flow Statement

If a Pakistani company's income statement says it earned PKR 50 crore in profit but its bank account shows it has less cash than at the start of the year, where did the money go? The **Cash Flow Statement** is the document that answers that question. It restates the year's activity on a pure cash basis, so you can see what was actually deposited and withdrawn, separately from the accrual-based profit on the income statement. Many experienced investors consider the cash flow statement the most important of the three core statements precisely because it is the hardest to manipulate.

## What you'll learn

- Why cash and accrual accounting can disagree
- The three activity sections: operating, investing, financing
- A worked example of each section
- How to read the bottom-line cash reconciliation
- Why operating cash flow matters more than reported profit

## Cash vs accrual

The starting point. The income statement is prepared on the **accrual basis**, which records revenue when it is earned and expenses when they are incurred, regardless of when cash actually moves. The cash flow statement is prepared on the **cash basis**, recording transactions only when cash actually changes hands.

Worked example: a Pakistani cement company has total annual sales of PKR 1 crore.

- Of this, PKR 60 lakh was paid in cash by customers during the year.
- PKR 40 lakh was sold on credit and remains as trade receivables at year-end.

On the **income statement**, revenue is **PKR 1 crore**. The full sale was earned during the year.

On the **cash flow statement**, cash from operations (specifically, cash from customers) is **PKR 60 lakh**. The PKR 40 lakh on credit has not yet hit the bank account.

The difference (PKR 40 lakh) is captured in working-capital movement: trade receivables went up by PKR 40 lakh during the year, which is a use of cash from the cash-flow perspective.

This kind of timing difference accumulates across every line of the financial statements: depreciation (a non-cash expense in the income statement), changes in inventory and payables, prepayments, deferred revenue, and many other items.

## The three sections

Every cash flow statement is divided into three activity-based sections.

### 1. Cash flow from operating activities

Cash generated from the company's main, ongoing business.

For a Pakistani manufacturer, this is the cash arriving from car sales, fertiliser sales, or whatever the company makes, minus the cash spent on raw materials, salaries, utilities, and other operating costs.

Operating activities exclude:

- **Investment income** like returns from mutual fund units (these are in investing activities).
- **Interest on debt or dividends paid** (these are in financing activities).
- **Capex spent on PP&E** (this is in investing activities).

Pakistani companies typically present operating-activities cash flow using the **indirect method**: they start with profit before tax, then add back non-cash items (depreciation, amortisation, provisions), then adjust for working-capital movements.

The line at the bottom of the operating section is the headline number: **net cash generated from (or used in) operating activities**.

### 2. Cash flow from investing activities

Cash spent on (or received from) investment-style activities. Common items:

- **Purchase of property, plant, and equipment** (the capex line). Outflow.
- **Disposal of property, plant, and equipment**. Inflow.
- **Purchase of short-term or long-term investments** (treasury bills, mutual fund units, equity stakes in other companies). Outflow.
- **Disposal of investments**. Inflow.
- **Dividends received** from investments. Inflow.
- **Interest received** from bank deposits and treasury bills. Inflow.

The line at the bottom: **net cash generated from (or used in) investing activities**.

For a healthy growing company, this is usually a net **outflow** because the company is investing in PP&E to grow productive capacity.

### 3. Cash flow from financing activities

Cash flows related to how the company is financed. Common items:

- **Loans taken from related parties or banks**. Inflow.
- **Repayment of loans**. Outflow.
- **Long-term financing arrangements**. Inflow.
- **Repayment of long-term financing**. Outflow.
- **Dividends paid to shareholders**. Outflow.
- **Issuance of new shares (rights issues, IPOs)**. Inflow.
- **Buyback of own shares**. Outflow.

The line at the bottom: **net cash generated from (or used in) financing activities**.

## A Honda Pakistan worked example

The source notes for this site walk through Honda Pakistan's cash flow statement. The pattern they describe is typical:

- **Operating activities**: cash generated from operations of about +PKR 16 million; after working-capital adjustments, net cash inflow from operations of about +PKR 12 million.
- **Investing activities**: purchase of PP&E (-PKR 3.8 million), other investment movements netting to about -PKR 3 million net outflow.
- **Financing activities**: short-term loan from related party (+PKR 2.1 million in the prior year, then repaid as a similar outflow), long-term financing taken or repaid, dividend paid (a negative). Net financing inflow of about +PKR 538,000.
- **Net change in cash for the year**: roughly +PKR 9 million.
- **Cash at the beginning of the year**: PKR 5.9 million.
- **Cash at the end of the year**: PKR 14.9 million.

You can verify the math: PKR 12 million from operations, minus PKR 3 million for investing, plus PKR 0.5 million from financing equals about PKR 9 million net change. Add the opening PKR 5.9 million and you get the closing PKR 14.9 million.

This bottom-up reconciliation is the same in every cash flow statement.

## Reading the bottom of the statement

The bottom of the cash flow statement always shows three lines:

1. **Net increase or decrease in cash and cash equivalents during the year**. The sum of the three section totals.
2. **Cash and cash equivalents at the beginning of the year**.
3. **Cash and cash equivalents at the end of the year**.

The third line should match the cash balance shown on the balance sheet at year-end.

This reconciliation is your sanity check. The cash flow statement and balance sheet have to agree on how much cash the company actually has.

## What investors actually look at

For a first-pass reading, the most important numbers are.

### Operating cash flow vs PAT

Compare net cash from operating activities to Profit After Tax (the income statement bottom line).

- **OCF significantly above PAT**: very healthy. The company's profit is translating into cash plus more (often because non-cash depreciation is being added back).
- **OCF roughly equal to PAT**: normal.
- **OCF significantly below PAT**: a flag. Profit is being absorbed by working capital growth (expanding receivables, inventory) or by paper gains. Either way, the cash is not actually accumulating.

A company with multiple consecutive years of OCF well below PAT is showing reported profit that is not real cash. This pattern often precedes accounting issues or a working-capital crisis.

### Free cash flow

A common derived metric:

```
Free cash flow (FCF) = Operating cash flow - Capital expenditure
```

FCF is the cash left over after the company has reinvested in maintaining and growing its productive assets. Positive FCF is what funds dividends, debt repayment, and acquisitions. Negative FCF means the company is funding capex from external sources (debt or new equity).

FCF is not a standard line on a Pakistani cash flow statement, but you can compute it easily from the operating-activities total minus the PP&E-purchase line in investing activities.

### Dividend coverage

Compare cash dividends paid (financing activities) to operating cash flow. If the company is paying out dividends roughly equal to or below operating cash flow, dividends are sustainable from operating activity. If dividends exceed operating cash flow consistently, the company is paying dividends from cash reserves or borrowings.

## A common pattern: profit without cash

A worked illustration of what to watch for.

Year 1: Revenue PKR 100 crore, PAT PKR 12 crore, Operating cash flow PKR 11 crore. Healthy.
Year 2: Revenue PKR 120 crore, PAT PKR 14 crore, Operating cash flow PKR 8 crore. Hmm.
Year 3: Revenue PKR 150 crore, PAT PKR 17 crore, Operating cash flow PKR 4 crore. Real concern.

Profit is rising every year, but cash from operations is falling. Something is absorbing cash: receivables are growing faster than revenue, inventory is piling up, or some combination. Even though the income statement looks great, the cash flow statement is screaming that the company is not actually producing cash from its growing sales.

This pattern has preceded many Pakistani and global accounting blow-ups. Never trust the income statement alone.

<Callout type="tip" title="Always read all three statements together">
  The income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement are
  three views of the same underlying business. Each on its own can
  mislead. Reading all three together (with the notes for context)
  is what gives you the real picture.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- The cash flow statement restates the year's activity on a cash basis, separately from accrual-based profit.
- Three sections: operating activities (the main business), investing activities (capex and investments), financing activities (loans, dividends, share issuances).
- The bottom of the statement reconciles opening cash, closing cash, and the year's net change.
- Operating cash flow well below PAT is a flag for working-capital deterioration or paper profits.
- Free cash flow (OCF minus capex) is what funds dividends and debt repayment.
- Always cross-read against the income statement and balance sheet; one statement alone can mislead.
