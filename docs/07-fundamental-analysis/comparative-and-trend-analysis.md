# Comparative and Trend Analysis

Every page in this category has shown how to compute one metric in isolation: a current ratio, a P/E, a ROE, a DSI. None of those numbers means anything in isolation. **Comparative and trend analysis** is the discipline of putting individual numbers in context, both across time (trends) and across companies (peer comparison). It is the final synthesis step in fundamental analysis, and it is where most of the actual investment decisions get made.

## What you'll learn

- The two dimensions of context: trend (across time) and comparative (across peers)
- How to set up a five-year trend table for a Pakistani company
- How to do industry comparison
- How to do peer-group comparison
- Common patterns to look for and what they mean

## Why context matters

A worked illustration. Consider a Pakistani company with the following metrics for one year:

- Revenue growth: 10 percent.
- Net profit margin: 8 percent.
- ROE: 15 percent.
- Debt-to-equity: 0.6.
- Current ratio: 1.5.

Are these numbers good, bad, or average? **You cannot tell from the numbers alone.**

- If the industry average net margin is 4 percent, an 8 percent margin is excellent.
- If the industry average ROE is 22 percent, a 15 percent ROE is below par.
- If the company posted a 20 percent net margin three years ago and is at 8 percent now, the trajectory is bad.
- If the company posted 4 percent margin three years ago and is at 8 percent now, the trajectory is great.

The absolute numbers gain meaning only when placed in two contexts: **trend** (how the company has changed over time) and **comparative** (how the company compares to peers).

## Trend analysis

Trend analysis means looking at the same financial data across **multiple periods**, typically five to ten years, to identify patterns of improvement, decline, or stability.

### What to track

Across five years, build a table for the company you are evaluating. Rows are metrics, columns are years.

Useful metrics to include:

- **Revenue** (absolute and growth rate)
- **Gross profit** and gross margin
- **EBIT** and operating margin
- **PAT** and net margin
- **EPS** (basic)
- **Operating cash flow**
- **Total assets**
- **Total equity**
- **Total debt**
- **Dividend per share**
- **Key ratios**: ROE, ROCE, ROA, current ratio, debt-to-equity, interest coverage, DSI

This table is your trend dashboard. It takes a few hours to build for a single company (extracting from five years of annual reports) but is the most useful single fundamental-analysis exercise you can do.

### Patterns to look for

| Pattern | Likely interpretation |
|---|---|
| Steadily rising revenue, stable margins | Healthy organic growth |
| Steadily rising revenue, declining margins | Market-share-buying through price competition |
| Stable revenue, declining margins | Pricing pressure or cost inflation |
| Stable revenue, rising margins | Operational improvement (cost discipline, efficiency) |
| Declining revenue, declining margins | Structural decline; serious flag |
| Volatile revenue and margins | Cyclical business; treat differently |
| Stable ratios but rising debt | Leverage building without operating improvement |
| ROE rising while ROCE flat | Leverage trick; financial engineering |
| ROCE rising | Genuine operating efficiency improvement |

A Pakistani company showing steady positive trends across multiple metrics over five years is a strong candidate. One showing deterioration in any major area is worth a careful explanation before investing.

### Growth rates

Compute year-over-year growth for revenue, EPS, and operating cash flow. Calculate the **compound annual growth rate (CAGR)** over the full five years.

```
CAGR = (Ending value / Starting value)^(1 / Number of years) - 1
```

A worked example. Revenue went from PKR 50 crore to PKR 80 crore over five years.

```
CAGR = (80/50)^(1/5) - 1 = 1.0986 - 1 = 0.0986 = 9.86%
```

The company has grown revenue at roughly 10 percent per year compounded over five years. Compare against the industry average and against Pakistani inflation (around 11 percent in recent years) to judge whether real growth is happening.

A company growing 10 percent in nominal terms when inflation is 11 percent is shrinking in real terms.

## Comparative (peer) analysis

Trend analysis tells you how a company has changed over time. Comparative analysis tells you how it stacks up against others doing the same thing.

### Industry comparison

Compare the company's metrics against the industry average. Sources for industry averages:

- **Sector reports** from major brokerage houses (Topline, Arif Habib, JS Global, Optimus Capital, AKD).
- **PSX sector indices** for aggregate sector trends.
- **Computing peer averages yourself** by taking the mean across the major listed companies in the sector.

A company outperforming the industry average across multiple metrics is genuinely strong. A company underperforming on most metrics is below par for its sector. Mixed performance (better on some, worse on others) is the most common case and requires interpretation.

### Peer-group comparison

A more focused version of industry comparison. Pick three to five companies that are most similar to the one you are evaluating: same sub-sector, similar size, similar geographic exposure. Compare side by side.

A worked example. You are evaluating a Pakistani cement company. Pick the major listed Pakistani cement companies (DGKC, Lucky Cement, Maple Leaf, Cherat, Fauji Cement, etc.). Build a comparison table:

| Metric | DGKC | Lucky | Maple Leaf | Cherat | Industry avg |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue (5-yr CAGR) | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Gross margin | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Operating margin | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| ROE | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| ROCE | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Debt-to-equity | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Interest coverage | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| P/E (current) | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| EV/EBITDA | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Dividend yield | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

Reading this table reveals which company is best on operating efficiency, which on capital structure, which on valuation, and which is most attractive overall on a risk-adjusted basis.

### What "best" means depends on style

Different investing styles weight the comparison differently.

- **Value investors** look for low P/E, low EV/EBITDA, high dividend yield, low price-to-book.
- **Quality investors** look for high ROE, high ROCE, low debt, stable margins.
- **Growth investors** look for high revenue growth, high EPS growth, high reinvestment rate at high ROCE.
- **Yield investors** look for high dividend yield with sustainable payout coverage.

The best company by one style might not be best by another. Be clear about your own style before comparing.

## Common output patterns

A few patterns that emerge frequently in Pakistani comparative analysis.

### "The cheapest is cheap for a reason"

The lowest-P/E company in a peer group often has the most issues. Maybe declining margins, weakening market share, governance concerns, or industry-specific headwinds it has not yet recovered from. Always understand why a company is cheap before declaring it a bargain.

### "The strongest gets a premium"

The best-performing company in a peer group typically trades at a premium P/E or EV/EBITDA. That premium is usually justified, but be cautious about paying a much-higher-than-peers multiple for incremental quality differences.

### "Volatile sector, stable winner"

In cyclical industries like Pakistani cement and fertiliser, the company that maintains stable margins and ROE through a cycle (rather than oscillating wildly) is usually the long-term winner. Look at the standard deviation of margins across five years, not just averages.

### "Rising tide lifts all boats"

Sometimes an entire industry has a great year. All players post improved margins and rising EPS. Distinguishing genuine company-specific outperformance from sector-wide tailwinds is critical. Compare individual companies' improvements against the sector average; if everyone improved 30 percent and your candidate improved 32 percent, that is not company-specific outperformance.

## A practical worked-example framework

For a Pakistani company you are evaluating, the comparative-and-trend exercise:

1. **Build a 5-year trend table** of the major financial metrics and ratios.
2. **Identify the major directional trends**: improving, stable, deteriorating.
3. **Pick 3 to 5 peers** of similar size and sector.
4. **Build a peer comparison table** for the most recent year, plus 5-year averages.
5. **Identify where the company is best, worst, and average** in the peer group.
6. **Write a one-paragraph summary** of strengths, weaknesses, and key questions.
7. **Use this summary** as the input to the [Intrinsic Value vs Market Price](/docs/fundamental-analysis/intrinsic-value-vs-market-price) decision.

A serious version of this exercise takes a full weekend per company. It is the work that separates fundamental investors from people who just look at a P/E ratio and click buy.

## Key takeaways

- Comparative and trend analysis is the synthesis step that gives meaning to individual ratios.
- Trend analysis (5 to 10 years) reveals whether a company is improving, stable, or deteriorating across multiple dimensions.
- Comparative analysis (against industry and peers) shows whether the company is best, worst, or average among similar businesses.
- Different investing styles (value, quality, growth, yield) weight the comparisons differently.
- The cheapest peer is usually cheap for a reason; the strongest peer usually trades at a premium.
- Distinguish company-specific outperformance from sector-wide tailwinds.
- Build a reusable template once; apply across many companies.
