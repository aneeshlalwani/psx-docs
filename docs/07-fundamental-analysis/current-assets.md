# Current Assets

The **Current Assets** section of the balance sheet is the company's short-term financial machinery: cash on hand, money owed by customers, raw materials waiting to be turned into products, finished products waiting to be sold, and short-term investments parked for liquidity. For Pakistani investors trying to understand whether a company can pay its short-term bills, the current assets section is half of the picture (the other half is current liabilities, which make up the [liquidity ratios](/docs/fundamental-analysis/liquidity-ratios) covered later).

This page walks through the main line items using the kind of structure typical in a Pakistani manufacturer's annual report.

## What you'll learn

- Stores and spares, stock-in-trade, and the difference
- Trade debts (receivables), and the unsecured / secured distinction
- Short-term prepayments
- Accrued return
- Other receivables and taxation net
- Short-term investments

## Stores and spares

The first line on most Pakistani manufacturers' current-asset section. **Stores and spares** are small consumable items used in the production process: lubricants, adhesives, small tools, cleaning supplies, packaging materials.

For a wooden chair: most of the cost is the wood, but you also need glue, screws, varnish, and small fittings. Those small items are "stores and spares". They are not what the business sells; they support the production of what the business sells.

The line is usually small relative to total inventory but always disclosed separately, because it represents items consumed in the production cycle rather than the products themselves.

## Stock-in-trade

The biggest current-asset line for most Pakistani manufacturing companies. **Stock-in-trade** is the inventory of products and materials directly tied to what the company sells.

If you click into the Note for Stock-in-trade in any Pakistani annual report, you typically see a structure like this:

### In Hand

Things the company physically possesses on the balance-sheet date.

- **Raw material** ("khaam maal"). Materials that have not yet been processed. For Toyota Indus Motor, this would include sheet metal, electronic components, paints, fabrics, and other inputs.
- **Work-in-process**. Products partway through manufacturing. For an automaker, half-built cars on the assembly line.
- **Finished goods**. Products built and ready for sale, sitting at the factory or in transit to dealers.
- **Spare parts**. Parts the company sells to support its products in the after-market.

### In Transit

Things the company has bought but not yet received. If the company ordered raw material from an overseas supplier and the goods are still on a ship, they show as "in transit" inventory.

### Why this matters

For an investor:

- **Raw material levels**: rising raw material can signal the company is stocking up for expected production growth, or it can signal goods that are not selling and inventory backing up.
- **Finished goods levels**: a sudden jump in finished goods often means sales slowed but production did not. This is usually a worry; the company will eventually have to discount the goods or write them down.
- **Total inventory days**: the [Days Sales Inventory (DSI)](/docs/fundamental-analysis/days-sales-inventory) ratio measures how long the company is sitting on inventory before it converts to revenue. Slowing DSI is a warning signal.

## Trade debts (receivables)

**Trade debts** is the money customers owe the company for goods sold on credit but not yet paid for.

Pakistani sales practice often involves credit terms, especially for B2B customers (e.g., government agencies, large dealers, institutional clients). The company delivers the product and waits 30 to 90 days for payment. The unpaid amount during that window sits as trade debts.

### Secured vs unsecured

A line item to watch for in the notes. Trade debts can be:

- **Secured**: the company holds **collateral** worth at least the amount owed. If the customer does not pay, the company can sell the collateral and recover the money.
- **Unsecured**: the company does not hold collateral. If the customer does not pay, the company can sue but recovery is uncertain.

A worked example. Toyota Indus has trade debts of about PKR 1.14 crore from a fleet sale to a buyer who has 6 to 8 months to pay.

- If Toyota took collateral worth PKR 1.14 crore from the buyer, the trade is **secured**. Risk is low.
- If Toyota sold without collateral, the trade is **unsecured**. Risk depends on the buyer's reliability.

If unsecured trade debts are a large amount, look at the note for who the counterparty is. In Toyota's case, the note discloses that "These balances relate to various customers, primarily government organizations, from whom there is no recent history of default." Because government counterparties have a strong track record of eventually paying, Toyota repeatedly extends unsecured credit.

This kind of detail is what makes the trade-debts line worth reading carefully rather than just glancing at the total.

### Allowance for expected credit losses

Pakistani financial reporting under IFRS 9 requires companies to recognise an **expected credit loss (ECL)** allowance against trade debts: a provision for the portion that is realistically uncollectable. The note discloses this allowance separately. If the allowance is growing fast year over year, the company is signalling that more receivables are at risk.

## Short-term prepayments

Money the company has paid in advance for something it has not yet received.

Common examples:

- **Building rent paid quarterly or annually in advance**. The portion covering periods after the balance-sheet date is a prepayment.
- **Insurance premium paid annually**. The unconsumed portion is a prepayment.
- **Advance to suppliers** for goods that will be delivered after year-end.
- **Security deposits** with utilities, landlords, regulatory bodies.

The economic logic: the company has parted with cash but received an asset (the right to a service or refund) in return. As the underlying period elapses, the prepayment is "consumed" and shifted to the income statement as an expense.

For investors, prepayments are usually a small line. Sudden growth can signal the company is locking in future commitments (e.g., bulk-buying supplies in advance of expected price rises).

## Accrued return

The flip side of advance receipt. **Accrued return** is profit the company has *earned* but not yet *received*.

Worked example. The company invests PKR 1 crore in a 6-month term deposit at 10 percent annual return. After 6 months, it will receive PKR 5 lakh in interest. At month 3 (the balance-sheet date in the middle of the deposit), the company has earned PKR 2.5 lakh but has not received the cash yet. That PKR 2.5 lakh is accrued return.

Other examples: dividends declared but not yet paid by an investee company, bond coupons due but not yet credited.

The line is usually small for Pakistani manufacturing companies. For investment-heavy companies (insurance, banks, asset managers), it can be larger.

## Other receivables and taxation net

Two often-overlooked but worth-knowing lines.

### Other receivables

Money the company is owed from miscellaneous sources that do not fit a specific category. Examples: refunds from utilities, claims under insurance, claims against suppliers for damaged goods, rebates from regulators.

Usually small. If "other receivables" is ballooning over years, ask why; vague growth in this line can hide receivables that should be classified more strictly.

### Taxation net

When a company has overpaid tax in prior years, it has a net tax-relief receivable from FBR. This shows as "Taxation - Net" in current assets. Conversely, if the company underpaid, it would show as a tax payable in current liabilities.

The "Net" suffix means the figure is the net of tax already paid, tax owed, and adjustments. For most retail investors this is a footnote-level item.

## Short-term investments

Money the company has parked in short-term instruments that will mature within the next 12 months.

Common Pakistani examples:

- **Term Deposit Receipts (TDRs)** with banks for periods under 12 months.
- **Treasury Bills** issued by the Government of Pakistan, typically with 3-month, 6-month, or 12-month maturities.
- **Pakistan Investment Bonds (PIBs)** in their final 12 months before maturity.
- **Money market mutual fund units** held for short-term liquidity.
- **Short-term investments in equity securities** (less common; usually classified differently).

A company with a large short-term investment line is essentially holding cash equivalents. This is a sign of strong liquidity but also (potentially) a sign that management has more cash than it knows how to deploy.

## A typical Pakistani manufacturer's current-assets section

For a solid Pakistani manufacturing company:

| Line | Typical share |
|---|---|
| Cash and bank balances | 10 to 30 percent |
| Short-term investments | 0 to 30 percent |
| Trade debts | 10 to 25 percent |
| Stock-in-trade | 25 to 40 percent |
| Stores and spares | 1 to 5 percent |
| Short-term prepayments | 1 to 5 percent |
| Other receivables, accrued returns, taxation net | 5 to 10 percent |

A manufacturer where **stock-in-trade plus trade debts** is most of current assets is normal: that is what manufacturing operations look like.

A manufacturer where **cash plus short-term investments** is most of current assets has lots of liquid surplus. This is a strong-liquidity profile but raises capital-allocation questions (why isn't the cash being deployed?).

A manufacturer where **trade debts** alone is huge relative to revenue is potentially in trouble: customers are not paying on time, and the company is effectively financing them.

<Callout type="tip" title="Watch the trade-debts trend">
  Track total trade debts as a percentage of annual revenue across
  five years. If trade debts are 60 days of revenue this year vs. 30
  days five years ago, customers are paying slower. That can signal
  weakening customer relationships, deteriorating end markets, or
  the company offering longer credit terms to maintain sales.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Current assets are short-term resources expected to convert to cash within a year.
- Stores and spares are small consumables; stock-in-trade is the actual inventory of raw material, work-in-process, and finished goods.
- Trade debts represent credit sales; secured vs unsecured matters, and the notes disclose counterparty quality.
- Short-term prepayments and accrued returns capture timing differences between cash flow and economic activity.
- Short-term investments include TDRs, treasury bills, PIBs, and money-market funds.
- For investors, the mix of operating items (inventory, receivables) vs liquid items (cash, investments) tells you whether the company is operating-heavy or liquidity-heavy.
