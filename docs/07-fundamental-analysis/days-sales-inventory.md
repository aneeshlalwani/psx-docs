# Days Sales Inventory (DSI)

Liquidity and solvency ratios are static. They tell you the snapshot at year-end. **Days Sales Inventory (DSI)** is one of a family of ratios called **efficiency ratios** that measure the speed at which the business is operating. DSI specifically measures: on average, how many days does inventory sit on the company's books before it is sold and converted into receivables (then eventually cash)?

For investors, DSI is one of the most useful early-warning indicators. A rising DSI usually signals that sales have slowed before the income statement shows it.

## The formula

```
DSI = (Inventory / COGS) × 365
```

Inventory comes from the balance sheet (typically the sum of stores and spares plus stock-in-trade for Pakistani manufacturers). COGS is the Cost of Sales line on the income statement.

A worked example with Nestle Pakistan-style numbers.

- Inventory = stores and spares (PKR 26 lakh) + stock-in-trade (PKR 1.62 crore) = roughly PKR 1.89 crore.
- COGS for the year = PKR 8.40 crore.

```
DSI = (1.89 / 8.40) × 365 = 0.225 × 365 = 82 days
```

The interpretation: Nestle is converting its inventory into cash on average every 82 days. Or, equivalently: the inventory it holds at any moment is enough to support roughly 82 days of operations.

## "How can low inventory generate high COGS?"

A common confusion. Inventory of PKR 1.89 crore generating COGS of PKR 8.40 crore in a year seems contradictory at first.

The resolution: **inventory is what the company holds at one moment**. Over a year, the company refills its inventory many times.

A simpler example. A small kiryana (grocery) shop has PKR 5 lakh of stock at any moment. Over a year, it sells PKR 25 lakh worth of goods.

```
DSI = (5 / 25) × 365 = 0.2 × 365 = 73 days
```

The inventory turns over roughly every 73 days. The shop refills its shelves about 5 times a year (365 / 73 ≈ 5).

The same logic applies at company scale. A company with PKR 2 crore in inventory can have PKR 20 crore in annual COGS if it turns over inventory 10 times in the year.

## Why DSI is an early-warning signal

Compare the kiryana example over time.

- Year 1: DSI = 73 days. Normal.
- Year 2: DSI = 90 days. Slightly slower.
- Year 3: DSI = 120 days. Inventory is sitting much longer.

A 73-to-120-days jump in DSI usually means one of the following.

- **Sales have slowed** but production or purchasing has not adjusted, so finished goods are piling up.
- **A new competitor has entered** and is taking market share. Customers are buying less from this company, so inventory does not move as fast.
- **The company has raised prices** beyond what the market is willing to bear, leading to slower sell-through.
- **Quality has dropped** and customers are shifting to alternatives.
- **The end market itself has weakened** (a recession, a regulatory shock, etc.).

Whatever the cause, rising DSI signals trouble that often shows up in revenue decline 1 to 2 quarters later. Watching DSI carefully gives you a leading view that the income statement does not.

The opposite is also true: falling DSI (from 90 to 60 days, say) often signals that demand is improving and inventory is moving faster.

## A practical Pakistani check

When evaluating a Pakistani manufacturer, compute DSI for the most recent year, then look up DSI for the same company three to five years ago. Patterns to interpret:

| DSI trend | Likely interpretation |
|---|---|
| Stable across years | Demand and operations are in equilibrium |
| Falling | Demand is improving; inventory turning faster |
| Slowly rising (5 to 10 days per year) | Demand softening, working capital tightening |
| Sharply rising (20+ days year-over-year) | Major demand or operational issue; investigate |

Cross-reference DSI changes with the directors' report. The company will usually disclose if there has been a strategic stocking decision (e.g., building inventory ahead of an expected price rise, or in anticipation of a new product launch).

## Lower DSI is generally better, but with limits

Lower DSI means faster cash conversion, less working capital tied up, and less risk of inventory obsolescence. So lower is generally better.

But there are limits.

- **Too low DSI** can mean the company is running too lean and risking stockouts. If demand spikes, the company cannot fulfil orders and loses sales.
- **Industry-specific minimums** apply. A perishable-goods company (dairy, fresh food) needs lower DSI than a building-materials company (cement, steel).

Compare against industry peers, not in isolation. A DSI of 30 days might be excellent for a textile company and dangerously low for a cement company that needs strategic stockpiles.

## Industry context

Approximate DSI ranges for Pakistani manufacturers in normal years.

| Sector | Typical DSI |
|---|---|
| FMCG (Nestle, Engro Foods, Unilever) | 30 to 90 days |
| Pharmaceutical | 60 to 120 days |
| Cement | 20 to 60 days |
| Fertiliser | 30 to 90 days (seasonal) |
| Steel and heavy industry | 60 to 120 days |
| Textiles | 60 to 150 days |
| Automobiles | 30 to 90 days |

Seasonal industries (fertiliser, certain agriculture-linked businesses) have DSI that spikes and falls within the year. Look at multiple years' data, not single snapshots, for these.

## Combine with receivables and payables

DSI is one of three efficiency metrics that together measure the **cash conversion cycle**.

```
Cash conversion cycle = Days Inventory + Days Receivables - Days Payables

where:
Days Receivables = (Trade debts / Revenue) × 365
Days Payables = (Trade payables / COGS) × 365
```

A worked example. A company has:

- DSI = 60 days
- Days Receivables = 45 days
- Days Payables = 30 days

```
Cash conversion cycle = 60 + 45 - 30 = 75 days
```

The company spends cash today on inventory, takes 60 days to sell it, then waits 45 more days to collect from customers, but pays its own suppliers in 30 days. Net: 75 days of working-capital exposure.

A shortening cash conversion cycle (cycle dropping from 75 to 50 days) is excellent. A lengthening cycle (75 to 100 days) is concerning.

DSI alone gives part of the picture; the full cash conversion cycle gives the rest.

<Callout type="tip" title="Compute DSI on a quarterly basis for early warning">
  Annual DSI is useful, but quarterly DSI is even better. If a
  Pakistani company's DSI rises 15 days quarter-over-quarter, the
  signal is fresher than waiting for the annual report. Pakistani
  quarterly statements (3-month, 6-month, 9-month, and year-end
  reports) are filed with PSX within 30 to 60 days, giving you
  near-real-time efficiency data.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- DSI = (Inventory / COGS) × 365. Number of days the company holds inventory before selling.
- Inventory turns over many times per year; that is why low inventory can generate large annual COGS.
- Rising DSI is an early-warning signal of slowing demand or operational issues, often visible before revenue decline.
- Lower DSI is generally better, but compare against industry peers; some industries need strategic inventory.
- Combine with Days Receivables and Days Payables to compute the full cash conversion cycle.
- Track DSI quarterly, not just annually, for the earliest signal.
