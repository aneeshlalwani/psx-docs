# Enterprise Value (EV)

If you wanted to buy a company outright (not just some of its shares, but the entire business), how much would it actually cost? The answer is not just the market capitalisation. The market cap tells you what the equity is worth, but when you take over a company you also inherit its debts (which you have to pay off) and you receive its cash (which is now yours). **Enterprise Value (EV)** captures all three. It is the metric most professional investors prefer when they want to compare companies with different capital structures.

## What you'll learn

- The EV formula and a worked example
- Why market cap alone misleads when companies have different debt and cash levels
- EV-based ratios: EV/EBITDA, EV/Sales, EV/EBIT
- When EV is more useful than P/E

## The formula

```
Enterprise Value (EV) = Market capitalisation + Total debt - Cash and cash equivalents

where:
Market capitalisation = Current share price × Number of shares outstanding
Total debt = Long-term loans + Short-term borrowings (interest-bearing)
Cash and cash equivalents = Cash + Short-term marketable investments
```

A worked example. A Pakistani company has:

- Market cap of PKR 1 crore (the equity is worth PKR 1 crore at current share price).
- Total debt of PKR 60 lakh.
- Cash on the balance sheet of PKR 20 lakh.

If you wanted to buy the whole company, what would it cost?

You would pay PKR 1 crore to current shareholders for their shares. But you would also inherit PKR 60 lakh of debt that you have to repay, and you would gain PKR 20 lakh of cash already in the company.

```
Net cost = Equity payment + Debt to be repaid - Cash gained
        = 1,00,00,000 + 60,00,000 - 20,00,000
        = 1,40,00,000
        = PKR 1.40 crore
```

The Enterprise Value is **PKR 1.40 crore**, not the PKR 1 crore market cap.

## Why this matters: capital structure neutrality

EV becomes useful when comparing companies with different debt and cash positions.

Two Pakistani companies in the same industry, same EBIT, same revenue.

| | Co X | Co Y |
|---|---|---|
| Market cap | PKR 100 crore | PKR 100 crore |
| Total debt | PKR 0 | PKR 50 crore |
| Cash | PKR 10 crore | PKR 5 crore |
| EV | 100 + 0 - 10 = 90 crore | 100 + 50 - 5 = 145 crore |
| EBIT | PKR 20 crore | PKR 20 crore |
| EV / EBIT | 4.5 | 7.25 |

Both companies have the same equity value, but Co Y has much more debt. Looking at market cap alone, you might think they are valued similarly. EV reveals that buying Co Y outright would cost much more than buying Co X, because you take on PKR 50 crore of debt with the deal.

EV / EBIT shows that Co X is much cheaper than Co Y on operating-earnings terms (4.5x vs 7.25x). The pure equity ratios (P/E) do not show this so clearly.

## EV-based ratios

Several common ratios use EV as the numerator.

### EV / EBITDA

The most popular EV-based ratio, especially for cross-border or cross-industry comparisons.

```
EV / EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA

where EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortisation
```

EBITDA strips out non-cash charges (depreciation, amortisation) and financing effects (interest, tax), giving an even more "operational" denominator than EBIT. EV/EBITDA is widely used by acquirers and private-equity investors.

A Pakistani manufacturer with EV of PKR 200 crore and EBITDA of PKR 25 crore has EV/EBITDA = 8x. Compare against industry peers; lower is generally cheaper.

### EV / Sales

Useful for companies that are not yet profitable or whose profit metrics are volatile.

```
EV / Sales = Enterprise Value / Annual revenue
```

EV/Sales lets you compare valuations across companies even when EBITDA is negative or distorted. It is most useful in growth sectors and during cyclical troughs.

### EV / EBIT

A cleaner version of P/E that accounts for capital structure. Used by professional analysts comparing operating businesses.

## When EV beats P/E

A Pakistani investor preferring EV-based ratios over P/E in several scenarios.

### 1. Comparing companies with very different debt levels

Two companies with the same P/E can have very different total business values. EV/EBITDA puts them on a level footing.

### 2. Comparing companies across capital-structure changes

A company that just took on a large amount of debt to fund an acquisition will see its P/E ratio change in ways that reflect financing rather than business performance. EV-based ratios are more stable through these changes.

### 3. Acquisition analysis

If you are evaluating whether one company would be a good acquisition target for another, or doing the analysis a strategic acquirer would do, EV is the natural metric. Acquirers always think in EV terms.

### 4. Cross-industry comparisons

Different industries have different typical debt levels. Banks operate with very high leverage; consumer-goods companies with very little. EV-based ratios are more comparable across these contexts than equity-only ratios.

## Limitations of EV

EV is not a magic ratio that solves all comparison problems.

- **EV ignores off-balance-sheet liabilities** like operating leases (somewhat addressed by IFRS 16 capitalisation but not fully), pension obligations, and contingent liabilities. A company that looks cheap on EV/EBITDA might be expensive once these are included.
- **EV depends on book values of debt and cash**. Market values might differ for distressed debt or illiquid investments.
- **For banks and financial institutions**, EV is not very meaningful because deposits are formally liabilities but function as raw material rather than financing. Banks have their own valuation frameworks.

For Pakistani non-financial businesses, these limitations are usually minor and EV-based ratios are very useful.

## A practical worked Pakistani example

A Pakistani cement company with:

- 1 crore (100 million) shares outstanding.
- Current share price: PKR 250.
- Total long-term debt: PKR 80 crore.
- Short-term borrowings: PKR 20 crore.
- Cash and short-term investments: PKR 30 crore.
- EBITDA for the year: PKR 60 crore.

Step 1: Market cap.

```
Market cap = 250 × 1,00,00,000 = 2,500 crore
```

Wait, that gives PKR 25,000 crore which is too large for a typical mid-cap. Adjusting: assume 100 lakh (10 million) shares.

Restart with cleaner numbers: 10 million shares × PKR 250 = PKR 25,000 lakh = PKR 250 crore market cap.

Step 2: EV.

```
EV = 250 + 80 + 20 - 30 = 320 crore
```

Step 3: EV/EBITDA.

```
EV/EBITDA = 320 / 60 = 5.33x
```

If the Pakistani cement industry typical EV/EBITDA is around 7x, this company is trading cheaper than peers, which is worth investigating further.

<Callout type="tip" title="Use EV in screening">
  When screening Pakistani companies for value, EV-based ratios
  often surface candidates that pure P/E screens miss. A company
  with high debt and low cash might look unattractive on P/E but
  cheap on EV/EBITDA because the debt is being efficiently
  serviced and the operating business is strong. Pair the two
  screens for a richer candidate list.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Enterprise Value = Market cap + Total debt - Cash. The full cost of buying the whole business.
- EV is more useful than market cap when comparing companies with different debt and cash levels.
- Common EV-based ratios: EV/EBITDA, EV/Sales, EV/EBIT.
- EV neutralises capital structure differences in a way that P/E does not.
- For banks, EV is less meaningful; specialised metrics apply.
- Use EV-based screening alongside P/E-based screening for a more complete value search.
