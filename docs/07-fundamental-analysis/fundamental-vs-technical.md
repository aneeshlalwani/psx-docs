# Fundamental vs. Technical Analysis

There is an old joke that fundamental analysts and technical analysts each think the other group is wasting their time. The honest truth is that both approaches have produced wealthy investors and both have produced failures. The two approaches answer different questions and suit different time horizons. Understanding the distinction helps you decide where to spend your study hours.

## What you'll learn

- The core question each approach tries to answer
- A side-by-side comparison
- The time horizons each fits
- Why most Pakistani retail investors should lean fundamental
- When to combine them

## The core question

Fundamental and technical analysis differ at the level of what they actually study.

**Fundamental analysis** asks: *"What is this business worth, and is the market offering me a fair price for a piece of it?"* It looks at the company's financial statements, management, competitive position, industry, and macro environment.

**Technical analysis** asks: *"Where is the price likely to go in the short term, given how it has moved recently and what patterns the chart shows?"* It looks at the share price chart, volume, moving averages, momentum indicators, and chart patterns. It does not particularly care what the company makes or how profitable it is.

Both approaches assume that history contains useful information. Fundamental assumes the *business history* tells you about future cash flows. Technical assumes the *price history* tells you about future price moves.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Feature | Fundamental Analysis | Technical Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| Primary focus | Intrinsic value of the business | Price patterns and momentum |
| Inputs | Financial statements, management, industry, economy | Price, volume, charts, indicators |
| Time horizon | Years (long-term investing) | Days to weeks (trading) |
| Approach | Mostly qualitative + quantitative | Mostly quantitative (chart-driven) |
| Decision style | "Is this a good business at a fair price?" | "Is the trend up or down right now?" |
| Updated when | New annual report, news, regulation, sector shift | Every trading session |
| Famous practitioners | Buffett, Munger, Lynch, Graham | Wyckoff, Livermore, modern quants |

## Time horizons

This is often the cleanest way to choose between the two.

- **Multi-year horizon, hands-off, building wealth**: lean fundamental. The page on [Long-Term vs Short-Term Investing](/docs/investing-101/long-term-vs-short-term-investing) explains why long horizons reward fundamental work.
- **Days-to-weeks horizon, watching the screen, looking for short-term moves**: lean technical. Recognise that short-term trading is harder than it looks; most retail traders lose money over time.

Most Pakistani retail investors should be in the first camp. The structural advantages of long-term, fundamentally driven investing (compounding, lower transaction costs, less tax drag, less time spent watching screens) are very large.

## Why most Pakistani retail investors should lean fundamental

Several practical points specific to the Pakistani market.

### 1. Transaction costs and taxes hurt active trading

Pakistani brokerage commissions plus capital-gains tax on short-term gains add up. The page on [Capital Gains Tax](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/capital-gains-tax) shows that holding longer reduces tax. Frequent trading turns reasonable gross returns into mediocre net returns.

### 2. PSX has fewer technical-analysis tools than developed markets

Sophisticated technical platforms, options markets, and short-selling infrastructure are less mature in Pakistan than in the US. Pure technical strategies that depend on these tools are harder to execute on PSX.

### 3. Long-term Pakistani equity returns have historically been strong

The KSE-100 has produced significant long-term returns over the last 30 years for buy-and-hold investors, even after rough decades. Capturing those returns requires patience, not chart-pattern recognition.

### 4. Fundamental analysis is more learnable from public information

Annual reports, audited financial statements, regulatory filings, and management calls are all publicly available for free or low cost. Technical-trading edges depend on speed, infrastructure, and discipline that retail investors rarely match against institutional traders.

## When the two combine

The strict either-or framing is overstated. Many real-world investors combine the two.

A common combination:

- **Use fundamental analysis to choose what to buy**. Pick companies whose business you understand, whose financials are strong, and whose price is reasonable.
- **Use technical analysis to fine-tune entry and exit timing**. Within companies you have already approved fundamentally, watching for support, resistance, or trend signals can improve the price you actually pay.

This is sometimes called the "**fundamentally pick, technically time**" approach. It is more work than either pure approach but can be productive for engaged investors.

The opposite combination, "**technically pick, fundamentally hope**" (buying based on a chart pattern and then hoping the company turns out to be good), is much weaker and usually loses money.

## What technical analysis actually looks at

For completeness, a brief tour of the technical toolkit.

- **Trend identification**. Is the price in an uptrend, downtrend, or sideways range? Tools like moving averages help quantify this.
- **Support and resistance**. Price levels where the share has historically struggled to fall below or rise above.
- **Momentum indicators**. Tools like RSI (Relative Strength Index) and MACD aim to measure how fast price is moving and whether the move is overextended.
- **Volume analysis**. Whether price moves are accompanied by high or low trading volume, which can validate or undermine the move.
- **Chart patterns**. Configurations like head-and-shoulders, double tops, ascending triangles, and so on, which technicians believe foreshadow specific price moves.

The category on [Technical Analysis](/docs/category/technical-analysis) elsewhere on this site goes deeper for those interested.

## Common misconceptions

A few worth flagging.

**"Technical analysis is mathematical and therefore objective."** The math is mathematical, but the pattern interpretation, indicator selection, and timeframe selection are all judgement calls. Two technicians looking at the same chart often disagree.

**"Fundamental analysis is too slow for the modern market."** It is slower than chart-watching, but the multi-year time horizon is exactly what compensates for the slow pace. The Pakistani investors who built wealth over decades did it slowly.

**"You need a math degree for technical analysis."** Most chart-based approaches require basic arithmetic and pattern recognition. Quant trading with statistical models does require advanced math, but that is a much narrower discipline than technical analysis as commonly practised.

**"Fundamental analysis cannot help you in a crash."** Fundamental analysis cannot prevent a crash, but it can prevent you from selling great businesses at panic prices, which is where most retail crash losses come from. The page on [Investor Psychology](/docs/investing-101/investor-psychology) covers this directly.

<Callout type="tip" title="Pick one and master it first">
  Trying to learn both fundamental and technical analysis at the same
  time tends to leave you weak at both. Pick the one that suits your
  intended time horizon, master it over a year or two, and only then
  decide whether to add the other as a complement.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Fundamental analysis studies the business; technical analysis studies the price chart.
- Time horizons differ: fundamental is years, technical is days to weeks.
- Most Pakistani retail investors should lean fundamental, both because of structural market features and because long-term compounding rewards patience.
- The two can be combined in the "fundamentally pick, technically time" pattern; the opposite combination tends to lose money.
- Pick one and master it first before trying the other.
