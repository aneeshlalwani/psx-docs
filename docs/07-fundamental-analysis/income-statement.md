# Income Statement

Where the [balance sheet](/docs/fundamental-analysis/balance-sheet) is a snapshot at a single moment, the **Income Statement** (officially the **Statement of Profit or Loss**) tells the story of the last 12 months. It starts at the top with the money the company earned, walks line by line through what it spent, and ends at the bottom with the profit or loss that flows into reserves on the balance sheet. For investors, this is where the question "is this business actually making money?" gets answered.

## Four core concepts

Before diving into line items, the four building blocks of any income statement.

- **Revenue**: total money received from selling goods or services.
- **Expenses**: what the company spent to earn that revenue.
- **Profit**: revenue minus expenses, when positive.
- **Loss**: revenue minus expenses, when negative.

A worked example: Toyota Indus Motor sold a car for PKR 36 lakh (revenue). The cost to build the car was PKR 34 lakh (expense). Revenue minus expense is PKR 2 lakh (profit). If Toyota had to discount the car to PKR 32 lakh because of weak demand, the same PKR 34 lakh cost minus PKR 32 lakh revenue is a loss of PKR 2 lakh.

The income statement scales this up across all units sold and all expenses incurred during the year.

## The standard layout

A typical Pakistani income statement has the following structure, top to bottom. Numbers in brackets are subtractions.

```
Revenue from contracts with customers       PKR XX
Cost of sales                              (PKR XX)
─────────────────────────────────────────
Gross profit                                PKR XX

Distribution expenses                      (PKR XX)
Administrative expenses                    (PKR XX)
Other operating expenses                   (PKR XX)
Other income                                PKR XX
─────────────────────────────────────────
Operating profit (EBIT)                     PKR XX

Finance cost                               (PKR XX)
─────────────────────────────────────────
Profit before taxation (PBT)                PKR XX

Taxation                                   (PKR XX)
─────────────────────────────────────────
Profit after taxation (PAT) / Net income    PKR XX

Earnings per share (EPS)                    PKR X.XX
```

Anything in brackets is a deduction. The line totals are the running balance after each deduction.

## Walking the lines

### Revenue from contracts with customers

The first line. Total money earned from the company's normal business operations. For Toyota Indus, this is car sales plus spare-parts sales. For a fertiliser company, it is sales of urea and DAP. For a bank, it is interest and fee income.

This line specifically excludes investment income and other non-core gains. Those appear later as "other income".

### Cost of sales (COGS)

Sometimes called **Cost of Goods Sold**. The direct cost of producing what was sold. For a manufacturer:

- Raw materials consumed.
- Direct labour (factory workers).
- Direct manufacturing overhead (factory utilities, factory depreciation).

COGS specifically excludes selling, marketing, administrative, finance, and tax costs. Those come later.

The note for COGS breaks it into raw material consumption, direct labour, fuel and power, repairs and maintenance, depreciation on factory assets, and so on.

### Gross profit

```
Gross profit = Revenue - Cost of sales
```

The first profitability checkpoint. Gross profit tells you how much margin the company has on its core production activity, before any selling, administrative, or financing costs. Gross profit margin (gross profit divided by revenue) is one of the most useful comparison numbers across companies in the same industry.

A Pakistani cement company with 30 percent gross margin and a peer with 18 percent gross margin are clearly differentiated on production efficiency or pricing power.

### Distribution expenses

The cost of getting the product to customers. Includes:

- Sales force salaries and commissions.
- Marketing and advertising.
- Freight and delivery.
- Distribution warehousing.
- Sales-related travel.

This is a large line for consumer-goods companies and a small one for B2B-only manufacturers.

### Administrative expenses

The cost of running the corporate office. Includes:

- Head-office salaries.
- Office rent.
- Audit fees, legal fees, professional services.
- Corporate IT and software.
- Director fees and corporate-governance costs.

Administrative expenses tend to be more stable year-to-year than other lines. Sudden growth here often signals corporate-overhead bloat.

### Other operating expenses

A residual category for operating expenses that do not fit cleanly into distribution or administrative. Common items:

- **Workers' welfare fund (WWF)** and **workers' profit participation fund (WPPF)** contributions, mandated by Pakistani labour law and applied as a percentage of profit before tax.
- **Provisions** for doubtful trade debts.
- **Provisions** for slow-moving inventory.
- **Loss on disposal of assets**.

The notes break this line into specific sub-categories.

### Other income

Income from sources other than the company's normal operating activity. Common items:

- **Bank deposit interest** earned on cash sitting in the bank.
- **Returns on treasury bills** and government securities.
- **Mutual fund distributions** received on units the company holds.
- **Realised and unrealised gains** on investment portfolios.
- **Gains on disposal of property, plant, and equipment**.
- **Rental income** from leased properties.

For pure operating companies, "other income" is usually a small percentage of total. For investment-heavy companies, it can be substantial. A worked example: Toyota Indus's other income comes mostly from bank deposits and treasury bills, plus mutual fund unit returns; it is meaningful but not dominant.

### Operating profit (EBIT)

```
EBIT = Gross profit - Operating expenses + Other income
```

EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) is the profit from running the business, before financing decisions and tax. It is the cleanest measure of operating performance because it removes the effects of how the company is financed (lots of debt vs little debt) and how much tax it pays (which depends on jurisdiction and timing).

EBIT is the input to the [Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)](/docs/fundamental-analysis/roce) and [Interest Coverage Ratio](/docs/fundamental-analysis/interest-coverage-ratio) calculations.

### Realised vs unrealised gains

A nuance worth flagging. **Unrealised gain** is a profit that exists only on paper.

Worked example: a company bought a plot of land in 2010 for PKR 20 lakh. The market value is now PKR 30 lakh. The PKR 10 lakh appreciation is unrealised; it shows up only if the company sells the land. Once sold, it becomes a realised gain.

Pakistani income statements distinguish between the two for marketable investments. Unrealised mark-to-market gains on investments held for trading typically appear in "other income" (or a separate fair-value line); realised gains appear when the investment is actually sold.

For investors, realised gains are cash that has actually arrived. Unrealised gains can disappear if the underlying asset's price falls. Treat them with corresponding skepticism.

### Finance cost

Interest paid on debt, plus related charges. Common items in the notes:

- **Interest on long-term loans** (including SBP refinancing schemes).
- **Interest on short-term borrowings** (running finance, working capital lines).
- **Bank charges** (service charges, transfer fees, account maintenance).
- **Lease finance charges** (under IFRS 16 for capitalised leases).
- **Foreign exchange losses** on FX-denominated debt, for companies with USD or EUR loans.

A company with rising finance cost relative to EBIT is becoming more leveraged or facing higher rates. Both are worth watching.

### Profit before taxation (PBT)

```
PBT = EBIT - Finance cost
```

The profit after subtracting financing costs but before tax.

### Taxation

The tax expense for the year. This is more nuanced than it looks because Pakistani corporate tax has both **current tax** (the portion actually owed and payable for the year) and **deferred tax** (timing differences that will affect future periods).

The note breaks the total tax expense into current and deferred components. Effective tax rate (tax expense divided by PBT) tells you what percentage the company is actually paying. The Pakistani statutory corporate tax rate is around 29 percent in recent years, with super tax additions for very large companies and some sector-specific rates.

A company with an effective tax rate consistently below the statutory rate has tax-saving structures (carry-forward losses, exemptions, R&D incentives). A company with an effective rate consistently above is paying additional levies (super tax, minimum turnover tax) on top of regular corporate tax.

### Profit after taxation (PAT)

```
PAT = PBT - Taxation
```

The bottom line. This is what flows to retained earnings on the balance sheet and what the company can decide to distribute as dividends or retain for reinvestment.

### Earnings per share (EPS)

```
EPS = PAT / Weighted-average number of shares outstanding
```

The per-share version of the bottom line. EPS is the input to the [Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio](/docs/fundamental-analysis/pe-ratio).

Pakistani income statements typically show **basic EPS** and **diluted EPS** separately (where dilution accounts for any convertible securities or stock options that could create more shares).

## How to read the income statement

A practical first-pass approach.

1. **Top line growth**. Has revenue grown vs the prior year? Is the growth rate stable across multiple years?
2. **Gross margin**. Stable, expanding, or compressing? A compressing gross margin while revenue grows can signal price pressure or input cost inflation.
3. **Operating margin**. Compute EBIT divided by revenue. This tells you operating efficiency including all overheads.
4. **Finance cost as a share of EBIT**. Computes loosely to the [Interest Coverage Ratio](/docs/fundamental-analysis/interest-coverage-ratio). Above one-third is concerning.
5. **Effective tax rate**. PAT divided by PBT, subtracted from 1. Compare against the statutory rate.
6. **EPS growth**. Year-over-year. The single number summarises whether the company is becoming more profitable per share over time.

## Cross-checking with the cash flow statement

Income-statement profit is not the same as cash. The page on the [Cash Flow Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement) covers why. Always cross-check that PAT is translating into operating cash flow. If PAT is growing but operating cash flow is flat or declining, the profit is being absorbed by working-capital growth or paper gains and is not actually accumulating in the business.

<Callout type="tip" title="Five years, side by side">
  The single most useful exercise for understanding a company's
  income statement is laying five years of statements side by side
  and watching the trends in revenue, gross margin, operating
  margin, and EPS. One year alone can be misleading. Five years
  show whether the business is getting stronger or weaker.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- The income statement starts with revenue and works down through COGS, operating expenses, finance cost, and tax to arrive at net income.
- Gross profit shows production efficiency; operating profit (EBIT) shows operating efficiency; PAT shows the bottom line.
- Other income captures non-operating sources (interest on deposits, investment returns, mutual fund distributions).
- Realised and unrealised gains are not the same; treat unrealised gains with care.
- Pakistani income statements distinguish current and deferred tax; effective tax rate is a useful comparison number.
- Always cross-check PAT against operating cash flow; profit that does not become cash is suspect.
