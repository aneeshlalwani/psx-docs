# Interest Coverage Ratio

A company can have a low debt-to-equity ratio and still struggle to pay its interest bill if operating profit is volatile. A company can have a high debt-to-equity ratio and pay interest comfortably if profits are stable and ample. The ratio that captures this is the **Interest Coverage Ratio**, also called **Times Interest Earned (TIE)**. It answers a simple question: how many times over can the company cover its interest expense from operating profit?

In Pakistan, where interest rates have swung sharply with the State Bank's policy rate (peaking near 22 percent and then falling), interest coverage matters even more than in stable-rate economies. A company that is fine when the policy rate is at 12 percent can be in serious distress at 22 percent if it has a lot of floating-rate debt.

## The formula

```
Times Interest Earned (TIE) = EBIT / Interest expense
```

EBIT is Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (operating profit). Interest expense is the finance cost line on the income statement.

A worked example. Two Pakistani companies, A and B, each took a PKR 1 crore loan at 10 percent annual interest. Each owes PKR 10 lakh per year in interest.

- **Company A** earns EBIT of PKR 50 lakh.
- **Company B** earns EBIT of PKR 15 lakh.

```
TIE(A) = 50 / 10 = 5
TIE(B) = 15 / 10 = 1.5
```

The interpretation:

- Company A's operating profit can cover interest **5 times over**. There is plenty of cushion.
- Company B's operating profit can cover interest only **1.5 times over**. Tight; if EBIT drops 33 percent, interest is barely covered. Below 1.5 means EBIT does not cover interest at all.

## Reading the number

Rough rules of thumb:

- **Below 1**: the company cannot cover interest from operations at all. Distress; the company is funding interest from cash reserves or new debt. Walk away.
- **1 to 1.5**: the company barely covers interest. A bad operating year tips it into distress. Risky.
- **1.5 to 3**: workable but not comfortable. Often acceptable for capital-intensive or regulated industries.
- **3 to 5**: comfortable. The default safe range for most operating companies.
- **Above 5**: very strong. The company has substantial buffer.

The classic rule given in beginner finance courses is **invest only in companies with TIE of 3 or higher** so they can cover interest comfortably even in a difficult year. This is a reasonable starting threshold for retail investors looking at Pakistani companies.

## Why TIE matters more in Pakistan

Pakistani interest rates have been volatile. Over the last several years, the State Bank policy rate has moved between roughly 7 percent and a peak of 22 percent. For companies with floating-rate debt, this swing translated into dramatically higher interest expense over short periods. A company with TIE of 5 at a 10 percent rate could see its TIE fall sharply if rates jumped and EBIT did not.

Practical implications:

- **For floating-rate borrowers**, TIE is a moving target. Stress-test by computing what TIE would be if interest rates rose 5 percentage points. If TIE drops below 2 in that scenario, the company's debt structure is fragile.
- **For fixed-rate borrowers**, TIE is more stable. The interest expense is locked in, so only EBIT volatility moves the ratio.
- **Cross-check the loan note** for the proportion of fixed vs floating debt. Most Pakistani long-term loans are floating-rate (linked to KIBOR plus a spread), which exposes earnings to rate cycles.

## Reading TIE alongside debt-to-equity

A company's leverage profile only makes sense if you read solvency and interest coverage together.

Four combinations:

| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| High D/E + High TIE | Highly leveraged but earning enough to comfortably service the debt. Generally safe, but watch interest-rate exposure |
| High D/E + Low TIE | Highly leveraged and struggling to service the debt. **Danger.** |
| Low D/E + High TIE | Conservatively financed and easily servicing the debt. Safe |
| Low D/E + Low TIE | Conservatively financed but operating profit is weak. Operating-business problem, not a debt problem |

The single combination to walk away from is high D/E plus low TIE. That is the classic distressed-company profile.

## A second worked example: rate cycle stress test

A Pakistani manufacturer with the following profile:

- Long-term loans: PKR 100 crore at floating KIBOR plus 3 percent.
- KIBOR (3-month) at the time of this snapshot: 12 percent. So total rate is 15 percent.
- Annual interest expense: PKR 100 crore × 15 percent = PKR 15 crore.
- Annual EBIT: PKR 60 crore.

```
TIE = 60 / 15 = 4
```

Comfortable. Now stress-test what happens if KIBOR jumps to 17 percent (total rate 20 percent):

- New annual interest expense: PKR 100 crore × 20 percent = PKR 20 crore.
- Same EBIT (or potentially lower if rates rise during a downturn).

```
TIE (stressed) = 60 / 20 = 3
```

Still acceptable. Now imagine the worst case: rates spike to KIBOR 19 percent (total 22 percent) and EBIT drops 25 percent because of the same recession that drove rates up:

- Annual interest expense: PKR 22 crore.
- EBIT: PKR 60 crore × 75 percent = PKR 45 crore.

```
TIE (stressed worse) = 45 / 22 = 2.05
```

Still above 1.5, but tight. A company that scores 4 on TIE today can be at 2 in a stress scenario if its debt is floating-rate. Always factor in the rate cycle.

## Pakistani sector context

Approximate TIE ranges for typical Pakistani companies in healthy years:

| Sector | Typical TIE range |
|---|---|
| Consumer goods (FMCG) | 8 to 30+ |
| Pharmaceutical | 8 to 20 |
| Banks (different metrics generally used) | n/a |
| Cement | 3 to 10 |
| Fertiliser | 4 to 12 |
| Power generation | 2 to 5 (often regulated) |
| Textiles | 1.5 to 5 |
| Heavy engineering | 3 to 8 |

Asset-light, low-debt sectors (consumer goods, pharma) tend to have very high TIE. Capital-intensive sectors (textiles, power) often run with lower TIE. Always compare against peers in the same sector.

<Callout type="warning" title="A falling TIE trend is a major flag">
  TIE that is dropping year-over-year (5 → 4 → 3 → 2 over four
  years) usually means either operating profit is weakening or
  interest costs are rising. Both eventually lead to debt-service
  pressure. Companies in this trend are worth treating as
  watch-list at best, regardless of their other ratios.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Interest Coverage Ratio (TIE) = EBIT divided by interest expense.
- Below 1.5 is dangerous; below 1 is distress; above 3 is comfortable; above 5 is strong.
- In Pakistan's volatile-rate environment, stress-test TIE for a 5-percentage-point rate rise to assess fragility.
- Most Pakistani long-term debt is floating-rate (KIBOR plus spread), exposing earnings to cycle.
- Read TIE alongside debt-to-equity; the dangerous combination is high D/E plus low TIE.
- Falling TIE trend is a leading indicator of debt-service pressure.
