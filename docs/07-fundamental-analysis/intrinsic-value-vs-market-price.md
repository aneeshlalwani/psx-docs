# Intrinsic Value vs. Market Price

Everything in this category, from balance-sheet line items through P/E ratios through capital-allocation analysis, ultimately serves a single decision rule. **You buy when the intrinsic value of a share is meaningfully above the market price. You avoid (or sell) when it is meaningfully below.** This page is the rule, plus the apple-shop analogy that makes it intuitive, plus a practical caveat about how precise intrinsic-value estimates ever actually are.

## The rule

```
If Intrinsic Value > Market Price → Stock is undervalued → Consider buying
If Intrinsic Value < Market Price → Stock is overvalued → Avoid or sell
If Intrinsic Value ≈ Market Price → Stock is fairly priced → No edge either way
```

The whole of fundamental analysis is about getting a better estimate of the left side of these inequalities than the market currently has on the right side. If your estimate is right and the market's is wrong, you profit when prices eventually converge to value.

## The apple-shop analogy

The cleanest way to internalise this concept.

Apples are nutritious. You could probably list a dozen genuine health benefits: good for heart, good for digestion, good for brain, helps with weight loss, contains vitamins and fibre, and so on.

Now imagine someone says: *"Apples are so beneficial that I'll only sell them at PKR 5,000 per kilo."*

This is absurd. Regardless of how many benefits apples have, PKR 5,000 per kilo makes no sense. The intrinsic value of an apple, given what they cost to produce and what alternatives are available, is somewhere around PKR 150 per kilo. Anything significantly above that price means you should buy something else, even if the original apple really does have all those benefits.

The same logic applies to stocks.

A Pakistani company can be excellent. Best-in-class management, deep moat, growing profits, dominant market share. Genuinely a great business. But if the share is trading at PKR 800 and your careful estimate of intrinsic value is PKR 200, the share is wildly overvalued. The qualities of the business do not change, but the price you are being asked to pay is irrational.

Eventually (the apple analogy continues), reality reasserts itself. Lies cannot last forever. The PKR 800 share will eventually fall to its real intrinsic value of around PKR 200, or close to it (PKR 220 to PKR 230). Investors who waited for that correction will profit. Investors who got caught up in the hype and bought at PKR 800 will take heavy losses.

The point: **the quality of the business is not the same question as the right price for the share**. Both questions matter, but they are separate.

## Why intrinsic value is an estimate

A critical practical caveat. Intrinsic value is **never a precise number**. Anyone who tells you a stock's intrinsic value is exactly PKR 432.17 is overstating the precision of their analysis.

In reality:

- **Different valuation methods** give different intrinsic-value estimates: discounted cash flow, multiple-based comparison, sum-of-parts, asset-based.
- **Different assumptions** within each method give different numbers. Discounted cash flow with a 12 percent discount rate vs 15 percent gives meaningfully different valuations.
- **Future growth rates** are inherently uncertain. A company that grows 15 percent for five years has a different intrinsic value than one that grows 8 percent.

So intrinsic value is best thought of as a **range**, not a point estimate. A careful Pakistani analyst might say "I think this share is worth roughly PKR 250 to PKR 350, depending on whether the new product line works out." That range is more honest than a precise number.

The investment decision then becomes: is the current market price above, below, or inside that range?

## Margin of safety

Benjamin Graham, Warren Buffett's mentor, introduced the concept of the **margin of safety**. It is the buffer between intrinsic value and the price you actually pay.

If you estimate intrinsic value at PKR 300 and the share is trading at PKR 295, that is technically undervalued, but only by 1.5 percent. Your estimate could be off by far more than that. The "margin of safety" is too thin.

If the share is trading at PKR 200 and you estimate intrinsic value at PKR 300, the margin of safety is 33 percent. Even if your estimate is materially off, you are still buying below value.

A common rule: only buy when the share is at least 25 to 35 percent below your estimate of intrinsic value. The margin protects you against your own analytical mistakes.

This is what makes fundamental investing fundamentally different from speculation. Speculators buy and hope. Fundamental investors estimate, demand a margin of safety, and only buy when the market disagrees with them by enough to justify the position.

## How to estimate intrinsic value

A serious answer to this would fill a textbook, but the methods used by practitioners cluster into a few approaches.

### 1. Multiple-based valuation

Apply a sensible P/E multiple, EV/EBITDA multiple, or P/B multiple to the company's current earnings or book value, calibrated to the industry's typical multiples and the company's growth and quality relative to peers.

A simple example. A Pakistani consumer-goods company has EPS of PKR 20. The Pakistani consumer-goods sector typically trades at P/E of 25 to 30. The company is slightly above average on growth and quality, suggesting the upper end of the range.

```
Intrinsic value range = EPS × P/E range
                      = 20 × (25 to 30)
                      = PKR 500 to PKR 600
```

If the share is trading at PKR 380, it is meaningfully below the intrinsic-value range. Worth investigating further.

This approach is fast and easy to apply but depends critically on the multiples being right. For most Pakistani retail investors, this is the practical method.

### 2. Discounted cash flow (DCF)

Estimate the company's future free cash flows for a forecast period (5 to 10 years), then discount them back to present using an appropriate discount rate. Add a terminal value for cash flows beyond the forecast period.

DCF is the conceptually purest method but is sensitive to assumptions about growth, margins, capex, and discount rate. Small changes in inputs produce large changes in output. It is more useful as an input to thinking than as a precise valuation tool.

### 3. Asset-based valuation

For asset-heavy or distressed companies, value the underlying assets directly (real estate at market value, cash and securities at face value, operating equipment at replacement cost) and subtract liabilities.

Less useful for going-concern operating businesses but very useful for special situations.

### 4. Sum-of-parts

For conglomerates with multiple distinct businesses, value each business separately using methods 1, 2, or 3, then sum.

For most Pakistani retail investors, **multiple-based valuation with a margin of safety is the right starting point**. Consult professional valuations for complex situations.

## A practical decision framework

Putting it all together for a Pakistani company you are considering buying.

1. **Run the qualitative checks** (management, moat, capital allocation, disruption risk, related-party transactions) from the [Qualitative Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/qualitative-analysis) page. If any major check fails, walk away.
2. **Read the financial statements** (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement) for the last three to five years.
3. **Compute the major ratios** (liquidity, solvency, profitability, efficiency) and compare against industry peers.
4. **Estimate an intrinsic-value range** using multiple-based valuation as the primary tool.
5. **Compare market price** to your range. If the price is at least 25 to 35 percent below the bottom of your range, you have a margin of safety.
6. **Re-check qualitative judgement**. Is there a fundamental reason the market is pricing the share so low? Sometimes the market is right and you have missed something.
7. **If everything still checks out, buy**. If not, wait. The market presents new opportunities every quarter.

Fundamental investing is mostly waiting. Most of the time, prices are roughly fair, and the right action is no action. The few times when prices are clearly wrong are when the discipline pays off.

<Callout type="tip" title="Trust ranges, not precision">
  When you do your first serious fundamental analysis of a Pakistani
  company, force yourself to think in ranges. Write down your
  estimate of intrinsic value as "PKR X to PKR Y", not a single
  number. The honesty about uncertainty is what protects you from
  overconfidence in either direction.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- The fundamental rule: buy when intrinsic value is meaningfully above market price, avoid otherwise.
- Intrinsic value is an estimate, expressed as a range, not a precise number.
- Margin of safety (25 to 35 percent below intrinsic value) protects against analytical errors.
- The apple-shop analogy: a great business at the wrong price is still a bad investment.
- Multiple-based valuation is the practical method for most retail investors; DCF and asset-based methods complement it.
- Most of the time, prices are fair and the right action is no action. Discipline pays off in the rare moments when prices are clearly wrong.
