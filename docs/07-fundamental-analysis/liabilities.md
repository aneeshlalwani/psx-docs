# Liabilities

If you bought a car on credit but stopped showing the loan on your personal balance sheet, you would be lying to yourself about your real financial position. The same logic applies to companies. Every Pakistani listed company has obligations to others (banks, suppliers, employees, government, shareholders), and those obligations sit on the **liabilities side** of the balance sheet. Reading the liabilities section carefully tells you how exposed the company is to outside creditors and how much working capital it needs to keep flowing in to service its commitments.

This page walks through both **non-current liabilities** (due after one year) and **current liabilities** (due within one year), with worked Pakistani examples.

## The current vs non-current split

A loan or obligation is classified as current if it falls due within the next 12 months. Anything beyond 12 months is non-current.

Worked example: a company takes a PKR 36 lakh loan repayable over 3 years at PKR 1 lakh per month.

- **Year 1 balance sheet**: PKR 12 lakh in current liabilities (next 12 months of payments) and PKR 24 lakh in non-current liabilities (the remaining 24 months).
- **Year 2 balance sheet**: again PKR 12 lakh in current and PKR 12 lakh in non-current. (One year of payments has been made; two years remain, with the next 12 months due currently.)
- **Year 3 balance sheet**: the remaining PKR 12 lakh is now all current; non-current is zero.

This same split logic applies to every long-term obligation.

## Non-current liabilities

Debts due after one financial year. The most common Pakistani line items.

### Long-term loans

The largest non-current liability for most operating companies. Bank loans and term-finance arrangements taken to fund capital projects, acquisitions, or major working capital cycles.

The notes typically break long-term loans into:

- **Loans under the State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) refinancing schemes**. SBP runs subsidised refinancing facilities (LTFF for export-oriented manufacturing, TERF for plant and machinery, financing schemes for renewable energy, agriculture, and exports). Loans under these schemes typically carry concessional rates and are visible as a distinct sub-line.
- **Long-term financing from commercial banks** at market rates.
- **Loans for specific purposes** like the "Loan for Payment of Wages and Salaries to Employees" scheme that was launched by SBP during the COVID period to help employers preserve jobs.
- **Term Finance Certificates (TFCs)** or **Sukuk** issued by the company itself.

The note discloses the lender, the rate of interest, the security (collateral), the repayment schedule, and any covenants attached.

### Deferred revenue

Money the company has **received in advance** for goods or services it has not yet delivered. The opposite of accrued revenue (which is income earned but not yet received).

Worked example: a software company sells a 3-year support contract for PKR 30 lakh, paid up front. The PKR 30 lakh is cash in hand, but the company still owes 3 years of support. The PKR 30 lakh sits on the balance sheet as deferred revenue, and is recognised as income over the 3 years as the company delivers the support.

The portion to be earned within the next 12 months is in current liabilities (current portion of deferred revenue); the rest is in non-current.

For Pakistani manufacturers, deferred revenue is usually small. For service-heavy or subscription-heavy businesses, it can be substantial.

### Deferred taxation

The flip side of the deferred-tax asset on the asset page. A **deferred tax liability** is tax that will become payable in future periods due to current-period timing differences.

A company can have a deferred tax asset in one year and a deferred tax liability in the next, or vice versa, simply because the timing pattern between accounting profit and taxable profit changes year to year. For most retail investors, this is a footnote-level detail.

### Long-term lease liabilities (IFRS 16)

Under IFRS 16, leases that meet certain criteria must be capitalised on the balance sheet: the right-of-use asset on one side and the lease liability on the other. The non-current portion of the lease liability sits in non-current liabilities; the current portion is in current liabilities.

This is a relatively new line for Pakistani financial statements (post-2019 adoption) and can be material for retail or distribution-heavy businesses with many leased properties.

### Employee benefit obligations

Long-term provisions for staff retirement benefits: pension fund commitments, gratuity, and other defined-benefit obligations. The actuarial calculation behind these is in the notes; the headline number on the balance sheet is the present value of the company's expected future obligations.

## Current liabilities

Debts due within the next 12 months. The most common items.

### Current portion of long-term loans

The portion of long-term debt due to be repaid within the next year. As shown in the worked example earlier, every long-term loan has both a non-current and a current portion that shifts as repayment progresses.

### Current portion of deferred revenue

Same logic on the deferred revenue side. Of the total deferred revenue balance, the portion to be earned (and recognised as income) within the next 12 months sits as a current liability.

### Trade and other payables

Money the company owes to others. The single largest current-liability line for most Pakistani manufacturers. The note breaks this into sub-categories:

- **Trade creditors**. Suppliers who delivered goods to the company on credit. This is the mirror image of trade debts: where the company is a buyer instead of a seller.
- **Accrued liabilities**. Expenses incurred but not yet paid: pending utility bills, rent due, contractor invoices in process.
- **Tax dues**. Income tax, sales tax, withholding tax, customs duty, and other government levies payable.
- **Government levies and contributions**. EOBI contributions, social security, workers' welfare fund contributions.
- **Payments to other companies**. Inter-company payables.

A growing trade-payables balance can mean two things, depending on context. In a healthy company, it can signal stronger negotiating power with suppliers (you can take longer to pay because suppliers want your business). In a stressed company, it can signal that the company is stretching payments because it does not have the cash.

### Short-term borrowings

Working capital lines from banks. Pakistani manufacturers typically run **running finance** facilities (an overdraft-style arrangement where the company can draw and repay flexibly within a credit limit) to manage day-to-day cash needs. The drawn balance at year-end shows as short-term borrowings.

Watch for short-term borrowings that have grown substantially year over year without commensurate revenue growth; this can signal that the company is funding losses or working capital deterioration with bank credit.

### Unclaimed dividends

Dividends the company has declared and "issued" but that shareholders have not yet collected.

Why does this happen? Some shareholders prefer to receive dividends by physical cheque rather than direct bank deposit. Cheques get lost, are sent to old addresses, or are simply not banked by the recipient. Either way, the company has issued the cheque (so it has fulfilled its declaration obligation), but the cash has not actually left its bank account. The unbanked amount sits on the balance sheet as a liability because, eventually, the shareholder may turn up to claim it.

The total unclaimed dividend balance is usually small. Pakistani securities law requires unclaimed dividends to be transferred to a centrally administered fund after a defined period (typically several years), so it does not stay on the balance sheet forever.

### Advances from customers and dealers

Money the company has received in advance from customers for goods or services that have not yet been delivered. The current-liability mirror of deferred revenue.

For Pakistani automakers, this can be significant. Customers booking a car often pay an advance; the company holds the advance as a liability until the car is delivered. The pattern of advances tells you about future committed sales; large advances suggest a strong order book, while shrinking advances can signal demand is softening.

## How to read the liabilities section

A practical first-pass approach.

1. **Total liabilities, divided into non-current and current**. The split tells you the company's funding structure: long-term debt-heavy vs working-capital-heavy.
2. **Total liabilities relative to total equity**. The basic [debt-to-equity check](/docs/fundamental-analysis/solvency-ratios). Above 1 means more debt than equity; the threshold for acceptability depends on industry.
3. **Long-term loans**: who is the lender, what is the rate, and what is the repayment schedule? The notes have all of this.
4. **Trade payables vs trade receivables**. Compare the two. A company with high payables and low receivables is borrowing from its suppliers (good leverage). A company with high receivables and low payables is lending to its customers (a use of working capital).
5. **Year-on-year changes**. Sudden growth in any line warrants explanation. The directors' report and notes usually disclose the reasons.

<Callout type="warning" title="A growing short-term borrowings line is a flag">
  If a company's short-term borrowings have grown 30 to 50 percent
  year-over-year while revenue has grown only modestly, the company
  may be funding deterioration with bank credit. Read the directors'
  report and the cash flow statement carefully before deciding the
  pattern is sustainable.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Liabilities are split into non-current (after one year) and current (within one year); long-term loans straddle both via the current-portion mechanic.
- SBP refinancing schemes (LTFF, TERF, COVID-era wages-and-salaries loans) appear as distinct sub-lines under long-term loans.
- Deferred revenue is cash received in advance for unfulfilled obligations; deferred tax is a timing-difference balance that can sit on either side of the balance sheet.
- Trade and other payables, short-term borrowings, and advances from customers dominate current liabilities.
- Unclaimed dividends are issued but unbanked dividends; usually small.
- Watch the trend in short-term borrowings; rapid growth without revenue growth is a flag.
