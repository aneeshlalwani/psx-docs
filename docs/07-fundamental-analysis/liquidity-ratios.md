# Liquidity Ratios

Imagine you owe PKR 80,000 in monthly bills due in the next four weeks but only have PKR 50,000 in your account. You have a liquidity problem, even if your overall net worth is positive. Companies face the same situation. **Liquidity ratios** are the simple arithmetic that tells you whether a Pakistani company has enough short-term assets to cover its short-term obligations. Three of them, in increasing order of strictness: current ratio, quick ratio, and cash ratio.

## What "liquidity" means

For a company, **liquidity** is the ability to meet short-term obligations as they come due, without disrupting operations. A liquid company can pay its bills on time. An illiquid company has to delay payments, take emergency loans, or sell long-term assets at a discount.

Liquidity is different from **solvency**, which is the ability to meet long-term obligations. A company can be solvent (more assets than liabilities overall) but illiquid (not enough cash to pay this month's bills). Both matter, but liquidity is the more immediate concern.

The page on [Solvency Ratios](/docs/fundamental-analysis/solvency-ratios) covers the long-term side.

## The current ratio

The simplest liquidity measure.

```
Current ratio = Current assets / Current liabilities
```

A worked example: a company has PKR 1 crore in current assets and PKR 80 lakh in current liabilities.

```
Current ratio = 1,00,00,000 / 80,00,000 = 1.25
```

The interpretation: for every PKR 1 of short-term obligation, the company has PKR 1.25 of short-term resources. The company can cover its current liabilities with current assets and have a buffer left over.

### What's a "good" current ratio?

There is no universal answer.

- **Below 1**: the company has fewer current assets than current liabilities. Likely facing or about to face liquidity problems.
- **1.0 to 1.5**: tight but workable, depending on industry and the speed of cash conversion.
- **1.5 to 2.5**: comfortable for most industries.
- **Above 2.5**: very comfortable, but can also signal that too much cash is sitting in current assets without being deployed.

Compare against industry peers. A ratio of 1.2 might be standard for retail (which has fast inventory turnover) and concerning for manufacturing (which has slow turnover).

## The quick ratio (acid-test ratio)

A stricter version of the current ratio. Inventory is excluded because it cannot always be quickly converted to cash; selling stock at full price takes time, and selling at fire-sale prices destroys value.

```
Quick ratio = (Current assets - Inventory) / Current liabilities
```

A worked example: same company has PKR 1 crore in current assets, of which PKR 30 lakh is inventory. Current liabilities are PKR 80 lakh.

```
Quick ratio = (1,00,00,000 - 30,00,000) / 80,00,000 = 70,00,000 / 80,00,000 = 0.875
```

The company has PKR 0.875 of immediately-saleable assets for every PKR 1 of short-term obligation. Below 1 means it cannot meet short-term obligations without selling inventory.

### Why exclude inventory?

In most industries, inventory takes weeks or months to sell at normal prices. If the company has to liquidate inventory urgently to pay bills, it usually accepts steep discounts. The quick ratio asks: setting inventory aside, can the company still pay its bills?

For Pakistani companies in industries with high inventory levels (cement, steel, automobiles, textiles), the quick ratio is often well below the current ratio. For service-oriented businesses with little inventory (banks, IT services, telecom), the two ratios are similar.

### What's a "good" quick ratio?

A common rule of thumb is **above 1**. That means the company can cover all its short-term obligations from non-inventory current assets alone, which is a comfortable position.

For Pakistani manufacturers with high inventory, a quick ratio of 0.7 to 1.0 is often acceptable as long as the inventory is genuinely sellable. For asset-light businesses, the threshold should be higher.

## The cash ratio

The strictest liquidity test. Only counts truly liquid assets: cash and marketable securities (short-term investments that can be sold instantly).

```
Cash ratio = (Cash + Marketable securities) / Current liabilities
```

A worked example: same company has PKR 30 lakh in cash and PKR 20 lakh in marketable securities. Current liabilities are PKR 80 lakh.

```
Cash ratio = (30,00,000 + 20,00,000) / 80,00,000 = 50,00,000 / 80,00,000 = 0.625
```

The company has PKR 0.625 of immediately-spendable resources for every PKR 1 of short-term obligation. If every creditor demanded payment today, the company could cover 62.5 percent of obligations from cash alone.

### What's a "good" cash ratio?

Cash ratios above 1 are unusual and often signal that too much cash is sitting idle. A ratio of 0.3 to 0.7 is common for healthy operating companies. Below 0.2 is tight.

The cash ratio matters more to **creditors** (who want to know they will be paid even in a crisis) than to **investors** (who want to know that cash is being deployed productively). A company with a sky-high cash ratio is safer but is potentially leaving returns on the table.

## A practical comparison

For the same Pakistani company in the worked example:

| Ratio | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Current ratio | 1.25 | Comfortable; current assets cover current liabilities with a small buffer. |
| Quick ratio | 0.875 | Tight; cannot cover all current liabilities without selling inventory. |
| Cash ratio | 0.625 | Reasonable; has substantial cash and marketable securities. |

Reading the three together: the company has working capital (mainly inventory and receivables) that gives it adequate overall liquidity, but it is not flush with cash. This is the typical profile of a healthy operating manufacturer.

## How to read liquidity ratios in practice

Two important framings.

### Compare against industry

A liquidity ratio means little in isolation. The same numerical value can be excellent in one industry and worrying in another.

- **Banks** and financial institutions have completely different liquidity profiles (most current assets are loans receivable, most current liabilities are customer deposits) and use specialised liquidity metrics.
- **Retail** and FMCG companies have fast cash conversion cycles; ratios in the low-to-mid range are normal.
- **Heavy manufacturing** has slower cash conversion and tends to operate with higher ratios.
- **Construction** has long project cycles and very industry-specific ratios.

Look up the average liquidity ratios for the industry you are evaluating before declaring any specific number good or bad.

### Watch the trend

A single year's ratio is less informative than a five-year trend. Three patterns to watch:

- **Stable or improving**: liquidity is not deteriorating; safe.
- **Slow deterioration**: ratio is dropping by 0.05 to 0.10 each year. Worth investigating; the company may be growing too fast for its working capital.
- **Sudden drop**: ratio falls 30 percent or more in a year. Major flag; usually associated with an acquisition, large dividend, or operational shock.

## Liquidity ratios and the cash conversion cycle

Liquidity ratios are static snapshots. The dynamic version is the **cash conversion cycle**: how many days it takes for a rupee spent on inventory to turn back into a rupee in the bank account from a customer.

```
Cash conversion cycle = Days Inventory + Days Receivables - Days Payables
```

A company with strong liquidity ratios but a lengthening cash conversion cycle is on a path to weakening liquidity. The page on [Days Sales Inventory (DSI)](/docs/fundamental-analysis/days-sales-inventory) covers part of this.

<Callout type="warning" title="Liquidity stress is often the early warning">
  Bankrupt companies almost always show liquidity-ratio
  deterioration well before they show profit-ratio deterioration.
  By the time a company reports a loss, the working-capital crunch
  has usually been visible in liquidity ratios for a year or more.
  Track liquidity ratios as your early-warning signal.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Liquidity ratios measure short-term ability to pay bills.
- Three ratios in increasing strictness: current ratio (all current assets), quick ratio (excludes inventory), cash ratio (cash and marketable securities only).
- Always compare against industry peers; "good" depends on the business.
- Watch the trend over five years; deterioration is more informative than absolute level.
- Liquidity stress is often visible before profit deterioration; treat it as an early-warning signal.
