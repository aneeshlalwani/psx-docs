# Non-Current Assets

The **Non-Current Assets** section of the balance sheet is where the company's long-term resources live: factory buildings, machinery, vehicles, computers, software, long-term loans given to employees, and longer-term investments. For asset-heavy industries like cement, fertiliser, automobiles, and textiles, this is the largest section of the balance sheet and often the strongest indicator of whether the company is investing in its future.

This page walks through the typical line items using the kind of structure you would see in a Toyota Indus Motor or similar Pakistani manufacturer's annual report.

## Why the balance sheet matters before investing

Before reading line items, a quick reframe of why this section matters at all.

The balance sheet tells you the company's **financial health**: how much it owes, how much it owns, and whether the assets exceed the liabilities by enough margin that the company will not go bankrupt. A company with substantially more assets than liabilities is structurally safer than one running close to the line.

Within assets, the **non-current section is where productive capacity lives**. A textile mill without machinery is just a building; a car company without an assembly line is just an office. Non-current assets are what generate revenue. Watching how they grow (or shrink) over time tells you whether management is actually investing in the business's future.

## Tangible vs intangible

Assets come in two physical types.

- **Tangible assets** are things you can touch: land, buildings, vehicles, equipment, machinery, furniture, raw materials.
- **Intangible assets** are not physical: computer software, patents, trademarks, goodwill from acquisitions, brand value, advertising assets in some interpretations.

Both can generate financial benefit for the company, and both appear on the balance sheet (intangibles in their own line item, separate from PP&E).

A company's commercials and advertising campaigns are often economically intangible assets (they create awareness which leads to sales), but accounting standards usually do not let companies put advertising on the balance sheet. They have to be expensed in the year they happen. Real intangibles on the balance sheet are typically computer software licences, purchased patents, trademarks, and goodwill from acquisitions.

## Property, Plant and Equipment (PP&E)

The largest non-current asset for most Pakistani manufacturing companies. PP&E is the tangible operating assets that physically exist where the company operates. Typical sub-items disclosed in the notes:

- **Land** (sometimes called "freehold land" or "leasehold land"). The site the factory or office sits on.
- **Buildings**. Factories, offices, warehouses, employee accommodation.
- **Plant and machinery**. The heavy equipment used in production. For Toyota Indus Motor, this would be the assembly-line equipment that builds cars.
- **Vehicles**. Cars and trucks the company owns for operational use (deliveries, employee transport, executive vehicles).
- **Computers and IT equipment**. Servers, PCs, networking equipment.
- **Tools** and small equipment. Wrenches, pliers, gauges, measuring instruments.
- **Furniture and fixtures**. Desks, chairs, fittings.

Even small items get listed because Pakistani accounting standards require comprehensive recording of capital assets. The notes to the financial statements break this into a detailed schedule showing **opening balance, additions during the year, disposals, depreciation, and closing balance** for each category.

### Reading the PP&E note

A typical PP&E note in a Pakistani annual report has columns for each sub-category and rows for: cost at the start of the year, additions, disposals, cost at year-end, accumulated depreciation, depreciation charge for the year, and net book value. This is a lot to look at, but the most useful summary view is:

- **Net book value at year-end**: what the assets are worth on the books after depreciation.
- **Additions during the year**: capital expenditure, the company's investment in itself.
- **Depreciation charge**: how much the existing PP&E "wore out" this year (in accounting terms).

If additions are consistently larger than depreciation over multiple years, the company is **net investing** in productive capacity. If depreciation is consistently larger than additions, the company is **harvesting** without reinvesting (a worry in capital-intensive industries).

### Numbers in '000 or in million

Pakistani annual reports almost always express financial-statement figures in thousands ("Rupees in '000" at the top) or millions ("Rupees in million"). A line saying "1,650,642" with "Rupees in '000" at the top means **PKR 1,650,642 thousand**, or roughly **PKR 165 crore** (PKR 1.65 billion). Always check the unit notation at the top of each statement.

## Intangible assets

The second-line non-current asset for most companies. Examples:

- **Computer software**. Licences for ERP systems, CAD software, accounting platforms.
- **Trademarks** and brand rights.
- **Patents** for proprietary technology.
- **Goodwill** from acquisitions. When a parent acquires a subsidiary at a price above the subsidiary's book value, the difference shows up as goodwill.
- **Customer relationships and other recognised intangibles** in some specialised acquisitions.

Intangibles are amortised over their useful lives, similar to how PP&E is depreciated. The amortisation hits the income statement annually.

For most Pakistani manufacturing companies, intangibles are a small share of total non-current assets. For technology and pharmaceutical companies, they can be larger. For companies that have done major acquisitions, goodwill can be substantial; large goodwill on the balance sheet warrants attention because it is the line most likely to be impaired (written down) if the acquisition does not perform.

## Long-term loans and advances

You might wonder why a company that builds cars or sells fertiliser has any loans on its balance sheet as an asset. The most common explanation: **internal loans to employees**, formalised as company assets.

A company might lend an employee PKR 5 lakh as a house loan, repayable over 5 years at a modest interest rate. From the company's view, that PKR 5 lakh is an asset (it will come back, potentially with some return). The portion due after one year sits in non-current assets; the portion due within a year is in current assets.

Other examples include long-term advances to suppliers (e.g., for a multi-year supply arrangement) and long-term advances to subsidiaries.

If the line is large relative to the company's size, the notes explain who the loans are to. Watch for related-party-loan patterns; the [Qualitative Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/qualitative-analysis) page covers why insider loans at off-market rates are a red flag.

## Long-term deposits

Money the company has parked somewhere for more than a year. Examples:

- **Long-term fixed deposits** with banks.
- **Long-term investments in equity securities** (other companies' shares held as long-term investments, not for trading).
- **Long-term investments in government securities** (sukuk, Pakistan Investment Bonds with longer maturities).
- **Real estate held for investment** rather than operations.

Long-term deposits are a sign that the company has surplus cash that it has chosen to lock away rather than reinvest into operations or distribute as dividends. This is not always bad, but it raises questions: why is the cash not being deployed into the operating business? The answer may be that the business has limited growth opportunities (in which case dividends might serve shareholders better) or that management is being prudent about timing future investments.

## Deferred tax (asset side)

This is a common source of confusion. Deferred tax appears on **both** sides of the balance sheet, depending on the situation.

A **Deferred Tax Asset** arises when the company has effectively **overpaid tax** in the current year (or has tax-deductible items that will provide future tax relief). The expectation is that this overpayment translates into reduced tax in future years.

Worked example: a company owes PKR 100 in tax for the year but, due to timing differences in depreciation, has paid PKR 150. The PKR 50 difference is a deferred tax asset; it will reduce next year's tax bill.

A **Deferred Tax Liability** arises in the opposite case: tax that will become payable in future periods due to current-period timing differences.

Both versions show up as separate balance-sheet lines. They appear on the balance sheet because they represent legitimate future economic benefits or obligations, not because they are immediately convertible to cash.

For most retail investors, deferred tax is a footnote-level detail that does not change the investment decision. For activist investors or careful analysts, large or growing deferred tax balances can flag aggressive accounting that is worth understanding.

## Putting it together: a Pakistani manufacturer

For a typical Pakistani auto, cement, fertiliser, or textile manufacturer, the non-current assets section roughly looks like:

| Line | Typical share of non-current assets |
|---|---|
| Property, Plant and Equipment | 70 to 90 percent |
| Intangible assets | 1 to 5 percent |
| Long-term investments and deposits | 5 to 20 percent |
| Long-term loans and advances | 1 to 5 percent |
| Deferred tax asset | 0 to 5 percent |

A company where PP&E is 95+ percent and everything else is negligible is a clean, capital-intensive operating business. A company where long-term investments dominate and PP&E is small is operating more like a holding or investment vehicle than a pure operating company.

<Callout type="tip" title="Watch capex over years">
  Open the last five years of a Pakistani company's annual reports
  and trace the "Additions" line in the PP&E note across years.
  A company with consistent annual capex (relative to revenue) is
  reinvesting; a company that has cut capex sharply may be
  preserving cash but stalling growth.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Non-current assets are the long-lived operating resources of the company: PP&E, intangibles, long-term loans, long-term deposits, and deferred tax.
- PP&E typically dominates for Pakistani manufacturing companies, with detailed schedules in the notes for each sub-category.
- Tangible assets are physical (land, machinery); intangible assets are not (software, patents, goodwill).
- Long-term loans and advances often include employee loans; check related-party patterns.
- Deferred tax appears as both an asset and a liability depending on whether the company has overpaid or underpaid tax in current periods.
- Capex (additions in PP&E) over multiple years tells you whether the company is reinvesting in itself.
