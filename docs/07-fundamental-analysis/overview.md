# What Is Fundamental Analysis?

If you bought a second-hand car, you would not pay just based on the way it looks in the photograph. You would ask for the registration papers, the maintenance history, who has driven it, what the engine sounds like, and what comparable cars are selling for. Buying a stock without doing the same kind of work is buying on belief, not on understanding. **Fundamental analysis** is the structured way Pakistani long-term investors do that work on a company before they put their money in.

This category, the largest on the site, is a 24-page walk through every part of fundamental analysis. The pedagogical order roughly follows: what fundamental analysis is, the qualitative side, then the financial statements (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow), then the ratios that summarise them, and finally how to put it all together as an investment decision.

## What fundamental analysis is

**Fundamental analysis** is the process of determining a stock's **intrinsic value** (its true worth, given everything we can know about the underlying business) and comparing it to the **market price** (what the share is currently trading at on PSX). The decision rule is simple to state, harder to execute:

- If **intrinsic value is meaningfully above market price**, the stock is undervalued. Consider buying.
- If **intrinsic value is meaningfully below market price**, the stock is overvalued. Avoid or consider selling.

Calculating intrinsic value precisely is impossible. The point is not exact precision; it is to develop a strong enough estimate that you can tell whether the market is offering you a good deal or a bad one.

The page on [Intrinsic Value vs Market Price](/docs/fundamental-analysis/intrinsic-value-vs-market-price) covers this rule in detail.

## Why this matters more for long-term investors

Two big schools of thought on how to analyse stocks exist. The page on [Fundamental vs Technical Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/fundamental-vs-technical) walks through the comparison. In short:

- **Fundamental analysis** looks at the *business*. It asks whether the company is profitable, whether it can pay its debts, whether management is competent, whether the industry is healthy, and whether the share is fairly priced. Its time horizon is typically multiple years.
- **Technical analysis** looks at the *price chart*. It asks where the price has been, what patterns have formed, and where it might go next based on those patterns. Its time horizon is typically days to weeks.

Both have their place, but for the long-term wealth-building most Pakistani retail investors are after, fundamental analysis is the right tool. Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, and almost every billionaire long-term investor uses some form of fundamental analysis. Day-trading a chart pattern has never made anyone rich at scale.

## The top-down logic: economy, then industry, then company

A common mistake is to fall in love with a single company without checking the broader environment it operates in. The structured way to do fundamental analysis is **top-down**.

### 1. Economy

Look at the country's macroeconomic situation first. For Pakistan: GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, currency strength, the State Bank's monetary stance, the federal budget direction, and FDI inflows. A company in an economy with 11 percent inflation and rising rates faces very different headwinds than one in a stable, low-rate environment.

The page on [Economic Indicators](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/economic-indicators) covers the specific Pakistani indicators worth tracking.

### 2. Industry

Within the economy, look at the industry the company operates in. Is the industry growing, mature, or declining? What is the regulatory environment? Are there structural tailwinds (a young population for consumer goods, a power deficit for energy companies) or headwinds (a fertiliser industry exposed to gas-pricing regulation)?

A great company in a dying industry will struggle. An average company in a strongly growing industry can do well almost by accident.

### 3. Company

Within the industry, look at the specific company. This is where 95 percent of your fundamental-analysis time goes. The financial statements, the management, the competitive moat, the capital allocation, the dividend history, and the price you are being asked to pay all matter.

Most of the rest of this category is about how to do this third step.

You can also do the analysis **bottom-up** (company first, then industry, then economy), and many successful investors prefer that. The order matters less than the discipline of doing all three.

## Quantitative and qualitative

Fundamental analysis has two complementary halves.

### Quantitative

The numbers. Revenue, profit, assets, debt, cash flow, ratios. These come from the company's [annual report](/docs/fundamental-analysis/annual-report) and quarterly financial statements. Quantitative analysis is rigorous and comparable across companies; it is also where most beginners start.

The pages on [balance sheet](/docs/fundamental-analysis/balance-sheet), [income statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/income-statement), [cash flow statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement), and the various ratio pages all sit on the quantitative side.

### Qualitative

The judgements that cannot be expressed as a number. Is the management honest and competent? Does the company have a moat that competitors cannot easily replicate? Are related-party transactions clean? Is the business at risk of technology disruption?

The page on [Qualitative Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/qualitative-analysis) covers this side. Qualitative judgement is harder than ratio computation but often makes more difference to long-term outcomes.

The best fundamental analysis combines both. A company with strong numbers but poor management is risky. A company with great management but weakening numbers might be in temporary trouble or might be slowly dying. Look at both.

## A realistic mental framing

Two practical points before you dive in.

**Studying a single company well takes weeks, not minutes.** This is the honest reality. A serious fundamental analysis of a single PSX-listed company involves reading at least three to five years of annual reports, computing a handful of ratios, comparing against industry peers, evaluating management, and arriving at a price you would pay. That work is rewarding, but it is real work. Treat it that way.

**Beating the index is hard, even with great fundamental analysis.** Most retail investors who do thorough fundamental analysis still find that a low-cost [KSE-100-tracking ETF or mutual fund](/docs/investment-basics/etfs) gives them most of the upside with much less work. Fundamental analysis is the path to *outperforming* the index, and a few careful investors do, but it is not a magic shortcut. Set realistic expectations.

<Callout type="tip" title="One company at a time">
  Do not try to fundamentally analyse 30 companies at once. Pick one
  Pakistani company you understand (a major bank, a fertiliser
  company, a consumer-goods name), apply everything in this category
  to it over a few weeks, and then move to the second company. Depth
  beats breadth in fundamental analysis.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Fundamental analysis determines a stock's intrinsic value by examining the company's financials, management, industry, and the broader economy.
- The decision rule is to buy when intrinsic value is above market price and avoid otherwise.
- A top-down approach (economy then industry then company) is the structured way to do it; bottom-up is also valid.
- Fundamental analysis has two complementary halves: quantitative (numbers, ratios, statements) and qualitative (management, moat, governance).
- Long-term investors rely on fundamental analysis. Short-term traders typically lean on technical analysis.
- This category's 24 pages cover everything from reading an annual report to computing the major ratios to integrating all of it into a buy-or-avoid decision.
