# Payout and Plowback Ratios

Every profitable Pakistani company faces a recurring decision: of the profit we just made, how much do we **pay out** as dividends to shareholders, and how much do we **reinvest** (plow back) into the business? The mix between these two is captured by the payout ratio and the plowback ratio. Together they shape the future trajectory of the share price more than almost any other capital-allocation choice.

## What you'll learn

- The payout ratio formula
- The plowback (retention) ratio formula
- Why high plowback only matters if the company can actually deploy the cash productively
- Watch out for "borrowed" dividends where payout exceeds 100 percent
- The Unilever Pakistan 2016 episode

## The formulas

```
Payout ratio = Dividend per share / Earnings per share (EPS)

Plowback ratio (retention ratio) = 1 - Payout ratio
```

The two always sum to 1 (or to 100 percent in percentage terms). Whatever is not paid out is by definition retained.

A worked example. A Pakistani company has:

- EPS of PKR 50.
- Annual cash dividend of PKR 20 per share.

```
Payout ratio = 20 / 50 = 0.40 = 40%
Plowback ratio = 1 - 0.40 = 0.60 = 60%
```

The interpretation: 40 percent of profit goes to shareholders as cash; 60 percent stays in the company as retained earnings, available for reinvestment.

## What this tells you

Payout vs plowback is a window into the company's growth strategy and capital-allocation discipline.

### High payout (low plowback)

The company is returning most of its profit to shareholders. This is appropriate when:

- **The business is mature**, with limited growth opportunities. Returning cash to shareholders lets them deploy it elsewhere.
- **Cash flow is strong and predictable**, so dividends are sustainable.
- **The company has minimal capex needs**.

Examples in Pakistan: large utility companies (subject to regulatory rules), some power-generation companies, mature consumer brands.

### Low payout (high plowback)

The company is retaining most of its profit to fund growth. This is appropriate when:

- **The business has strong reinvestment opportunities** (new products, capacity expansion, geographic expansion).
- **Returns on incremental capital are high** (i.e., reinvesting profit at high ROCE).
- **Management has a track record of allocating capital well**.

Examples in Pakistan: growing technology companies, expanding manufacturers, companies in early-stage industries.

### The trap: high plowback with no growth

This is where capital allocation fails most often. A company retains most of its profit (high plowback) but does not actually deploy it productively. The retained earnings pile up on the balance sheet as cash or unproductive assets. Revenue and profit do not grow, the share price does not move, and shareholders eventually conclude the company is wasting their money.

A worked example. A company has:

- 40 percent payout ratio, 60 percent plowback.
- EPS PKR 10 in Year 1.
- Five years later, EPS is still PKR 10.

The company retained 60 percent of EPS for five years (PKR 30 per share over five years), and produced zero growth. That retained cash is sitting somewhere unproductively: on the balance sheet as cash that earns 12 percent in T-bills, or as poor-quality investments, or as related-party loans.

If management cannot deploy retained earnings at returns higher than what shareholders could earn elsewhere, the shareholders should be receiving the cash as dividends instead.

The page on [Qualitative Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/qualitative-analysis) covers this directly under capital allocation. Stagnant earnings combined with high plowback is one of the clearest signals of bad capital allocation.

## When payout exceeds 100 percent: borrowed dividends

A particularly weird pattern. Sometimes a company pays a dividend that **exceeds** its EPS for the year. Payout ratio above 100 percent.

Where does the extra money come from? Two places:

- **Cash reserves** built up in prior profitable years.
- **New borrowing** taken on specifically to fund the dividend.

Worked example. A company has EPS of PKR 25 but pays PKR 40 per share in dividends.

```
Payout ratio = 40 / 25 = 1.60 = 160%
```

The PKR 15 difference per share has come from somewhere other than this year's earnings. The annual report should disclose the source.

### Why this is sometimes okay

A large, cash-rich company can absorb occasional high-payout years. The classic Pakistani example is **Unilever Pakistan in 2016**, when the company paid out roughly 1,178 percent of EPS. Earnings per share were about PKR 207, but the company paid roughly PKR 370 per share in dividends, funded from accumulated cash reserves.

Why was Unilever Pakistan willing to do this? Several plausible reasons: the company had built large cash reserves over years of strong profitability and was returning excess cash to shareholders; profit had dropped temporarily in 2016 due to one-off factors; management was comfortable that 2017 profits would recover.

The next year (2017), Unilever returned to roughly 93 percent payout, which is high but normal for a mature consumer-goods company. The 2016 episode was a deliberate one-off return of capital, not a structural problem.

### Why it is sometimes a flag

A small or financially weaker company that pays dividends in excess of EPS, **funded from borrowings**, is a different story. The company is essentially borrowing money to pay shareholders. This:

- Increases leverage.
- Reduces interest coverage.
- Often signals that management is more worried about share price (and the message a maintained dividend sends) than about long-term financial health.

If you see payout above 100 percent on a Pakistani company, check the cash flow statement. If the dividend is funded from cash reserves (financing activities show the dividend outflow but no major new borrowing), it is probably fine. If the dividend is funded from new debt, treat it as a flag.

## Pakistani sector patterns

Approximate typical payout ratios by sector, in normal years.

| Sector | Typical payout ratio |
|---|---|
| Mature consumer goods | 60 to 95% |
| Pharmaceutical | 40 to 70% |
| Banks | 30 to 60% (subject to regulatory capital requirements) |
| Cement | 30 to 60% (cyclical) |
| Fertiliser | 50 to 80% |
| Power generation (regulated) | 70 to 100% (often capped near 100%) |
| Textiles | 20 to 50% |
| Growth-stage technology / pharma | 0 to 30% |

For sectors with regulated dividend rules (banks, certain regulated utilities), the payout pattern is partly externally constrained.

## A capital-allocation framework

When evaluating a Pakistani company on payout / plowback:

1. **Compute the payout ratio** for the most recent year and average over the last five years.
2. **Look at the trend**. Is the company moving toward more or less payout?
3. **Cross-check against ROCE**. A company with high ROCE retaining cash is plausibly creating value. A company with low ROCE retaining cash is destroying it.
4. **Check the balance sheet for unproductive cash buildup**. If retained earnings have grown sharply but cash is sitting unproductively in T-bills, the company is not deploying capital well.
5. **For dividend stocks specifically**: cross-check that operating cash flow comfortably covers the dividend (covered on the [Cash Flow Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement) page).

<Callout type="tip" title="Match payout policy to ROCE">
  A useful rule of thumb: if the company can reinvest profits at
  ROCE higher than what shareholders could realistically earn
  elsewhere (say, 15 percent or more in Pakistan), high plowback
  is good. If reinvestment ROCE is below 10 percent, high payout
  serves shareholders better. The page on
  [ROCE](/docs/fundamental-analysis/roce) covers the cutoff.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Payout ratio = Dividend per share divided by EPS. Plowback is 1 minus payout.
- High payout suits mature businesses with limited growth. High plowback suits growth businesses with productive reinvestment opportunities.
- The trap: high plowback with no growth is wasted capital. Force the question of how retained earnings are being deployed.
- Payout above 100 percent (borrowed dividend) is sometimes a deliberate one-off (Unilever Pakistan 2016) and sometimes a flag for financially stressed companies.
- Match payout policy to ROCE; high reinvestment returns justify retention, low ones do not.
