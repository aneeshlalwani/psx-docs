# Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

If you went to a friend's restaurant and asked how much the business was worth, a sensible question to ask back is "how much does it earn in a year?" If the answer is PKR 10 lakh and your friend wants PKR 1 crore for it, you are paying 10 times annual earnings. That is the P/E ratio in plain language. Applied to PSX-listed companies, the **price-to-earnings ratio** is the single most-watched valuation metric: how much the market is paying for each rupee of a company's annual earnings.

## The formula

```
P/E ratio = Current share price / Earnings per share (EPS)

EPS = Profit after tax (PAT) / Number of shares outstanding
```

A worked example. A Pakistani company has:

- Profit after tax of PKR 50 crore
- 25 lakh (2.5 million) shares outstanding
- Current share price of PKR 500

```
EPS = 50,00,00,000 / 25,00,000 = PKR 200 per share
```

Wait, that EPS would imply a P/E of 500/200 = 2.5, which is unreasonably low. The numbers in the source notes for this kind of example are illustrative; let's redo with more realistic Pakistani numbers.

A second worked example. A Pakistani company has:

- Profit after tax of PKR 50 crore
- 5 crore shares outstanding (50 million)
- Current share price of PKR 200

```
EPS = 50,00,00,000 / 5,00,00,000 = PKR 10 per share
P/E = 200 / 10 = 20
```

The market is paying PKR 20 for every PKR 1 of annual earnings. Whether 20 is high or low depends on context, covered below.

## Reading the P/E ratio

The naive way to read P/E is:

- **Low P/E**: stock is cheap. You pay little for each rupee of earnings.
- **High P/E**: stock is expensive. You pay a lot for each rupee of earnings.

The naive reading is wrong as often as it is right. P/E only tells you how much you pay relative to *current* earnings. The question that determines whether the price is fair is: **what will earnings do in the future?**

A high P/E can be perfectly justified if earnings are expected to grow rapidly. A low P/E can be a trap if earnings are about to collapse.

### The same number in two industries

Suppose two Pakistani companies both have a P/E of 25.

- **Company A** is in a sector with industry-average P/E of 15. It is trading at a 67 percent premium to peers.
- **Company B** is in a sector with industry-average P/E of 30. It is trading at a 17 percent discount to peers.

Both have the same numerical P/E, but Company A looks expensive and Company B looks cheap. Always compare against industry, never in isolation.

### The same number across time

Track a company's P/E over its own history.

- Three years ago: EPS PKR 10, share price PKR 150, P/E 15.
- Two years ago: EPS PKR 11, share price PKR 110, P/E 10.
- This year: EPS PKR 12, share price PKR 120, P/E 10.

The company is growing slowly, the share price has crept up, and the P/E has flatlined at 10. If the industry trades at 15, this company is consistently cheap relative to peers. The market is not giving credit for the slow growth, possibly because growth is too slow to justify a higher multiple, or possibly because investors do not yet understand the story.

## Why P/E should not be used in isolation

Several reasons.

### 1. Earnings can be one-off

A company that had a great year (sold a big subsidiary, won a tax case, received a regulatory windfall) might post artificially high EPS for one year. Its P/E looks low, but next year's EPS will revert to normal and the P/E will jump back up. Always check whether current EPS is sustainable.

### 2. Earnings can be manipulated

The income statement is on accrual basis and contains many judgement calls. Aggressive revenue recognition, deferred expenses, or one-time write-backs can flatter EPS. The page on the [Cash Flow Statement](/docs/fundamental-analysis/cash-flow-statement) covers why operating cash flow is a sanity check on reported earnings.

### 3. Cyclical industries play tricks

For cyclical sectors (cement, steel, automobiles, fertilisers), P/E ratios mislead in both directions. At the **peak** of a cycle, EPS is high and P/E looks low (cheap!), but earnings are about to fall. At the **trough**, EPS is low and P/E looks high (expensive!), but earnings are about to recover. Cyclical investors often invert the rule: buy when P/E looks high, sell when P/E looks low.

### 4. Capital structure matters

Two companies with the same P/E and same earnings might have very different debt loads. The page on [Enterprise Value](/docs/fundamental-analysis/enterprise-value) covers why EV-based ratios (EV-to-EBITDA in particular) sometimes give a cleaner comparison.

## The PEG ratio

A common refinement: divide P/E by the company's earnings growth rate.

```
PEG ratio = P/E / Annual EPS growth rate (%)
```

A worked example. A company has P/E of 20 and is growing EPS at 25 percent per year.

```
PEG = 20 / 25 = 0.8
```

The interpretation:

- **PEG below 1**: cheap relative to growth. Worth investigating.
- **PEG of 1**: fairly priced for the growth.
- **PEG above 1**: expensive relative to growth.
- **PEG above 2**: very expensive; the market is pricing in growth that may not materialise.

PEG is most useful for comparing growth companies. It is less useful for stable mature businesses where growth is low single-digits and small denominator changes swing the ratio wildly.

## Forward vs trailing P/E

Two versions of the ratio.

- **Trailing P/E (P/E TTM)**: based on the last twelve months of actual EPS. The standard reported number on PSX data sites.
- **Forward P/E**: based on analyst-estimated EPS for the next twelve months.

Forward P/E is more useful conceptually (you are buying future earnings, not past) but only as good as the estimates feeding it. Pakistani equity-research coverage is thinner than developed markets, so forward P/Es are sometimes based on a single broker's estimates that may not be reliable.

For most retail investors, trailing P/E with a careful sense of whether earnings are sustainable is the practical metric.

## Pakistani context: typical P/E ranges

Approximate historical P/E ranges by Pakistani sector. These shift with market conditions; check current data on PSX before relying on specific numbers.

| Sector | Typical P/E range |
|---|---|
| Banks | 4 to 9 |
| Cement | 5 to 12 |
| Fertiliser | 5 to 10 |
| Oil and gas | 4 to 8 |
| Power generation | 4 to 7 |
| Consumer goods (FMCG) | 15 to 35 |
| Pharmaceutical | 15 to 30 |
| Textiles | 4 to 10 |
| Automobiles | 6 to 14 |
| IT services | 10 to 30+ |

The KSE-100 index trailing P/E often sits in the **6 to 10 range**, low by global standards, reflecting Pakistan's specific risk profile (currency, political, economic). When the market P/E rises above its historical norm, broad valuation is stretched; when it falls well below, value tends to emerge.

## Common misuses

Three common ways retail investors misuse P/E.

**Buying any low-P/E stock.** Some stocks are cheap because the market is right about their problems. The page on [Comparative and Trend Analysis](/docs/fundamental-analysis/comparative-and-trend-analysis) covers how to confirm that a low P/E reflects undervaluation rather than a genuine business problem.

**Avoiding any high-P/E stock.** A high P/E might be the market's reasonable estimate of future growth. Pakistani consumer goods companies often trade at P/Es of 25 to 35; that does not automatically make them overvalued.

**Reading P/E without earnings quality.** A company with low-quality earnings (heavy non-cash gains, aggressive accounting, declining cash flow) can have a flattering P/E that does not reflect underlying business health.

<Callout type="tip" title="Always pair P/E with two things">
  Never read P/E without also looking at:
  (1) the industry-average P/E for the same sector,
  (2) the company's own multi-year P/E range.
  These two anchor the number; without them, P/E is a free-floating
  statistic that can mislead in either direction.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- P/E ratio = share price divided by earnings per share. Tells you how much the market pays for each rupee of annual earnings.
- A low P/E is not automatically cheap; a high P/E is not automatically expensive.
- Always compare against industry average and against the company's own historical P/E range.
- PEG ratio (P/E divided by growth rate) is a refinement for growth companies.
- Trailing P/E is standard; forward P/E is conceptually better but depends on estimate quality.
- Cyclical industries mislead with P/E; the metric is most useful for stable businesses with predictable earnings.
- Never read P/E alone. Pair with growth rate, earnings quality (cash flow check), and industry context.
