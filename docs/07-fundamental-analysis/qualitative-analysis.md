# Qualitative Analysis

If you spend a weekend computing every ratio in this category for a Pakistani company and conclude that the numbers look great, you have done one half of the work. The other half is judging things that no spreadsheet captures: who is running the company, whether they are honest and competent, whether the company has a structural advantage that protects it, and whether changing technology is about to make its business obsolete. **Qualitative analysis** is the harder half of fundamental analysis and often the more decisive one.

## What you'll learn

- The five qualitative checks every fundamental investor should run
- How to evaluate management honesty and competence in Pakistan specifically
- What a "moat" is and what counts as one
- How to read capital-allocation decisions
- Technology-disruption risk
- Related-party transactions and what to watch for

## The five qualitative checks

A practical checklist before buying any PSX-listed company.

1. **Management quality** — who is running this and how have they performed?
2. **The moat** — what protects the company from competitors?
3. **Capital allocation** — how does management deploy retained earnings?
4. **Technology disruption** — could a new technology kill this business?
5. **Related-party transactions** — are insiders extracting value at shareholder expense?

The rest of this page works through each.

## 1. Management quality

Long-term company performance depends overwhelmingly on the quality of decisions management makes. A great business with bad management can be destroyed; a mediocre business with great management often grows into something excellent. So before investing, get to know the people running the company.

### Look at the board of directors

The annual report lists the board of directors with biographies. Read them.

- **Tenure**: how long have the directors been with the company? Long, stable boards (10+ years) are usually a positive signal of consistency. Constant churn (multiple new directors every year) is a red flag.
- **Backgrounds**: do the directors have relevant experience? An automobile company with a board full of bankers and bureaucrats with no industry experience is concerning.
- **Independence**: how many independent directors are on the board? Pakistani corporate governance rules require a minimum number, but more is generally better.

### Search the names

Take the names of the CEO, Managing Director, and key board members and put each one into Google. Add the search terms "fraud", "scam", "scandal", "court case", "SECP", and "FBR notice" individually. Past disputes are often public record. If multiple directors have a history of regulatory issues, that is significant.

LinkedIn is also useful. Check education, prior roles, and how long they have been in the industry. Are these people actually qualified to be running this size of company?

### Check the company itself

Same exercise for the company name. "Company name + fraud", "+ scandal", "+ dispute", "+ court case", "+ SECP". You are looking for unresolved regulatory issues, governance failures, or a pattern of disputes with employees, partners, or shareholders.

### Watch for compensation patterns

Pakistani annual reports disclose director and CEO compensation. A few signals:

- **Compensation rising while profit falls** for several years is a misalignment of interests.
- **Massive bonus payouts during downturns** suggest a board that does not enforce performance discipline.
- **Compensation packages out of line with peer companies** can signal capture by the executive team.

## 2. The moat

A **moat** (Warren Buffett's term, drawn from the water-filled defences of medieval castles) is a structural advantage that protects a company from being overtaken by competitors. Companies with deep, durable moats tend to compound for decades. Companies without a moat often get eroded over time.

The most common kinds of moat:

### Cost advantage

The company can produce more cheaply than competitors, either because of scale, location, technology, or supply-chain integration. Pakistani fertiliser companies that own gas-supply contracts at preferential prices have a structural cost advantage in their input mix.

### Network effect

The product becomes more valuable the more people use it. Stock exchanges, payment networks, and large social platforms have network effects. Pakistani examples are limited but growing.

### Switching costs

Customers find it costly or painful to leave. Banks benefit from this; once you have set up auto-debits, salary deposits, and bill payments through one bank, switching is annoying. Software companies often build switching costs into their products deliberately.

### Brand

A strong brand lets the company charge a premium. Pakistani consumer-goods names like Nestle, Engro Foods, and Lucky Cement (in some segments) have brand premiums that competitors cannot easily replicate.

### Distribution

A retail or distribution network that took decades to build is hard to replicate quickly. Suzuki Motors in Pakistan has a famously deep dealer and after-sales network spread across the country, which is one reason competitors like Chery, Hyundai Santro, and Daihatsu struggled to dent its market share for decades while Suzuki Mehran kept selling well for over 30 years.

### Regulatory moat

Some businesses operate under a regulatory licence that is hard for new entrants to obtain. Pakistani cement, banking, insurance, and pharmaceutical companies all have regulatory barriers that limit competition.

### How to evaluate a moat

Ask: if a well-funded competitor walked in tomorrow with PKR 500 crore and said "I am going to take this market", how easy or hard would it be? If the answer is "very easy", there is no moat. If the answer is "they could try, but the existing player has 30 years of brand, distribution, and supply-chain head start", there is a moat.

## 3. Capital allocation

Every profitable company faces a question every year: *what do we do with the profit we just made?* The options are limited.

1. **Pay it as a dividend** to shareholders.
2. **Reinvest it** into the existing business (capex, working capital).
3. **Acquire** another company.
4. **Pay down debt**.
5. **Buy back shares** (less common in Pakistan but allowed under some rules).
6. **Hold it as cash** for opportunities.

The right answer depends on the company. A high-return business with growth opportunities should reinvest aggressively. A mature business with limited growth should pay generous dividends. A business with high debt and weak cash flow should pay down debt.

The wrong answer is to retain large amounts of profit, do nothing useful with it, and watch the share price stagnate. The page on [Payout and Plowback Ratios](/docs/fundamental-analysis/payout-and-plowback) has a worked example of this failure mode.

Ask yourself: looking at the last five years, has management deployed retained earnings into projects that grew revenue and profit? Or has retained capital piled up without producing growth?

Bad capital allocation is one of the most common ways shareholders lose value in good businesses.

## 4. Technology disruption

A great business model today can be obsolete in a decade. Examples from recent history:

- **Cable TV** got disrupted by YouTube and Netflix.
- **Yellow taxis and rickshaws** got disrupted by Careem and Uber.
- **Petrol vehicles** are slowly being disrupted by electric.
- **Bricks-and-mortar retail** has been disrupted by e-commerce.
- **Newspaper advertising** has been disrupted by digital ad platforms.

Before investing in any company, ask: what would technology have to do for this company's product or service to become obsolete? How likely is that within ten years?

A company in a stable, hard-to-disrupt industry (cement, fertiliser, basic banking, food production) has a long runway. A company in a fast-changing industry (consumer electronics, media, retail) faces real disruption risk.

This does not mean avoid all technology-exposed businesses. It means understand the risk before signing up for it.

## 5. Related-party transactions

A **related-party transaction** is a transaction between the company and someone connected to insiders (the owner, the owner's family, the owner's other companies). These are not always wrong; in fact, regulated relationships between holding companies and subsidiaries are normal.

The annual report includes a **Related Party Transactions** disclosure section. Read it.

Red flags to watch for:

- **The company lending to insiders or their other businesses at below-market rates** (e.g., 0 percent interest, or a fraction of normal market rates). This is shareholder cash being given to insiders cheaply.
- **The company guaranteeing personal loans** taken by directors or owners. If the borrower defaults, the company is on the hook.
- **The company buying or selling assets to related parties at off-market prices**.
- **Large management fees** paid to related-party entities for services that are vague or non-essential.
- **Frequent intercompany transfers** that suggest the listed company is being used to fund private interests.

If related-party transactions are clean (at market rates, properly disclosed, and clearly serving the listed company's interests), there is nothing to worry about. If they look like value extraction, walk away. The dilution of minority-shareholder value through poor related-party governance has been a major source of losses in emerging markets.

## Putting it together

After running these five checks, you should have a qualitative judgement about the company that complements the quantitative ratios in the rest of this category. A company that scores well on all five (clean management, deep moat, smart capital allocation, low disruption risk, clean related-party governance) is a strong candidate for further analysis. A company that fails any one materially is a higher-risk proposition; multiple failures usually mean walk away.

<Callout type="tip" title="Be honest with yourself">
  Qualitative analysis is mostly judgement, and your judgement can
  fail. The best safeguard is humility: if your numbers look good but
  your gut says something is off, take the gut seriously. The opposite
  pattern, falling in love with a company despite uncomfortable
  qualitative signals, is how most retail investors lose money on
  individual stock picks.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Qualitative analysis covers the parts of the company that resist easy numerical measurement: management, moat, capital allocation, disruption risk, and related-party governance.
- Run all five checks; do not skip them because the numbers look good.
- Management quality matters more than any single ratio over multi-year horizons.
- The moat (cost, network, switching costs, brand, distribution, regulation) is what protects long-term returns.
- Capital allocation is the recurring decision that compounds or destroys shareholder value.
- Related-party transactions are not always wrong, but bad ones are a major red flag.
