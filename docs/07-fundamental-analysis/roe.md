# Return on Equity (ROE)

Of all the ratios covered in this category, **Return on Equity (ROE)** is the one Warren Buffett has talked about more than any other. The reason is straightforward: ROE tells you, in a single number, how much profit the company is generating on the money shareholders have entrusted to it. A company with consistently high ROE is using shareholder capital efficiently; a company with low or declining ROE is wasting it. But like most powerful ratios, ROE has a leverage trap that misleads beginners. This page walks through both the metric and the trap.

## What you'll learn

- The ROE formula and a Pakistani worked example
- Why Buffett favours ROE
- The leverage trap: how high debt inflates ROE without creating value
- Why ROE on book equity is not your personal return as a secondary-market buyer
- How to interpret ROE alongside debt levels and historical trend

## The formula

```
ROE = (Net income / Total shareholders' equity) × 100
```

Net income is the bottom-line PAT from the income statement. Shareholders' equity is the total of share capital, share premium, and reserves on the balance sheet (often called "book value of equity").

A worked example. A Pakistani company has:

- Equity raised via IPO of PKR 40 crore.
- A bank loan of PKR 20 crore.
- Total assets of PKR 60 crore.
- Total liabilities of PKR 20 crore.
- Therefore total equity is PKR 40 crore.

The income statement shows:

- Total sales of PKR 20 crore.
- After cost of sales, operating expenses, interest, and tax, profit after tax (PAT) of PKR 8 crore.

```
ROE = (8 / 40) × 100 = 20%
```

The interpretation: shareholders earned a 20 percent return on the PKR 40 crore of equity sitting in the company.

## Why Buffett favours ROE

A few reasons.

### It captures management's actual job

Management has been entrusted with shareholders' money. The single best test of how well they are doing the job is whether they are generating high returns on that money. ROE is exactly that test.

### It is comparable across companies

Unlike absolute profit, ROE is normalised by the equity invested. A company with PKR 1 crore of equity generating PKR 30 lakh in profit (30 percent ROE) is a more efficient business than one with PKR 100 crore of equity generating PKR 5 crore (5 percent ROE), even though the second company is much bigger in absolute terms.

### High and stable ROE compounds beautifully

A company that consistently earns 20 percent on equity and reinvests most of it grows equity at roughly 20 percent per year (assuming low payout). Over decades, that compounding produces extraordinary wealth. The page on [Power of Compounding](/docs/personal-finance/power-of-compounding) shows what 20 percent compounded over 20 to 30 years actually produces.

This is why Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway has, over decades, sought out exactly these high-ROE compounders.

## The leverage trap

A critical caveat. ROE can be inflated by **leverage** without the underlying business actually being more profitable.

Two companies in the same industry, both with PAT of PKR 8 crore:

| | Company A | Company B |
|---|---|---|
| Total assets | PKR 60 crore | PKR 60 crore |
| Total debt | PKR 20 crore | PKR 40 crore |
| Total equity | PKR 40 crore | PKR 20 crore |
| PAT | PKR 8 crore | PKR 8 crore |
| ROE | 20% | **40%** |

Company B looks much better on ROE alone (40 percent vs 20 percent). But it has half the equity and twice the debt of Company A. In a downturn, Company B's higher leverage will hurt it disproportionately. The interest cost is fixed; if profits drop by half, A still earns 10 percent ROE while B might earn nothing or post a loss.

**Higher ROE is not always better.** Always check the debt-to-equity ratio (covered on the [Solvency Ratios](/docs/fundamental-analysis/solvency-ratios) page) alongside ROE. A company with 30 percent ROE and debt-to-equity of 0.3 is genuinely excellent. A company with 30 percent ROE and debt-to-equity of 3.0 is a leveraged bet, and the same number means something completely different.

## ROE is not your personal return

A subtle but important point. ROE is computed on **the equity sitting in the company's books**, which is typically the original IPO investment plus retained earnings. This is **not** the same as your personal return as a secondary-market buyer.

A worked example. A company has book value per share of PKR 200 but its share is currently trading at PKR 350 on PSX.

You buy at PKR 350. A year later, the share is at PKR 400 and the company also pays a PKR 50 dividend. Your personal return is:

```
Personal ROI = (50 + 50) / 350 = 28.5%
```

But the company's ROE, as reported in the annual report, is computed on the PKR 200 book value per share, not your PKR 350 cost. The company's ROE might be reported as 20 percent (based on book equity).

These two numbers are different and measure different things:

- **ROE (the company's number)**: how efficiently the company uses its book equity. A management-quality measure.
- **Your ROI**: how well you did on the price you actually paid. A function of both the company's performance and the price you paid for the stock.

Both matter, but for different decisions. ROE helps you decide whether the company is well-run. Your ROI tells you how the trade actually worked out.

## Why share price often exceeds book value

Following from the previous point: why would a company with book value PKR 200 trade at PKR 350?

Because price reflects expectations, not just current book equity:

- If the company has low debt, growing equity each year, growing profit, and pays regular dividends, all of that adds up to high expected future returns.
- A PKR 350 share paying PKR 50 per year in dividends recovers your investment in 7 years. After that, every dividend is pure profit. Plus the share might be trading at PKR 1,000 by then because the same growth cycle continues.
- Buyers in the secondary market pay a premium to book value when the company can compound at high ROE for many years. Buyers refuse to pay more than book value (and often less) when ROE is mediocre or declining.

This relationship between ROE and the price-to-book multiple is one of the deepest concepts in investing. High-ROE businesses justify high price-to-book ratios; low-ROE businesses do not.

## A practical framework

When evaluating a Pakistani company on ROE.

1. **Compute ROE** for the most recent year.
2. **Look at the trend** over five to ten years. Stable or rising is good; declining is a flag.
3. **Compare against peers** in the same industry. ROE is highly industry-specific.
4. **Cross-check with debt-to-equity**. If ROE is high but D/E is also high, the high ROE is probably leveraged rather than genuinely efficient.
5. **Cross-check with operating cash flow**. If ROE is rising but operating cash flow is flat, the rise is being driven by paper profits rather than real cash generation.

A company that scores well on all five (high stable ROE, conservative debt, strong cash flow, high relative to peers) is a strong candidate for further analysis.

## Industry context

Approximate ROE ranges by Pakistani sector. These shift with market and economic conditions.

| Sector | Typical ROE range |
|---|---|
| Consumer goods (FMCG) | 25 to 60% |
| Pharmaceutical | 15 to 25% |
| Banks (top tier) | 15 to 25% |
| Cement | 10 to 25% |
| Fertiliser | 15 to 30% |
| Power generation | 10 to 20% |
| Textiles | 5 to 15% |
| Heavy industry / engineering | 10 to 18% |

A company posting 30 percent ROE consistently in any sector is usually a top performer; one posting 5 percent ROE is at risk of underperforming the broader market over time.

<Callout type="tip" title="Use ROE × payout × time to forecast">
  A useful back-of-envelope: a company that earns 20 percent ROE
  and reinvests 70 percent of earnings (paying 30 percent as
  dividends) grows book equity at roughly 20% × 70% = 14% per year.
  Over 10 years, that compounds book equity by 3.7 times. Share
  prices follow book equity loosely over long horizons. Strong
  long-term return potential.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- ROE measures how much profit the company generates on shareholders' equity.
- Buffett favours ROE because it captures management's job and rewards compounders.
- ROE can be inflated by leverage; always cross-check against debt-to-equity.
- ROE on book equity is not your personal return as a secondary-market buyer; the price you pay matters too.
- Compare against industry peers and trend over multiple years.
- Cross-check against operating cash flow to confirm the profit is real.
