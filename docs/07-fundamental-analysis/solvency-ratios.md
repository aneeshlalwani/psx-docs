# Solvency Ratios

Where [liquidity ratios](/docs/fundamental-analysis/liquidity-ratios) measure whether a company can pay its bills this month, **solvency ratios** measure whether the company is structurally stable over the long term. The simplest framing: if the company shut down today and sold everything, would it be able to pay all its debts? If yes, the company is solvent. If no, it is technically insolvent and on a path to bankruptcy. Two ratios capture this: debt-to-equity and debt-to-assets.

## Liquidity vs solvency

A quick recap of the distinction.

- **Liquidity** is short-term: can the company pay obligations due in the next 12 months?
- **Solvency** is long-term: does the company have enough total assets to cover total liabilities, with reasonable margin?

A company can be liquid but insolvent (lots of cash but even more debt over the long term, indicating eventual collapse). It can also be solvent but illiquid (plenty of long-term assets but not enough cash to pay this month's bills, leading to forced sales). Both ratios matter; a healthy company should pass both tests.

## Debt-to-equity ratio

The most-watched solvency ratio.

```
Debt-to-equity = Total debt / Total equity
```

Where:

- **Total debt** is the sum of long-term loans and short-term borrowings (sometimes the broader "total liabilities" is used; see the note below).
- **Total equity** is the shareholders' stake from the balance sheet (share capital + share premium + reserves).

A worked example. A company has PKR 1.7 crore in total assets and PKR 1 crore in total liabilities. Equity therefore equals PKR 70 lakh.

```
Debt-to-equity = 1,00,00,000 / 70,00,000 = 1.43
```

The interpretation: the company has PKR 1.43 of liabilities for every PKR 1 of equity. It is more leveraged than it is owner-funded.

### Reading the number

- **Below 0.5**: low leverage. The company is mostly funded by owners' equity rather than debt. Generally safe.
- **0.5 to 1.0**: moderate leverage. Common for stable operating companies.
- **1.0 to 2.0**: meaningful leverage. Common for capital-intensive industries like banks (much higher), utilities, real estate.
- **Above 2.0**: high leverage. Risk of distress under adverse conditions.

The "right" debt-to-equity ratio depends heavily on industry. **Banks routinely operate at 8 to 10 times equity** (because deposits are technically liabilities). **Utilities and infrastructure companies** sit at 1 to 2 times. **Asset-light services and consumer-goods companies** often sit below 0.5.

Compare against peers, not across sectors.

### Total debt vs total liabilities

Two definitions are commonly used.

- **Strict debt-to-equity**: only interest-bearing debt (loans, bonds, leases) divided by equity.
- **Total liabilities to equity**: all liabilities (including non-interest-bearing items like trade payables, deferred revenue) divided by equity.

The strict version is cleaner for measuring financial leverage. The broader version is common in introductory finance and gives a higher number. Always note which definition is being used. The source notes for this site use the broader version, where PKR 1 crore of total liabilities includes everything the company owes.

## Debt-to-assets ratio

A complementary view: what percentage of the company's total assets is funded by debt rather than by equity?

```
Debt-to-assets = Total debt / Total assets
```

A worked example. A company has PKR 10 crore in debt and PKR 40 crore in total assets.

```
Debt-to-assets = 10,00,00,000 / 40,00,00,000 = 0.25
```

The interpretation: 25 percent of assets are debt-funded; the remaining 75 percent is equity-funded. If the company sold off 25 percent of its assets, it could pay off all debt and still operate with the remaining 75 percent.

### Reading the number

- **Below 0.3**: conservatively financed. Most assets are equity-funded.
- **0.3 to 0.5**: moderate leverage. Common for healthy operating companies.
- **0.5 to 0.7**: meaningful leverage. The company is more debt-funded than equity-funded.
- **Above 0.7**: high leverage. Could be acceptable in capital-intensive regulated industries; risky elsewhere.
- **Above 1.0**: technically insolvent. Total liabilities exceed total assets. Even selling everything would not cover all debts. **Stay away.**

A second worked example for the bankruptcy boundary. A company has PKR 10 crore in debt and PKR 5 crore in assets.

```
Debt-to-assets = 10 / 5 = 2.0
```

This company is 200 percent debt-funded. Selling everything brings in PKR 5 crore against PKR 10 crore of debt. The company is technically insolvent. Any retail investor seeing this ratio should walk away regardless of how attractive other parts of the company look.

## Industry context

A practical table of typical debt-to-equity ranges.

| Sector | Typical debt-to-equity range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Banks | 8 to 12 | Customer deposits are liabilities |
| Utilities (power, gas) | 1 to 2.5 | Capital-intensive, regulated |
| Real estate / construction | 0.5 to 1.5 | Project-based |
| Cement, steel, fertiliser | 0.3 to 1.0 | Cyclical, capex-heavy |
| Automobile | 0.2 to 0.8 | Working-capital-heavy |
| Consumer goods (Nestle, Engro Foods) | 0.1 to 0.5 | Cash-generative |
| IT services / pharma | 0.0 to 0.3 | Asset-light |
| Mutual funds / asset managers | Near zero | Pass-through |

A Pakistani fertiliser company with debt-to-equity of 1.5 might be normal; a pharma company with the same number is unusually leveraged.

## Interest coverage as a complement

Solvency ratios alone do not tell you whether the company can actually service its debt. A company can have a low debt-to-equity ratio but very thin interest coverage if its operating profit is volatile. Conversely, a company with high debt-to-equity but stable, strong cash flows can comfortably service interest.

The page on [Interest Coverage Ratio](/docs/fundamental-analysis/interest-coverage-ratio) covers this directly. Always read solvency ratios and the interest coverage ratio together; one without the other is incomplete.

## A quick framework

When evaluating a Pakistani company on solvency:

1. **Compute debt-to-equity** and compare against the industry typical range.
2. **Compute debt-to-assets** as a sanity check; anything above 1 is a serious red flag.
3. **Look at the trend** over five years. Slow leverage growth without revenue growth is concerning.
4. **Cross-check against interest coverage**. If interest coverage is strong (say, above 4), high leverage is more sustainable than if it is thin.
5. **Look at the loan note** for the average interest rate, currency exposure, and maturities. Most Pakistani long-term loans are PKR-denominated, but exporters with USD loans face FX risk.

<Callout type="warning" title="Walk away from technical insolvency">
  Any Pakistani listed company with debt-to-assets above 1.0 is
  technically insolvent: total liabilities exceed total assets.
  Selling everything would not cover all debts. Investing here is
  speculation, not analysis. Walk away.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Solvency ratios measure long-term financial stability, complementing the short-term liquidity ratios.
- Debt-to-equity is total debt divided by equity; total liabilities divided by equity is a related broader measure.
- Debt-to-assets shows what percentage of assets is debt-funded; above 1 means insolvent.
- "Good" depends on industry: banks operate with high leverage by design, asset-light services with very little.
- Always combine solvency ratios with interest coverage to assess true debt-servicing capacity.
- A trend of rising leverage without revenue growth is a flag.
