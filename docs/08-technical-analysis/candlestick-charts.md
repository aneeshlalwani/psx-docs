# Candlestick Charts

If you open any modern broker terminal on PSX, the default chart type is almost always a **candlestick chart**. The format originated in 18th-century Japan, where rice traders developed it to track price movements visually, and has since become the standard for technical analysis worldwide. Each candle compresses four pieces of information about a single trading session (open, high, low, close) into a single visual element that even a beginner can read after a few minutes of practice.

## Anatomy of a candle

Every candle represents one **time period**. On a daily chart, each candle is one trading day. On a weekly chart, each is one week. On an intraday chart, each might be one minute, five minutes, or one hour.

Within that period, four price points matter:

- **Open**: the price at which the period began.
- **High**: the highest price reached during the period.
- **Low**: the lowest price reached during the period.
- **Close**: the price at which the period ended.

The candle visualises these four points:

```
       │     ← Upper wick (high)
       │
   ┌───┴───┐  ← Top of body
   │       │
   │ BODY  │     The body spans from open to close
   │       │
   └───┬───┘  ← Bottom of body
       │
       │     ← Lower wick (low)
       │
```

The **body** is the rectangle between open and close. The thin lines extending above and below the body are called **wicks** (or shadows or tails) and indicate the high and low of the period beyond where open and close were.

## Bullish vs bearish

The colour or fill of the body tells you immediately whether the period closed up or down.

- **Bullish candle (green or hollow / white)**: close was higher than open. Buyers dominated during the period.
- **Bearish candle (red or filled / black)**: close was lower than open. Sellers dominated during the period.

A simple at-a-glance read: a chart with mostly green candles in recent periods is in an uptrend; one with mostly red candles is in a downtrend.

The **size of the body** matters too. A long body indicates a strong directional move; a short body indicates indecision or a balanced session.

The **length of the wicks** is also informative. Long upper wicks indicate that buyers pushed price up but sellers pulled it back before the close. Long lower wicks indicate the opposite: sellers pushed down but buyers came back.

## Reading a single candle

Three quick examples.

### A long bullish body with small wicks

The session opened, climbed steadily, and closed near the high. Buyers dominated all session. Strong bullish signal.

### A long upper wick with a small body

Price ran up sharply during the session but sellers pushed it back near where it opened. Indecision or, in some contexts, an early sign of buyer exhaustion.

### A long lower wick with a small body near the top

Sellers pushed price down hard, but buyers came in and pushed it back up to near the open or higher. Often a sign of buying pressure stepping in at lower levels.

The interpretation of single candles depends on context (where the candle sits in the broader trend). The same long lower wick is bullish at the bottom of a downtrend (potential reversal) and less significant in the middle of a sideways range.

## Common single-candle patterns

A few configurations that traders watch for.

### Doji

A candle with **almost no body**: open and close are at virtually the same price. The wicks may be long or short.

A doji indicates that the period ended in indecision; neither buyers nor sellers won. Significance depends on context: a doji at the top of an uptrend often signals trend exhaustion, while a doji in a sideways market is usually meaningless.

### Hammer

A candle with a **small body at the top** and a **long lower wick** (typically at least twice the body length). Appears at the bottom of a downtrend.

The hammer says: sellers pushed price down sharply during the session, but buyers stepped in and pushed it back up close to the open. The shape suggests buying pressure is starting to assert itself. Often considered a bullish reversal signal, especially when followed by a bullish candle the next session.

### Hanging man

A candle visually identical to a hammer (small body at top, long lower wick) but appearing at the **top** of an uptrend. Reading: even though the close was near the open, the long lower wick shows that sellers attempted to push price down. Considered a bearish reversal signal in this context.

### Shooting star

The mirror image of a hammer. **Small body at the bottom**, long upper wick, appearing at the top of an uptrend. Buyers pushed price up but sellers slammed it back down. Considered a bearish reversal signal.

### Inverted hammer

Same shape as a shooting star but appearing at the bottom of a downtrend. The reading is more contested: the long upper wick suggests buyers attempted to break out, even though they failed by the close. Some traders read it as a bullish reversal, others as a continuation of weakness.

## Common two-candle patterns

Patterns that span two consecutive candles and are stronger than single-candle reads.

### Bullish engulfing

A small bearish candle followed by a large bullish candle whose body **fully engulfs** the previous candle's body. The bullish candle opens below the prior close and closes above the prior open.

Reading: a clear shift from selling pressure to buying pressure within two sessions. Considered a strong bullish reversal signal at the bottom of a downtrend.

### Bearish engulfing

The mirror image. A small bullish candle followed by a large bearish candle that engulfs the previous body. Considered a strong bearish reversal signal at the top of an uptrend.

### Piercing line

A bearish candle followed by a bullish candle that opens below the prior close (gap down) and closes **at least halfway into** the prior bearish body. Bullish reversal signal at the bottom of a downtrend.

### Dark cloud cover

The mirror image. A bullish candle followed by a bearish candle that opens above the prior close (gap up) and closes at least halfway into the prior bullish body. Bearish reversal signal at the top of an uptrend.

### Morning star and evening star

Three-candle patterns (technically beyond "two-candle" but commonly grouped). A morning star is: a long bearish candle, then a small-bodied candle (a doji or spinning top) gapping down, then a long bullish candle. Bullish reversal at the bottom.

The evening star is the opposite: long bullish, small-bodied, long bearish. Bearish reversal at the top.

## How candles fit into broader analysis

A common beginner mistake is to look at a single candle pattern in isolation and conclude that a price move is imminent. Candles are most useful when:

- **They appear at significant levels** (support, resistance, prior highs and lows).
- **They are confirmed by volume**. A bullish engulfing candle on heavy volume is more meaningful than the same pattern on thin volume.
- **They align with the broader trend or with a clear potential reversal point**. A hammer at the bottom of a clear downtrend is a stronger signal than a hammer in a sideways range.
- **They are followed by confirmation in the next one to two candles**.

The pages on [Chart Patterns](/docs/technical-analysis/chart-patterns) and [Technical Indicators](/docs/technical-analysis/technical-indicators) cover the broader context. Candles alone are a partial picture.

## Pakistani context

Several practical points specific to PSX.

### Daily candles are the standard

Most PSX retail trading uses daily candles. Intraday traders use five-minute or fifteen-minute candles, but the lower liquidity of many Pakistani stocks makes minute-by-minute candles noisy. Daily is usually the cleanest timeframe.

### Watch for thin-volume candles

Many small-cap PSX stocks trade thinly. A long bullish candle on a stock with only 50,000 shares of daily volume is much less reliable than the same candle on a million-share-volume stock. Always check volume alongside the candle pattern.

### Gaps are less common

Pakistani circuit breakers (covered on the [Circuit Breakers and Lower Lock](/docs/trading-and-orders/circuit-breakers) page) cap daily price moves at 10 percent or PKR 1, whichever is higher. Large overnight gaps are less common on PSX than on US markets where there are no such limits. Some traditional gap-based candle patterns therefore translate less cleanly to Pakistani charts.

<Callout type="tip" title="Three sessions of practice">
  The fastest way to learn candlestick reading is to spend three
  evenings looking at recent charts of three Pakistani stocks you
  know well (a major bank, a fertiliser, a cement company). Try to
  guess the next session's direction from the candle pattern, then
  check what actually happened. After a few weeks of this you will
  read candles fluently. Reading about candles is no substitute for
  pattern recognition under your own eyes.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- A candle visualises four data points (open, high, low, close) for one time period.
- Body colour shows direction (green / hollow for up, red / filled for down). Body size indicates conviction.
- Wicks above and below the body show the period's high and low; long wicks indicate rejected price moves.
- Common single-candle patterns: doji (indecision), hammer (potential bullish reversal), shooting star (potential bearish reversal).
- Common multi-candle patterns: bullish / bearish engulfing, piercing line / dark cloud cover, morning / evening stars.
- Candles are most useful when they appear at significant levels, with volume confirmation, and aligned with the broader trend.
- Pakistani circuit breakers limit gaps; some traditional gap-based patterns translate less cleanly.
