# Chart Patterns

Where [candlestick patterns](/docs/technical-analysis/candlestick-charts) capture short-term sentiment within one or two sessions, **chart patterns** capture broader formations that develop over weeks or months. These are the head-and-shoulders, triangles, double tops, and flags that fill technical-analysis books. Each is a configuration that traders believe carries predictive information based on the psychology that produced it. This page covers the most-recognised ones with a clear-eyed view of their strengths and limitations.

## What you'll learn

- Support and resistance levels
- Trendlines and channels
- Reversal patterns: head and shoulders, double top, double bottom
- Continuation patterns: triangles, flags, pennants
- The cup-and-handle pattern
- How volume confirms (or contradicts) chart patterns

## Support and resistance

Two foundational concepts that every chart pattern builds on.

### Support

A **support level** is a price at which buying interest has historically been strong enough to halt a price decline. When the price falls to support, buyers tend to step in and the price often bounces back up.

Support is identified by looking for prior lows: prices at which the share has touched repeatedly without falling below. The more times a level has held as support, the stronger the level is considered.

### Resistance

A **resistance level** is the mirror image: a price at which selling interest has historically been strong enough to halt a price advance. When the price rises to resistance, sellers tend to step in.

Resistance is identified by looking for prior highs: prices at which the share has stalled repeatedly without rising above.

### The role-reversal property

A common observation: once a support level is **broken** (price falls clearly below it), it often becomes a resistance level on the way back up. Once a resistance level is broken, it often becomes a support level on the way back down.

This is sometimes called "role reversal" or "polarity". The psychological logic: investors who bought at the old support level and saw it break are now sitting on losses; when price rallies back to that level, many of them sell to break even, creating selling pressure (resistance) at that price.

### Practical reading

When evaluating a Pakistani share's chart:

1. Look for horizontal price levels that have been touched multiple times in the last 6 to 24 months.
2. Mark them on the chart.
3. Distinguish active support / resistance from broken levels.

The most actionable patterns happen when price approaches a clear support or resistance level. The [Candlestick Charts](/docs/technical-analysis/candlestick-charts) page describes how candle patterns at these levels often provide the entry signal.

## Trendlines and channels

A **trendline** is a diagonal line connecting a series of higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs (downtrend).

- An **uptrend line** is drawn beneath the price, connecting at least two (preferably three or more) significant lows.
- A **downtrend line** is drawn above the price, connecting at least two significant highs.

A **channel** is two parallel trendlines: one beneath the price and one above. The price oscillates between them within the trend. Upper channel boundary acts like resistance; lower like support.

### Trendline breaks

When price clearly breaks a trendline (with conviction and volume), it is often interpreted as a signal that the trend is reversing or weakening. A break of a long-standing uptrend line, especially on heavy volume, is one of the more-watched signals in technical analysis.

## Reversal patterns

These patterns are interpreted as signals that the existing trend is exhausting and may reverse.

### Head and shoulders (top)

A pattern that forms at the **top of an uptrend**. Shape:

- **Left shoulder**: a peak.
- **Head**: a higher peak.
- **Right shoulder**: a peak roughly the same height as the left shoulder, lower than the head.
- **Neckline**: a roughly horizontal line connecting the lows between the shoulders and the head.

The pattern is "completed" when price falls through the neckline. The implied target (a rough rule of thumb) is the height of the head above the neckline projected downward from the neckline.

Volume typically declines from the left shoulder through the head and right shoulder, then expands as the neckline breaks. Volume confirmation is part of the classic interpretation.

### Inverse head and shoulders (bottom)

The mirror image at the **bottom of a downtrend**. Three troughs, with the middle one (the head) being the deepest. Completion when price breaks above the neckline. Bullish reversal signal.

### Double top

Two roughly equal peaks separated by a moderate trough. The pattern says: the price tried twice to break through the resistance level at the top, failed both times, and is likely to reverse.

Completion: price falls below the trough between the two peaks. Implied downside target: the height between the trough and the peaks projected downward from the trough.

### Double bottom

Mirror image. Two roughly equal troughs separated by a peak. Bullish reversal at the bottom of a downtrend.

### Triple top and triple bottom

Three peaks (or troughs) at a similar level. Considered stronger reversal signals than double tops or bottoms because the level has held three times.

## Continuation patterns

Patterns that suggest the price is taking a breather within an existing trend before continuing in the same direction.

### Symmetrical triangle

Price oscillates between a downward-sloping resistance line and an upward-sloping support line, converging to a point. The triangle represents a tightening range as buyers and sellers hold the line at progressively closer prices.

The triangle resolves when price breaks decisively above resistance or below support. The break direction can be either way; the pattern itself does not predict direction. Many traders watch for the break and trade in the breakout direction.

### Ascending triangle

A flat resistance line at the top with an upward-sloping support line at the bottom. Buyers are bidding up the lows even though sellers are holding the resistance level. Often resolves with an upward breakout. Bullish bias.

### Descending triangle

Mirror image. Flat support at the bottom, downward-sloping resistance at the top. Sellers are pushing down the highs even though buyers are holding the support level. Often resolves with a downward breakout. Bearish bias.

### Flags

A short-term continuation pattern that follows a sharp directional move (the "flagpole"). The flag itself is a small parallelogram of consolidation, sloping slightly against the prior trend.

After the flag completes (typically after a few sessions to a few weeks), the prior trend is expected to resume. The implied target is often the length of the flagpole projected from the breakout point.

### Pennants

A pennant is similar to a flag but the consolidation forms a small symmetrical triangle rather than a parallelogram. Same continuation logic.

### Cup and handle

A longer-term continuation pattern. The price forms a rounded "cup" (a U-shaped bottom) followed by a smaller "handle" (a brief dip) before breaking out above the cup's resistance level. Considered bullish.

## Volume as confirmation

A pattern's reliability depends heavily on volume.

- **Reversal patterns** (head and shoulders, double top, etc.) are typically considered confirmed when the breakdown or breakout happens on **heavy volume**. A break on thin volume is less reliable.
- **Continuation patterns** (flags, triangles) are considered confirmed when the prior strong move was on heavy volume, the consolidation is on lighter volume, and the resolution breakout is again on heavy volume.

A pattern that completes on thin volume is suspect and often produces a "false breakout" that reverses within a few sessions.

## Limitations and Pakistani context

A few realistic caveats.

### Pattern recognition is subjective

Two technicians often see different patterns in the same chart. The same data can support contradictory interpretations. Be honest about the subjectivity.

### Backtesting is hard

Patterns that "worked" historically may have been coincidence. With enough patterns and enough data, something always looks predictive in hindsight.

### Lower liquidity affects reliability

Many Pakistani small-cap and mid-cap stocks have thin daily volume. Patterns can form and resolve on artificial-looking volume that does not reflect genuine market consensus. Patterns are more reliable on liquid PSX names (KSE-30 constituents, the larger KSE-100 names) than on thinly traded stocks.

### Circuit breakers truncate patterns

Pakistani daily price-band rules (10 percent or PKR 1, whichever is higher; covered on the [Circuit Breakers](/docs/trading-and-orders/circuit-breakers) page) limit how fast a pattern can complete. Sharp moves get spread over more sessions than they would in markets without circuit breakers.

### Confirmation bias is real

Once you start looking for patterns, you tend to see them everywhere, including where they are not really there. Discipline yourself: a pattern is a pattern only when it has formed clearly, not when you can squint and see one.

<Callout type="tip" title="Combine patterns with stops">
  The disciplined way to trade chart patterns is to define a
  specific entry price (typically just past the breakout level), a
  specific stop loss (typically just inside the pattern boundary
  on the wrong side), and a specific target. If the pattern fails
  and your stop is hit, you exit the trade with a small known loss
  rather than letting it become a large unknown one.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Support and resistance are the foundational concepts; many patterns are built on top of them.
- Trendlines connect higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs (downtrend); their break often signals trend change.
- Reversal patterns: head and shoulders, double tops, double bottoms, inverse head and shoulders.
- Continuation patterns: triangles (symmetrical, ascending, descending), flags, pennants, cup and handle.
- Volume confirmation is critical; patterns on thin volume are unreliable.
- Pakistani-specific limits (lower liquidity, circuit breakers) constrain pattern reliability on smaller stocks.
- Use stops to manage risk; pattern recognition without disciplined risk management produces poor outcomes.
