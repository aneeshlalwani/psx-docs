# What Is Technical Analysis?

If fundamental analysis is the patient business of reading annual reports and computing ratios, **technical analysis** is the noisier, faster business of reading price charts. Where a fundamental analyst asks "is this business worth what the market is charging for it?", a technical analyst asks "given the way the price has moved recently, what is it likely to do next?" The two disciplines are very different, and on PSX, technical analysis has a smaller but real following among short-term traders. This category is a four-page introduction to the basics.

## The three core assumptions

Modern technical analysis rests on three foundational assumptions, attributed to Charles Dow and developed further over the 20th century.

### 1. Price discounts everything

Whatever is publicly known about a company, an industry, the economy, or sentiment is already reflected in the share price. Technical analysts argue that you do not need to study every news item, every annual report, or every macroeconomic release because by the time you read them, the market has already adjusted prices. The price chart is therefore a sufficient summary of all that information.

This assumption is closely related to (a weak version of) the **efficient markets hypothesis**.

### 2. Prices move in trends

Once a price starts moving in a direction, it tends to continue in that direction for some time before reversing. Trends can be **uptrends** (sequence of higher highs and higher lows), **downtrends** (lower highs and lower lows), or **sideways / range-bound**.

The technician's job is to identify the trend early, ride it, and recognise when it is exhausting. "The trend is your friend" is the most-quoted aphorism in the discipline.

### 3. History repeats itself

Human psychology is consistent over time. The same emotions (fear, greed, hope, panic) that drove price movements 50 years ago drive them today. Therefore, patterns that have appeared historically tend to recur. A formation that often signalled reversal in the past is likely to signal reversal again.

This assumption justifies the use of **chart patterns** and **candlestick patterns**, both covered on dedicated pages later in this category.

## A short history: Dow Theory

The intellectual foundation of technical analysis comes from **Charles Dow**, the late-19th-century journalist who co-founded Dow Jones and the Wall Street Journal. Dow wrote a series of editorials between 1899 and 1902 outlining his observations about market behaviour. After his death, his colleagues compiled these ideas into what is now called **Dow Theory**.

Dow Theory's core tenets:

- The market has three trends: **primary** (months to years), **secondary** (weeks to months), and **minor** (days to weeks). Primary trends are what long-term technical traders focus on.
- Each trend has three phases: **accumulation** (smart money buying when others are pessimistic), **public participation** (the broader market joining in), and **distribution** (smart money selling to the latecomers).
- Volume should confirm price. A rising market with rising volume is healthier than one with falling volume.
- A trend continues until clear signals indicate reversal.

Dow Theory is over 120 years old but its core insights still sit underneath modern technical analysis.

## The four building blocks

Practical technical analysis uses four kinds of tools, each covered on a dedicated page in this category.

### 1. Price charts

The starting point. A chart shows the price of a share over time, typically in one of three formats:

- **Line chart**: a single line connecting closing prices.
- **Bar chart**: each period shown as a vertical bar with marks for open, high, low, and close.
- **Candlestick chart**: each period shown as a candle that visually emphasises open vs close, with wicks for high and low.

Most technical analysts on PSX use candlestick charts because they show more information at a glance. The next page on [Candlestick Charts](/docs/technical-analysis/candlestick-charts) covers them in detail.

### 2. Chart patterns

Specific configurations that traders believe foreshadow particular price moves. Examples:

- **Head and shoulders**: a reversal pattern at the top of an uptrend.
- **Double top / double bottom**: two attempts that fail to break through a level.
- **Triangles** (symmetrical, ascending, descending): consolidation patterns that often resolve in the direction of the prior trend.
- **Flags and pennants**: short-term continuation patterns after a sharp move.

The page on [Chart Patterns](/docs/technical-analysis/chart-patterns) covers the most common ones.

### 3. Technical indicators

Mathematical formulas applied to price (and sometimes volume) data to produce a smoother or more interpretable signal. Examples:

- **Moving averages** (simple, exponential): smooth out short-term noise to reveal the underlying trend.
- **Relative Strength Index (RSI)**: a momentum indicator suggesting overbought or oversold conditions.
- **MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)**: a trend and momentum indicator.
- **Bollinger Bands**: volatility-based bands around a moving average.

The page on [Technical Indicators](/docs/technical-analysis/technical-indicators) covers the most-used ones.

### 4. Volume

How many shares change hands. Volume is the second-most-important data point in technical analysis after price. Strong price moves on strong volume are considered more meaningful than the same price moves on weak volume.

## What technical analysis can do well

Used appropriately, technical analysis offers some real advantages.

- **Speed of analysis**. A technical view of a chart can be formed in minutes, vs hours or days for a serious fundamental analysis.
- **Useful in any timeframe**. The same tools work on intraday, daily, weekly, and monthly charts.
- **Helps with timing**. Even pure fundamental investors often use technical signals to fine-tune entry and exit prices for stocks they have already approved fundamentally.
- **Identifies trends and momentum** that fundamentals alone do not capture (price can move ahead of fundamentals or lag behind).
- **Defines risk**. Technical analysis often suggests specific stop-loss levels (e.g., "below this support") that disciplined traders use to limit losses.

## What technical analysis cannot do

Equally important to understand the limits.

- **It does not work in isolation for long-term wealth-building**. The most-wealthy long-term investors in Pakistan and globally have built their fortunes on fundamentals, not on chart-reading.
- **Pattern recognition is subjective**. Two technicians often see different patterns in the same chart. The same data can support contradictory conclusions.
- **Indicators lag**. Most indicators are derived from past price; by definition, they tell you what already happened, not what will. Some are leading indicators in theory, but their predictive power in practice is contested.
- **Backtesting is hard**. A pattern that "worked" in the past may have been a coincidence. With enough patterns and enough data, something always looks predictive in hindsight.
- **PSX-specific limitations**. Pakistani markets have lower liquidity than developed markets, which makes some technical patterns less reliable. Bid-ask spreads can make tight-stop-loss strategies expensive. Short-selling is restricted, which limits some technical strategies that rely on shorting.

## Is technical analysis right for you?

A few honest questions to ask before investing time in this category.

### What is your time horizon?

If you plan to hold investments for years, technical analysis is mostly a complement at best. The page on [Long-Term vs Short-Term Investing](/docs/investing-101/long-term-vs-short-term-investing) covers why long horizons reward fundamental work.

If you plan to hold positions for days to weeks, technical analysis is your primary tool.

### How much screen time can you commit?

Technical trading requires regular monitoring. If you can only check the market once a week, do not try to be a technical trader. Stick to fundamentals.

### How is your discipline?

Technical strategies require strict adherence to rules: enter when the signal triggers, exit when the stop is hit, do not second-guess. Most retail technical traders fail not because the tools are bad but because they cannot follow their own rules under emotional pressure.

### What about transaction costs and tax?

Pakistani brokerage commissions plus capital-gains tax on short-term trades add up quickly. The page on [Capital Gains Tax](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/capital-gains-tax) covers the tax structure. Frequent trading turns reasonable gross returns into mediocre net returns. Make sure your strategy can clear those costs before declaring it profitable.

## Key takeaways

- Technical analysis assumes price discounts everything, prices trend, and history repeats.
- Dow Theory is the intellectual foundation, with primary / secondary / minor trends and accumulation / participation / distribution phases.
- Four building blocks: charts, patterns, indicators, and volume.
- Technical analysis is fast and helps with timing, but does not replace fundamental analysis for long-term wealth-building.
- Pakistani-specific limitations (lower liquidity, restricted short-selling, transaction costs) constrain some technical strategies.
- Match the tool to your time horizon, discipline, and screen-time availability.
