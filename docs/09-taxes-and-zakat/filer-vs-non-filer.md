# Filer vs. Non-Filer

If you take only one tax-related decision on this site seriously, it should be this one. Pakistani tax law applies **higher withholding rates** to people who are not on the FBR's Active Taxpayer List, on almost every kind of investment income. The differential is not small. For most retail PSX investors, becoming a filer pays for itself within the first dividend cycle. This page explains the mechanics of filer vs non-filer status, what it costs to file, and what it saves you on the rest of this category's pages.

## What you'll learn

- What the FBR Active Taxpayer List (ATL) is

## What the ATL is

The **Active Taxpayer List (ATL)** is a list maintained and published by the **Federal Board of Revenue (FBR)** that names every individual or entity that has filed an income tax return for the relevant tax year. It is the formal, FBR-recognised definition of "filer" status in Pakistan.

- **Filer**: your name appears on the ATL because you filed your annual income tax return for the relevant tax year.
- **Non-filer**: you do not appear on the ATL.

The list is publicly searchable on the [FBR's website](https://www.fbr.gov.pk/). You can check your own status (or a counterparty's status) by entering a CNIC.

## Why the filer / non-filer split exists

The Pakistani tax base is narrow relative to the size of the economy. Most economic activity happens in cash or off-the-books, and a small percentage of the population files returns. Successive Finance Acts have introduced **higher withholding rates for non-filers** as a deliberate nudge to encourage filing.

The mechanism: at the point of any taxable transaction (dividends, vehicle registration, large bank transfers, property purchase), the deductor is required by law to apply a higher withholding rate if the recipient is not on the ATL. The differential is significant enough that most people who do any of these things at scale find it pays to file.

## What it costs to become a filer

Becoming a filer involves filing an income tax return for the tax year. The components:

1. **Registering with FBR** for an NTN (National Tax Number) if you do not already have one. The IRIS portal handles registration online.
2. **Filing your annual income tax return** by the deadline (typically late September for individuals; the exact date shifts).
3. **Paying any tax owed** based on your total income and applicable slabs.
4. **(Often) hiring a tax practitioner** to file on your behalf. Practitioner fees vary by complexity.

## The lapse trap

A common "I thought I was a filer" trap. Your ATL status applies to a specific tax year. If you filed for tax year 2025 (which makes you on the ATL after FBR publishes the relevant list) but then **fail to file** for tax year 2026 by the deadline, you can drift back to non-filer status.

Practical implications:

- Check the FBR ATL portal periodically to confirm your name is still on it.
- File on time every year, not just once.
- If you missed a year, file with the late-filing surcharge as soon as possible to restore filer status.

The lapse can happen quietly. You will not get a letter from FBR. You will just notice that your next dividend has a higher withholding rate than expected.

## A practical action list

If you are currently a non-filer and plan to invest meaningfully on PSX:

1. **Get an NTN** if you do not have one. Free and online via FBR IRIS.
2. **Estimate your tax liability** for the current tax year. Use FBR's online calculators or a practitioner.
3. **File your return** by the deadline (typically late September for individuals; verify the exact date).
4. **Pay any tax owed**.
5. **Check the ATL portal** to confirm your name appears on the list.
6. **Repeat annually**.

The whole process for a salaried individual without complex investments takes a few hours and a few thousand rupees in practitioner fees once a year.
