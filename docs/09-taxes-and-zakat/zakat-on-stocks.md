# Zakat on Stocks

Zakat is a religious obligation, not a tax. The rules for how it applies to PSX shares and mutual fund units come from Islamic jurisprudence rather than from FBR. Different schools of thought (madhabs) and individual scholars treat shares slightly differently, particularly the question of whether shares are "trading goods" or "long-term investment assets". This page covers the mainstream Hanafi position commonly applied in Pakistan, presents the alternative scholarly opinion respectfully, and walks through a worked example. As always with religious matters, consult a qualified scholar for your specific situation.

## The basic principles

Three concepts underpin all zakat calculations.

### Nisab

**Nisab** is the minimum threshold of wealth above which zakat is owed. It is set in Islamic jurisprudence based on a fixed weight of gold (87.48 grams) or silver (612.36 grams). The PKR equivalent is recalculated each year based on current market prices.

- The **silver nisab** in PKR for the current year is **`<TODO: verify the current PKR silver nisab>`**.

Most Pakistani scholars and Shariah boards (including those associated with major Islamic banks like Meezan Bank) publish the nisab annually. The silver nisab is typically lower and more conservative; many scholars recommend using it.

If your total zakatable wealth (cash + investments + jewellery + business stock, minus immediate liabilities) is below the nisab, no zakat is owed.

### Hawl

**Hawl** is the lunar-year holding requirement. Zakat becomes due once one full lunar year (about 354 days, slightly shorter than a solar year) has passed on assets above the nisab.

Most Pakistani households pick a fixed lunar date each year (commonly 1 Ramadan or another consistent date) and compute zakat on whatever assets they hold above nisab on that date.

### The rate: 2.5%

The **standard zakat rate is 2.5 percent (1/40)** of the value of zakatable assets. This is a religious constant; it does not change.

```
Annual zakat = Zakatable wealth × 2.5%
```

## The two main scholarly opinions on shares

This is the part where scholars genuinely differ. Both opinions below are held by recognised scholars; do not treat one as definitively correct.

### Opinion 1: Trading shares (the conservative default)

If you bought shares with the intention to **sell them**, even if held long-term, they are considered **trading goods** (sila' tijarah). The mainstream Hanafi position commonly applied in Pakistan treats trading shares this way: zakat is owed at 2.5% on the **full market value** of the shares on the zakat date.

This is the conservative default. It applies cleanly to:

- Active traders who buy and sell frequently.
- Anyone who bought shares specifically to capture price appreciation.
- Anyone uncertain about which opinion to follow.

### Opinion 2: Long-term investment shares

Some scholars hold that if you bought shares as a **long-term investment** (e.g., to hold the company for years and earn dividends, not to sell), the shares are more like a partnership stake in the underlying business. Under this view, zakat is owed only on the **company's zakatable assets per share** (typically cash, receivables, and inventory), which is usually a smaller base than the full share price.

To compute zakat under this opinion, you need:

- The company's annual report (specifically, its zakatable-assets disclosure).
- A scholar or Shariah board to interpret which line items count as zakatable.

Most Shariah-compliant mutual funds in Pakistan publish a **per-unit zakat value** that lets unit holders compute their zakat under this opinion without doing the underlying analysis themselves.

This opinion requires more work but produces a smaller zakat liability for typical operating companies (where zakatable assets are usually less than market cap).

### Which to follow?

Consult your scholar. If you have not consulted a scholar, the conservative default (full market value at 2.5%) is mainstream Hanafi practice in Pakistan and is the safer choice in the absence of specific guidance.

## A worked example: trading shares

Suppose on 1 Ramadan you hold:

- 1,000 shares of XYZ Ltd. at a market price of PKR 250 per share.
- Total market value: **PKR 250,000**.

Under the trading-shares treatment (the conservative default):

```
Zakat = 250,000 × 0.025 = PKR 6,250
```

You owe PKR 6,250 in zakat for the year on this position, payable to eligible recipients (the eight categories described in Surah At-Tawbah 9:60).

If you have other zakatable wealth (cash savings, gold, etc.), you would compute zakat on the combined total above the nisab, not on each asset separately.

## Mutual funds

Pakistani Shariah-compliant mutual funds (Meezan Islamic Fund, Al-Meezan Mutual Fund, NBP Islamic, UBL Islamic, etc.) publish a **per-unit zakat value** at the time of zakat (typically Ramadan). This is the amount of zakat owed per unit held, computed by the fund's Shariah advisory board.

To compute mutual-fund zakat:

```
Total zakat = Number of units held × Zakat-per-unit value (PKR)
```

## Bank-deducted Zakat

Pakistani banks deduct zakat **automatically** from savings-account balances on 1 Ramadan each year. This applies to accounts not specifically declared as exempt (Sahibs of Nisab who follow a different fiqh, or non-Muslims). The bank deducts 2.5% of the balance held above the prescribed exemption limit.

This automatic bank deduction does **not** include your share holdings. You compute and pay zakat on your shares separately, on top of whatever the bank deducts from your bank account.

If you do not want bank deduction (because you intend to follow a different opinion or compute zakat yourself), you can submit a **Zakat Declaration form (CZ-50)** to your bank annually before 1 Ramadan to opt out of automatic deduction. The form requires you to affirm that you will personally pay zakat in line with your fiqh.

## Recipients of zakat

Zakat is paid to one of the **eight categories** described in Surah At-Tawbah 9:60:

1. The poor (al-fuqara).
2. The needy (al-masakin).
3. Those employed to administer the zakat funds.
4. Those whose hearts are to be reconciled (typically interpreted in modern contexts as new converts or those near Islam).
5. Those in bondage (the unfree, in historical contexts).
6. Those in debt.
7. In the cause of Allah (typically interpreted as supporting Islamic activities).
8. The wayfarer (stranded travellers).

In modern Pakistan, registered charity organisations, mosques, madrasas, and direct giving to identified poor families are common channels. The specific allocation across categories is generally a matter of personal judgement guided by a scholar.

## Purification of haram income

Distinct from zakat but adjacent: if you hold shares in a company that has some non-Shariah-compliant income (e.g., a small interest-bearing portion in a mostly-compliant business), the corresponding portion of any dividend you receive is sometimes considered impure and should be **purified** by donating it to charity (without expectation of reward, since the money was never legitimately yours).

The mechanics:

```
Purification amount = Non-compliant income ratio × Dividend received
```

Most Shariah-compliant mutual funds publish a per-unit purification amount alongside the per-unit zakat value. The page on [Shariah Investing](/docs/category/shariah-investing) covers this in more detail, particularly the [purification page](/docs/shariah-investing/purification).

The non-compliant ratio for individual stocks is rarely published; for Shariah-compliant funds the fund does the calculation for unit holders.

## A practical action list

For a Pakistani retail investor wanting to discharge zakat properly on PSX holdings:

1. **Pick a fixed lunar date** each year (1 Ramadan is convenient and customary).
2. **List all your zakatable wealth** on that date: cash, gold, silver, business stock, share holdings, mutual fund units.
3. **Confirm you are above nisab**. If below, no zakat is owed.
4. **Decide which opinion** to follow on shares (trading vs long-term investment). Default to trading-shares if uncertain.
5. **Compute your zakat** based on the chosen opinion.
6. **Pay zakat** to eligible recipients within a reasonable time after the calculation date.
7. **Keep records** of computations and payments for your own reference.
8. **(Optional) Submit Form CZ-50** to your bank if you want to opt out of automatic bank-account deduction.

Most Pakistani families repeat this exercise every Ramadan as part of the broader spiritual practices of the month.
