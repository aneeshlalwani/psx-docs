# KMI-30 Index

If the [KSE-100](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/psx-indices) is the headline number every Pakistani business news bulletin quotes for "the market", the **KMI-30** is its Shariah-compliant counterpart. KMI stands for **KSE Meezan Index**, reflecting that the index is a joint creation of the Pakistan Stock Exchange and the Al-Meezan group (now Meezan Bank). For Pakistani Muslim investors looking for a single benchmark that captures Shariah-compliant equity performance, the KMI-30 is the standard reference.

## What the KMI-30 is

The **KMI-30 Index** tracks the **30 most liquid Shariah-compliant companies listed on PSX**, weighted by free-float market capitalisation, as a benchmark for measuring the performance of Shariah-compliant equity investments in Pakistan.

It is conceptually parallel to the [KSE-30](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/psx-indices) (also 30 stocks, also free-float weighted) but with the additional layer of Shariah screening. Where the KSE-30 includes any large Pakistani company, the KMI-30 includes only those that pass the [Shariah-screening criteria](/docs/shariah-investing/shariah-screening).

## History and purpose

According to the [PSX KMI-30 Index Methodology brochure](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/uploads/brochure-kmi-30-index.pdf):

- The index was **launched on 2 September 2008**, initially under the Karachi Stock Exchange (now PSX) brand.
- The **base period** for the index is **30 June 2008** with a base value of 1,000.
- It was a joint creation of the Karachi Stock Exchange (now PSX) and **Al-Meezan Investment Bank** (now Meezan Bank Limited).

The motivation for launching the index was to give Pakistani Muslim investors and Shariah-compliant fund managers a credible, transparent benchmark for performance measurement. Before KMI-30, fund managers had to construct their own custom benchmarks, which made cross-fund comparison difficult.

Today, the KMI-30 plays a role similar to what the FTSE Shariah Indices play globally and to what the Dow Jones Islamic Market Index plays in the US: a standardised reference for the Shariah-compliant slice of a national market.

## The free-float methodology

KMI-30 uses **free-float market capitalisation weighting**, the same modern methodology used by the [KSE-100](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/psx-indices).

```
Free-float market cap = Free-float shares × Market price per share
```

Where **free-float shares** are the publicly tradable shares, excluding:

- Strategic holdings by sponsors and promoters.
- Government holdings.
- Locked-in shares from corporate actions.
- Cross-holdings between group companies.
- Shares held under stable long-term arrangements that limit trading.

By using free-float rather than total market cap, the index reflects what is actually available for trading, not the company's total nominal value. The page on the [KSE-100](/docs/pakistan-stock-market/psx-indices) explains this distinction in more detail.

## The 12 percent individual-stock cap

A specific feature of the KMI-30: any individual company's weight in the index is **capped at 12 percent**. If a company's free-float market cap would otherwise give it more than 12 percent of the index, the cap kicks in and the excess weight is redistributed across the other constituents.

The reason for the cap is **diversification**. Without it, a single very large company (e.g., a major Pakistani fertiliser company or oil-and-gas explorer that happens to be Shariah-compliant) could dominate the index and drag the entire benchmark with its individual fortunes. The 12 percent cap ensures that no single name exceeds a meaningful threshold of total exposure.

This is similar to the cap used in the broader **PSX-KMI All Share Index** and is consistent with global Shariah-index practice (the Dow Jones Islamic Market and similar indices typically use comparable caps).

## Semi-annual re-composition

The constituent list of the KMI-30 is reviewed and updated **twice a year**, on:

- **30 June** (covering the period 1 January to 30 June).
- **31 December** (covering the period 1 July to 31 December).

At each review:

1. Every PSX-listed company is re-screened against the [Shariah criteria](/docs/shariah-investing/shariah-screening).
2. The 30 most-liquid currently-compliant companies are selected.
3. New additions and deletions are announced.
4. The index is rebalanced to reflect the updated constituent list and free-float weights.

This semi-annual cadence is a balance: more frequent re-composition would increase trading costs for index-tracking funds; less frequent would mean the index could carry stale or non-compliant names for longer.

## How investors actually use KMI-30

Several practical use cases.

### As a benchmark for Shariah-compliant funds

Most Pakistani Shariah-compliant equity mutual funds (Meezan Islamic Fund, Al-Meezan funds, NBP Islamic, UBL Islamic, and others) report their performance against KMI-30. A fund returning 15 percent in a year when KMI-30 returned 12 percent has outperformed; a fund returning 8 percent has underperformed. KMI-30 is the yardstick.

### As a starting universe for direct stock picking

Investors who want to build a portfolio of individual Shariah-compliant Pakistani stocks often start with the KMI-30 constituent list. The 30 names are by definition the most-liquid Shariah-compliant blue chips. From there, the investor applies their own [fundamental analysis](/docs/category/fundamental-analysis) to select positions.

### As a market-sentiment indicator

Watching the KMI-30 alongside the KSE-100 gives a sense of how Shariah-compliant names are doing relative to the broader market. In some periods, Shariah names outperform (e.g., when banks and conventional financials are under pressure); in other periods, they underperform (e.g., when banks are leading the market).

### As an underlying for index-tracking products

Pakistani asset managers offer ETFs and index funds that track the KMI-30, giving investors instant exposure to Shariah-compliant blue chips through a single buy. Check the [PSX product list](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/product-and-services/shariah-compliant-product) for currently-listed Shariah-compliant ETFs.

## Where to find current KMI-30 constituents

Because the constituents change with each semi-annual re-composition, do not rely on stale lists. Current constituents are published:

- **On the [PSX website](https://www.psx.com.pk/)** in the indices section.
- **In the [PSX KMI-30 brochure](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/uploads/brochure-kmi-30-index.pdf)** and quarterly methodology updates.
- **By Sarmaaya** at [sarmaaya.pk/indexes/KMI30](https://sarmaaya.pk/indexes/KMI30) and similar third-party data sites.
- **By Meezan Bank** in their investor education resources.

Always check the as-of date on whatever list you reference.

## KMI-30 vs PSX-KMI All Share Index

A quick clarification. PSX maintains two main Shariah-compliant equity indices:

- **KMI-30**: 30 most liquid Shariah-compliant companies. Concentrated, blue-chip-focused.
- **PSX-KMI All Share Index**: every Shariah-compliant company on PSX, not just the largest 30. Much broader, including small caps. The methodology was approved by SECP in May 2024.

KMI-30 is the right benchmark for liquid blue-chip exposure. PSX-KMI All Share is the right benchmark for broader Shariah-compliant equity exposure including smaller names.

Most Pakistani Shariah-compliant ETFs and index funds track the KMI-30 (because it is more liquid and easier to replicate); smaller actively-managed funds may benchmark against the All Share index for broader scope.

## A practical action list

For a Pakistani investor wanting to use the KMI-30:

1. **Look up current constituents** on the PSX website or Sarmaaya.
2. **Decide your approach**: invest in a KMI-30 ETF / index fund, or pick from the constituent list directly.
3. **For direct stock picking**: do your own [fundamental analysis](/docs/category/fundamental-analysis) on the names you select.
4. **Set a calendar reminder** for the 30 June and 31 December re-composition dates so you can re-check your holdings against the updated constituent list.
5. **Apply [purification](/docs/shariah-investing/purification)** on dividends from individual stocks; Shariah-compliant funds do this automatically.

<Callout type="tip" title="The simplest first step">

  picking individual KMI-30 stocks vs buying a fund feels overwhelming,

  benchmarked to KMI-30. You get the same underlying exposure with the

## Key takeaways

- KMI-30 is Pakistan's main Shariah-compliant equity benchmark, launched 2 September 2008.
- Joint creation of PSX (then KSE) and Al-Meezan Investment Bank (now Meezan Bank).
- Tracks 30 most liquid Shariah-compliant PSX companies, free-float weighted, with a 12 percent individual cap.
- Re-composed semi-annually on 30 June and 31 December.
- Used as a benchmark for Shariah-compliant funds, a starting universe for direct stock picking, and an underlying for ETFs and index funds.
- Complements the broader PSX-KMI All Share Index for narrower vs broader Shariah equity exposure.
