# Purification

Even after a company passes the [Shariah screening](/docs/shariah-investing/shariah-screening), it can still earn small amounts of non-compliant income (e.g., interest on operating cash held with a conventional bank, before the company moves it to an Islamic alternative). The threshold-based screens permit this up to 5 percent of revenue. But the dividends a shareholder receives include a proportional share of that non-compliant income, and Shariah principles require that **the non-compliant portion be removed and donated to charity** so that the rest of the dividend is genuinely halal. This process is called **purification** (tazkiyah).

## Why purification is needed

Pakistani Shariah screening allows a company to be considered compliant even if it has up to 5 percent non-compliant income (and up to 33 percent interest-bearing debt, etc.). This pragmatic threshold permits Pakistani Muslims to invest meaningfully in equities without a zero-tolerance screen that would exclude almost every modern company.

But "permissible to hold" is not the same as "every rupee of income from this company is halal". A company whose revenue is 96 percent permissible and 4 percent interest income is producing a dividend whose 4 percent portion is technically traceable to riba.

The scholarly resolution: the company is acceptable to hold (because the non-compliant exposure is small relative to the main business), but the **proportional non-compliant share of any dividend** received must be donated to charity. The remaining 96 percent is fully halal. The total **after purification** is the legitimate gain that the shareholder may keep.

## The basic formula

```
Purification amount = Non-compliant income ratio × Dividend received
```

A worked example. Suppose a Pakistani manufacturer's annual report shows:

- Total revenue: PKR 500 crore.
- Interest income (non-compliant): PKR 15 crore.
- Non-compliant income ratio: 15 / 500 = 3 percent.

You hold 1,000 shares of this company and receive a cash dividend of PKR 5 per share, gross.

```
Gross dividend = 1,000 × 5 = PKR 5,000
Purification = 3% × 5,000 = PKR 150
Halal portion = 5,000 - 150 = PKR 4,850 (less applicable withholding tax)
```

You donate PKR 150 to charity (without expectation of reward, since this money was never legitimately yours). The remaining PKR 4,850 (less withholding tax) is your halal income from the dividend.

## Shariah-compliant funds publish per-unit purification

For investors using Shariah-compliant mutual funds, the calculation is done by the fund's Shariah advisory board. The fund publishes a **per-unit purification amount** for each year (or quarter, depending on the fund's reporting cadence).

```
Total purification = Number of units held × Per-unit purification
```

A worked example. You hold 10,000 units of a major Pakistani Shariah-compliant equity fund. The fund's Shariah advisory board publishes a per-unit purification of **PKR 0.30** for the year.

```
Total purification = 10,000 × 0.30 = PKR 3,000
```

You donate PKR 3,000 to charity. The fund's annual reporting will tell you exactly what the per-unit purification figure is and what its calculation reflects.

For the page on [zakat-on-stocks](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/zakat-on-stocks), the same fund typically also publishes a per-unit zakat value. Both are computed by the fund and reported to unit holders annually.

## Direct stockholders: the harder version

If you hold individual stocks directly, you need to compute purification yourself. The steps:

1. **Find the company's annual report**. Pull the most recent audited income statement.
2. **Identify non-compliant income lines**. Common items: interest from bank deposits, returns on conventional treasury bills, income from any non-compliant subsidiary or investment, certain insurance reimbursements.
3. **Sum the non-compliant items**. This is your numerator.
4. **Divide by total revenue** to get the non-compliant income ratio.
5. **Apply the ratio** to your dividend received from this company.
6. **Donate the resulting amount** to charity.

For a Pakistani investor with a portfolio of 10 to 20 individual stocks, this is non-trivial annual work. Many investors choose Shariah-compliant funds specifically to avoid this overhead.

A simplification many scholars accept: applying a **standard conservative ratio** (e.g., 5 percent, the maximum allowed under the screen) across all individual holdings, rather than computing the precise ratio per company. This produces a higher purification amount than the precise calculation would yield, which is the safe direction. Consult your scholar before adopting any simplification.

## Where the purified amount goes

The purified amount is donated to **charity**, with the specific intention that the donor receives no spiritual reward for it (because the money was never legitimately theirs). This is doctrinally distinct from voluntary charity (sadaqah) or zakat.

In Pakistan, common channels:

- **Hospitals serving low-income patients** (e.g., SIUT, Indus Hospital, Shaukat Khanum's free patient programmes).
- **Madrasas and educational charities** for poor students.
- **Direct giving to specific identified poor families**.
- **Registered relief and development charities** (e.g., Edhi Foundation, JDC, Saylani Welfare).

Most scholars prefer that purification donations go to causes that benefit the poor and disadvantaged, given the doctrinal nature of the funds.

The donation should be made **in addition to**, not instead of, regular zakat. Purification and zakat are separate religious obligations.

## Purification vs zakat

A common point of confusion. The two are separate.

| Aspect | Zakat | Purification |
|---|---|---|
| Doctrinal basis | Pillar of Islam (5 pillars) | Removing impermissible from otherwise-permissible income |
| Frequency | Annual (lunar year) | Each time non-compliant income is received |
| Calculated on | Total zakatable wealth above nisab | Specifically, the non-compliant portion of investment income |
| Rate | 2.5 percent | The non-compliant income ratio (typically 0 to 5 percent for screened companies) |
| Spiritual reward | Yes, the donor receives reward | No, the donor receives no reward (the money was never theirs) |
| To whom | Eight categories defined in Surah At-Tawbah 9:60 | Charity benefiting the poor (broader than zakat categories) |
| Required for | Practising Muslims with assets above nisab | Anyone receiving non-compliant income |

Both are doctrinally separate but adjacent. A Pakistani investor practising both typically computes them once a year, donates each separately, and tracks each separately for personal records.

The page on [Zakat on Stocks](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/zakat-on-stocks) covers the zakat side in detail.

## A practical action list

For a Pakistani retail investor wanting to discharge purification properly.

1. **For Shariah-compliant fund holdings**: read the fund's annual report for the published per-unit purification value. Multiply by your unit count. Donate that amount.
2. **For direct stock holdings**: compute the non-compliant ratio per company from the company's annual report, apply to your dividends, and donate the result. Or use a conservative blanket ratio with scholarly approval.
3. **Donate to charity** with the specific intention of removing impermissible income, not as voluntary sadaqah.
4. **Keep records** of computations and donations for your own reference.
5. **Pay zakat separately** (covered on the [Zakat on Stocks](/docs/taxes-and-zakat/zakat-on-stocks) page); purification is in addition to, not instead of, zakat.
6. **Consult your scholar** for any unusual situations (special dividends, capital gains on partial-year holdings, mid-year compliance changes).

## Key takeaways

- Purification is the donation of the non-compliant portion of investment income from otherwise-Shariah-compliant companies.
- The purification ratio is the company's non-compliant income divided by its total revenue, capped at the 5 percent screening threshold.
- Shariah-compliant funds compute and publish a per-unit purification amount annually.
- Direct stockholders compute purification themselves from the company's annual report.
- The purified amount is donated to charity with no expectation of reward.
- Purification is doctrinally distinct from zakat; both apply, separately.
