# Shariah Screening

If you want to invest in a specific Pakistani company while staying within Shariah principles, the question is straightforward: **does this company pass the Shariah screen?** This page walks through the two-step screening methodology jointly developed by PSX, Al-Meezan Investment Management, and Meezan Bank, and approved by SECP in May 2024 for the PSX-KMI All Share Index. The same methodology is the foundation for KMI-30 inclusion and is what every Pakistani Shariah-compliant mutual fund applies to its holdings.

## What you'll learn

- The two-step screen: business activity, then financial ratios
- The four financial-ratio thresholds in detail
- A worked example of each ratio
- What "non-compliant income" actually means
- Why threshold-based screens are used instead of zero tolerance
- How the screen is re-applied semi-annually

## The two-step screen

Pakistani Shariah screening applies in two sequential steps. A company must pass **both** steps to be considered Shariah-compliant.

### Step 1: Business activity screen

The company's **core business** must not involve any of the prohibited categories. According to the [Meezan Bank Shariah Screening Criteria](https://www.meezanbank.com/shariah-screening-criteria/), prohibited categories include:

- **Conventional banking and financial services on interest** (because they are built on riba). Conventional banks, conventional insurance, leasing companies on interest-based contracts.
- **Liquor and alcohol** production or distribution.
- **Pork and other haram meat** production or processing.
- **Gambling and gaming** including casinos and lottery.
- **Adult entertainment** including nightclubs, pornographic content, and similar.
- **Other industries** prohibited by Shariah scholars (tobacco and weapons fall here in some scholarly opinions, with significant difference of view).

A company whose primary business falls in any of these categories is excluded at Step 1, regardless of how clean its financials look.

### Step 2: Financial ratios screen

For companies whose core business is permissible, four financial-ratio tests apply. All four must be satisfied.

## The four financial ratios

Per the SECP-approved methodology and Meezan Bank's published criteria, the current ratio thresholds are as follows. Note that SECP has tightened some thresholds in recent years; always verify the current numbers from the [official methodology document](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/uploads/brochure-kmi-30-index.pdf) before applying them.

### Ratio 1: Interest-bearing debt to total assets

```
Interest-bearing debt / Total assets < threshold
```

**Threshold**: historically 37 percent in older methodology; SECP recently tightened to 33 percent for the PSX-KMI All Share Index methodology. Always check the latest official methodology.

**What this captures**: how much of the company's funding comes from interest-bearing debt (bank loans, conventional bonds, term-finance certificates with fixed interest). A company funded heavily through interest-bearing instruments fails this test even if its core business is permissible.

**Worked example**: a Pakistani cement company has total assets of PKR 100 crore. Its long-term loans plus short-term borrowings total PKR 25 crore. Ratio = 25 / 100 = 25 percent. **Passes** the threshold.

If the same company's interest-bearing debt grew to PKR 40 crore, the ratio would be 40 percent, which would **fail** under either the older 37 percent or the tightened 33 percent threshold.

### Ratio 2: Non-compliant investments to total assets

```
Non-compliant investments / Total assets < 33%
```

**What this captures**: how much of the company's assets are invested in non-compliant instruments. Examples of non-compliant investments:

- **Investments in conventional banks' deposits** earning interest.
- **Treasury bills and conventional government bonds** earning interest (note: sukuk are Shariah-compliant alternatives).
- **Equity stakes in companies that fail the business-activity screen**.
- **Conventional commercial paper or money market instruments** earning interest.

**Worked example**: a Pakistani fertiliser company has total assets of PKR 200 crore. It holds PKR 30 crore in treasury bills and PKR 5 crore in deposits with conventional banks. Total non-compliant investments: PKR 35 crore. Ratio = 35 / 200 = 17.5 percent. **Passes**.

If the same company's treasury-bill holdings ballooned to PKR 80 crore, the ratio would be 42.5 percent, which **fails**.

### Ratio 3: Non-compliant income to total revenue

```
Non-compliant income / Total revenue < 5%
```

**What this captures**: how much of the company's annual revenue comes from prohibited sources. Examples of non-compliant income include:

- **Interest income** on bank deposits, T-bills, conventional bonds.
- **Income from gambling, casinos, alcohol, addictive drugs** (typically the activity-screen catches this earlier, but a small subsidiary engaged in such could create non-compliant income).
- **Insurance claim reimbursements from conventional insurance** (takaful is the compliant alternative).
- **Penalties and late-payment charges** treated as gharar-based income by some scholars.
- **Income from interest-bearing derivatives**.
- **Dividend income from non-compliant companies** held in the investment portfolio.

This is often the most-debated ratio. Many otherwise-compliant Pakistani companies hold operating cash in conventional bank accounts that earn modest interest. The 5 percent threshold reflects scholarly recognition that small incidental non-compliant income is unavoidable in modern operations and that companies should not be excluded over de minimis exposure.

**Worked example**: a Pakistani textile company has total revenue of PKR 50 crore. It earned PKR 1.5 crore in interest on operating cash held with conventional banks (perhaps the company has not yet moved to Islamic banks). Ratio = 1.5 / 50 = 3 percent. **Passes**.

If interest income grew to PKR 3 crore, ratio = 6 percent, which **fails**.

### Ratio 4: Illiquid assets to total assets

```
Illiquid assets / Total assets >= 25%
```

**What this captures**: a structural requirement that the company has a meaningful base of real, productive (illiquid) assets, not just financial holdings. Illiquid assets typically include property, plant, equipment, inventory, and intangibles such as patents and trademarks (the page on [Non-Current Assets](/docs/fundamental-analysis/non-current-assets) covers what counts).

The reasoning: a Shariah-compliant company should be a real economic enterprise, not a thinly-disguised financial vehicle. The 25 percent floor ensures that operating substance is present.

**Worked example**: a Pakistani fertiliser company has total assets of PKR 200 crore, of which PKR 130 crore is property, plant, equipment, and inventory. Illiquid ratio = 130 / 200 = 65 percent. **Passes** with a wide margin.

A pure financial-services holding company with mostly cash and short-term investments would likely fail this ratio.

## Why threshold-based, not zero-tolerance

A natural question: if the principle is "no riba", why does Pakistani Shariah screening allow up to 33 percent interest-bearing debt or 5 percent non-compliant income?

The scholarly answer rests on the principle of **necessity (darurah)** combined with the practical observation that:

- Zero-tolerance screening would exclude almost every modern listed company.
- Pakistani Muslims would then be unable to invest meaningfully in equities.
- This would harm individual financial well-being and divert wealth into less-regulated alternatives.

The thresholds are a scholarly compromise: companies whose non-compliant exposure is **small relative to their main activities** are acceptable, with **purification** required for the dividend portion attributable to non-compliant income. The page on [Purification](/docs/shariah-investing/purification) covers the latter.

This compromise is not unique to Pakistan. Major global Shariah index providers (Dow Jones Islamic Market, FTSE Shariah, S&P Shariah) all use threshold-based screens with broadly similar (though not identical) ratios.

## How the screen is re-applied

Companies do not stay statically compliant or non-compliant. As they take on more debt, divest businesses, or change their financial mix, their compliance status can shift.

Pakistani Shariah indices and funds re-screen their holdings:

- **Twice a year** on 30 June and 31 December for the [KMI-30](/docs/shariah-investing/kmi-30-index) and the broader PSX-KMI All Share Index.
- **Quarterly or annually** for individual mutual funds, depending on their Shariah advisory board's policies.

A company that drops out of compliance is removed at the next re-composition. The fund or investor is then expected to divest within a defined window (typically a few weeks to a quarter), with any residual gains potentially subject to purification rules.

This semi-annual cadence means a stock that was Shariah-compliant when you bought it might not be 6 to 12 months later. Long-term Shariah-compliant investors check the published constituent lists at each re-composition.

## A practical action list

If you want to verify whether a specific PSX-listed company is currently Shariah-compliant.

1. **Check the latest [KMI-30 constituent list](https://www.psx.com.pk/)** as the simplest test: if the company is in KMI-30, it has passed at the most recent re-composition.
2. **For non-KMI-30 companies**, check the broader **PSX-KMI All Share Index** constituents.
3. **If the company is not on either list**, look at the [Meezan Bank screening criteria](https://www.meezanbank.com/shariah-screening-criteria/) and apply the four ratios using the company's most recent annual report.
4. **For close-to-threshold cases**, consult a recognised Shariah scholar or board before investing.
5. **For ongoing monitoring**, set a calendar reminder for the 30 June and 31 December re-composition dates.

## Key takeaways

- Shariah screening on PSX has two steps: a business-activity screen and a four-ratio financial screen.
- The four ratios cover interest-bearing debt, non-compliant investments, non-compliant income, and illiquid assets.
- Thresholds: roughly 33 percent (debt and investments), 5 percent (income), and 25 percent (illiquid assets minimum). Verify current numbers from the official methodology.
- Threshold-based screens reflect scholarly compromise between strict interpretation and practical participation in modern equity markets.
- Compliance is reassessed semi-annually; a compliant company today may not be so in 6 months.
- The simplest path for retail investors is to use a Shariah-compliant fund whose advisory board applies the screening continuously.
