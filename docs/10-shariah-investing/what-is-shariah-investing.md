# What Is Shariah Investing?

For many Pakistani Muslims, the choice is not just whether to invest, but **how to invest in a way that aligns with Islamic principles**. Conventional finance has built itself on interest-bearing debt, which Shariah considers prohibited. Insurance, gambling, alcohol, pork, and adult content are similarly off-limits. Shariah investing is the disciplined process of identifying companies whose businesses and financial structures are compatible with Islamic teachings, and investing only in those.

Pakistan has built one of the most developed Shariah-compliant equity infrastructures in the Muslim world, anchored by the **KMI-30 Index** and the screening methodology jointly developed by PSX, Al-Meezan Investment Management, and Meezan Bank. This category covers what that infrastructure looks like in practice and how a Pakistani retail investor uses it.

## The principles

Shariah investing rests on a few foundational Islamic-law principles, drawn primarily from the Quran, Sunnah, and centuries of fiqh (Islamic jurisprudence).

### Prohibition of riba (interest)

The most-cited principle. **Riba** (commonly translated as interest, usury, or unlawful gain) is prohibited in Islamic finance. This rules out:

- Earning interest on bank deposits or bonds.
- Investing in companies whose primary business is lending at interest (conventional banks, conventional insurance, leasing companies).
- Investing in companies that fund themselves primarily through interest-bearing debt (above defined thresholds).

The structural alternative in Islamic finance is **profit-and-loss sharing**: arrangements like mudarabah (silent-partner investment), musharakah (joint partnership), and sukuk (asset-backed certificates that share underlying business returns rather than paying fixed interest).

### Prohibition of gharar (excessive uncertainty)

**Gharar** refers to excessive uncertainty in contracts. Conventional derivatives, speculative options, and certain insurance structures are considered to involve gharar. Shariah-compliant alternatives (takaful for insurance, sukuk for fixed-income-style exposure) are structured to avoid this.

### Prohibition of investment in haram industries

Categories of business activity that are prohibited regardless of financial structure:

- **Alcohol production and distribution**.
- **Pork and haram meat production**.
- **Gambling and casinos**.
- **Conventional banking and insurance** (because they are built on interest).
- **Adult entertainment** including nightclubs and pornographic content.
- **Tobacco** in many scholarly opinions (with some difference of view).
- **Weapons** in some interpretations (with significant difference of view).

### Avoidance of zulm (injustice)

A broader principle that some scholars apply: avoiding investments in companies that engage in significant unjust practices (exploitative labour, environmental harm, deceptive marketing). This is more interpretation-dependent than the structural prohibitions above.

## The two-step screen

Modern Pakistani Shariah-screening methodology, as approved by SECP and applied by Al-Meezan Investment Management and Meezan Bank, uses a **two-step screen**.

### Step 1: Business activity screen

The company's **core business** must not violate Shariah. This rules out the haram-industry list above. A company whose primary revenue comes from prohibited activities is out, regardless of how its financials look.

### Step 2: Financial ratios screen

For companies whose core business passes the activity screen, additional financial-ratio tests apply. The current screens (per the [Meezan Bank Shariah Screening Criteria](https://www.meezanbank.com/shariah-screening-criteria/) and the SECP-approved PSX-KMI All Share Index methodology approved in May 2024):

- **Interest-bearing debt to total assets** must be below a threshold (recently tightened by SECP to 33 percent from a previous 37 percent in some methodologies).
- **Non-compliant investments to total assets** must be below 33 percent.
- **Non-compliant income to total revenue** must be below 5 percent.
- **Illiquid assets to total assets** must be at least 25 percent.

A company that fails any one of these ratios is excluded. The page on [Shariah Screening](/docs/shariah-investing/shariah-screening) walks through each criterion with worked examples.

### Why ratios at all?

A natural question: if a company is in a permissible industry but has some interest-bearing debt or some non-compliant income, why exclude it on a *threshold* basis rather than a strict zero-tolerance basis?

The answer is practical. Almost no large modern company is 100 percent free of interest-bearing debt or 100 percent free of incidental non-compliant income (e.g., bank deposit interest on operating cash). A pure zero-tolerance screen would exclude most listed companies, leaving Pakistani Muslims unable to invest meaningfully in equities. The threshold-based screen is a scholarly compromise: companies whose non-compliant exposure is small relative to their main activities are acceptable, with **purification** required for the dividend portion attributable to non-compliant income.

The [Purification](/docs/shariah-investing/purification) page covers how to handle that residual.

## How compliance is monitored

A company that passes Shariah screening today is not guaranteed to pass next year. Companies take on more debt, acquire non-compliant subsidiaries, or change their business mix. Pakistani Shariah indices and Shariah-compliant funds re-screen their holdings periodically.

- The **KMI-30 Index** undergoes [semi-annual re-composition](https://www.psx.com.pk/psx/themes/psx/uploads/brochure-kmi-30-index.pdf) on 30 June and 31 December each year.
- The broader **PSX-KMI All Share Index** is screened on the same schedule.
- Major Pakistani Shariah-compliant mutual funds (Meezan Islamic Fund, Al-Meezan funds, NBP Islamic, UBL Islamic, and others) re-screen their portfolios at similar cadence.

A company that drops out of compliance is removed at the next re-composition; the fund or the investor is then expected to divest within a defined window.

## Two practical paths for Pakistani retail investors

A useful framing before going deeper.

### Path 1: Invest through Shariah-compliant mutual funds

For most retail investors, the simplest and most rigorous approach is to invest through a Shariah-compliant mutual fund. The fund's Shariah advisory board does the screening, the re-composition, and the purification calculation on the investor's behalf. The investor receives regular reporting on the fund's NAV, performance, and any purification amounts.

Major Pakistani Shariah-compliant funds include Meezan Islamic Fund, Al-Meezan funds, NBP Islamic, UBL Islamic, and others. The category on [Mutual Funds](/docs/investment-basics/mutual-funds) covers the broader mutual-fund landscape.

### Path 2: Invest directly in Shariah-screened individual stocks

For investors who prefer direct stock ownership, the [KMI-30](/docs/shariah-investing/kmi-30-index) provides a public list of currently-compliant blue-chip Pakistani names. Building a portfolio from KMI-30 constituents (or from the broader PSX-KMI All Share Index) gives direct ownership while staying within Shariah boundaries.

This path requires the investor to do the [purification](/docs/shariah-investing/purification) calculation themselves and to monitor for compliance changes between index re-compositions.

## Key takeaways

- Shariah investing aligns equity investing with Islamic principles: no riba, no gharar, no haram industries.
- The Pakistani screening methodology has two steps: a business-activity screen and a financial-ratios screen.
- Companies are screened on interest-bearing debt, non-compliant investments, non-compliant income, and illiquid assets.
- Compliance is monitored on a semi-annual cycle; the KMI-30 re-composes every June 30 and December 31.
- Two practical paths: Shariah-compliant mutual funds (simpler) or direct stocks from screened lists (more work).
- Purification of incidental non-compliant income is part of the discipline, covered on a dedicated page.
